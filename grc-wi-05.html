GRC-WI-05: Asset Submission Reviewer Work Instruction

Operational guidance for the GRC team member reviewing an asset submission against the Asset Management procedure (GRC-SOP-05). Covers the validation checklist, common review failures, evidence to capture, and edge-case handling. Audience: GRC reviewer; assumes familiarity with GRC-SOP-05 and GRC-REG-CODES.

Section 1
Purpose & Audience

This Work Instruction tells the GRC reviewer how to review an asset submission. It is a desk-side reference, not a methodology document — for the methodology, see GRC-SOP-05.

The reviewer's responsibility is to confirm that a proposed asset record is fit to publish in GRC-REG-ASSET. Approval moves the asset's status from Onboarding to Live (per GRC-SOP-05 §9). Approval is independent of the submitter (separation of duties); the reviewer is the second pair of eyes that catches what the submitter missed.

1.1 Audience

  • GRC team members who hold the reviewer role (Head of GRC, GRC analysts, delegated reviewers)
  • LCSOs / CSLCs acting as reviewer for their operating unit's submissions where the GRC team has delegated authority

1.2 Out of scope

  • How to create an asset submission — that is the submitter's responsibility, covered by the Asset Onboarding Form (GRC-FRM-XX, future delivery)
  • Asset retirement/decommissioning reviews — covered separately under GRC-SOP-05 §12
  • Periodic re-reviews of existing live assets — different process (GRC-SOP-05 §11)
  • Reconciliation findings handling — separate workstream owned by Head of IT Operations
Section 2
Reviewer Principles

Five operating principles. Reviewers internalise these so that judgement calls are consistent across the team:

  • Trust but verify. Submitters are not adversaries. Most submissions are honest attempts. The reviewer's job is to catch mistakes, not to police.
  • Reject early, not late. If a submission is missing key fields, reject in minutes, not days. Don't try to fill gaps yourself.
  • Be specific in feedback. "Field 8 (technical owner) is a team mailbox; SOP-05 §10.1 requires a named individual where one is reasonably available" — not "ownership unclear".
  • Document everything. Approval and rejection both leave audit trails. Note what you checked and what you noticed, even if you approved.
  • Escalate genuine ambiguity. If you can't decide between approve and reject, escalate to the Head of GRC. Don't half-approve.
Section 3
Inputs & Service Level

3.1 Inputs to the review

  • The asset submission record (typically a completed Asset Onboarding Form or equivalent)
  • Existing GRC-REG-ASSET (to check for duplicates and conflicts)
  • Existing GRC-REG-CODES (to check that all referenced codes exist)
  • Submission metadata: submitter identity, operating unit, submission date

3.2 Service level

TierSLA from submission to first responseSLA to final disposition
Tier 0 / Tier 1 asset3 working days10 working days
Tier 2 asset5 working days15 working days
Tier 3 asset10 working days20 working days

"First response" means an acknowledgement that the review has begun, including any initial questions to the submitter. "Final disposition" means approve, reject, or request resubmission with a defined gap closure path.

Bulk submissions. Where an operating unit submits 20+ assets in a single batch (e.g. an initial seeding from an existing inventory), the SLA applies to the batch overall, not to individual records. Coordinate with the Head of GRC for prioritisation.
Section 4
Validation Checklist

Work through these checks in order. Each check has a clear pass/fail outcome. Stop at the first hard fail; record what you found and notify the submitter. Do not proceed past a hard-fail item.

4.1 Naming-conformance checks (hard fails)

N1Asset ID format conforms to GRC-SOP-05 §6 (Pattern A) or §7 (Pattern B/C). Pattern A names use AGENCY+COUNTRY+CITY+TYPE+SEQ with no separators. Pattern B uses ASSET-AGENCY-TYPE-SEQ. Pattern C uses ASSET-CENTRAL-TYPE-SEQ.
N2All segments are uppercase A–Z and 0–9 only. No lowercase, no special characters, no embedded whitespace.
N3Each code segment exists in GRC-REG-CODES. Agency code, city code, asset-type code all must already be approved. If any is missing, reject with "code request required first".
N4Sequence number is the next available in its (Agency × Country × City × Type) series. Look up the most recent existing sequence for that combination. Sequences are not recycled (per GRC-SOP-05 §7.5 and §12.3).
N5Asset ID is unique within GRC-REG-ASSET. Duplicate Asset IDs are a hard fail. If duplicate, the submitter must either claim the existing record (and update it) or pick a different ID.
N6Pattern selection is appropriate for asset type. Servers, endpoints, network devices, peripherals → Pattern A. Applications, SaaS, identity stores, data stores → Pattern B or C. Cloud workloads can use either depending on whether they have a hostname.

4.2 Schema completeness checks (hard fails for mandatory fields)

S1All mandatory fields are present. See GRC-SOP-05 §8 — fields marked mandatory in groups A–F.
S2Asset type (field 4) is exactly one value from the §5.1 taxonomy. "Server/Application" hybrid choices are a fail — pick the primary technology.
S3Operating unit (field 6) is a single agency code or CENTRAL. "Multiple agencies" is not a valid value — that's CENTRAL.
S4Business owner, technical owner, security contact (fields 7–9) are all populated with specific role + name OR (where genuinely unavailable) a team mailbox with documented justification.
S5Criticality tier (field 10) is Tier 0 / 1 / 2 / 3. Reviewer sanity-checks against asset description: a single staff laptop should not normally be Tier 0; a domain controller should not be Tier 3.
S6Data classification (field 11) is L1 / L2 / L3 / L4 per IRP §10. Sense-check against personal-data flag (field 12) — if personal data is present, classification should be L2 or higher.
S7Lifecycle dates (fields 20–22) make sense. Onboarded date is not in the future. Last reviewed date is not before onboarded. Next review due reflects the Tier-driven cadence.
S8Backup, monitoring, patching ownership (fields 23–25) are populated. "Unknown" or "TBD" is not acceptable for an asset moving to Live status.

4.3 Consistency checks (soft fails — request clarification)

C1Function tags align with asset type. A SaaS service tagged "End-User Computing" warrants a question — usually a tag is misapplied.
C2Hosting model and OS/platform are consistent. "Hosting model: SaaS" with "OS: Windows Server 2022" suggests confusion — SaaS is vendor-managed.
C3Network zone makes sense for the asset type. An "External-facing" function tag without a DMZ or public network zone, or vice versa, warrants a question.
C4Tier and data classification jointly justify backup/monitoring. A Tier 0 asset with backup "Out of scope" is suspicious — confirm or escalate.
C5Regulatory tags are present where they should be. Personal data flag = Yes but no GDPR tag is a likely error.
C6Dependencies are plausible. "No dependencies" is rarely true — most assets depend on identity, network, or other infrastructure.

4.4 Cross-reference checks

X1Linked risks (field 26) exist in GRC-REG-RISK and are still open. Closed/retired risks should not be linked.
X2Linked exceptions (field 27) exist and are within their approved validity period.
X3Dependencies referenced exist in the register, or are explicitly noted as "external / not in scope" with rationale.
X4Submitter and approver are different individuals. Separation of duties (GRC-SOP-05 §4.2). If submitter = approver, immediate escalation.
How long should a review take? A clean Tier 3 endpoint submission typically takes 5–10 minutes. A Tier 1 application with multiple integrations takes 30–60 minutes. A Tier 0 cross-agency asset can take 2+ hours including consultations. If a "simple" review is consuming an hour, that is a signal: either the submission is incomplete, or you are doing the submitter's job. Stop and ask.
Section 5
Common Review Failures

The patterns below are seen often. Recognising them speeds up reviews.

F1 Code not yet in GRC-REG-CODES

Submitter has used a city code, asset-type code, or agency code that has not been formally added. Action: reject with link to the code-request workflow (GRC-REG-CODES section "Code Request Workflow"). Do not approve the asset record until the code is approved.

F2 Mismatched naming pattern

Pattern A used for a SaaS service, or Pattern B used for a physical server. Action: reject with reference to GRC-SOP-05 §7. Common cause: submitter unfamiliar with the patterns. Provide example from §7 in feedback.

F3 Ownership "team mailbox" without justification

Business owner = "ITops@..." or technical owner = "infra@...". Action: request a named individual. A team mailbox is acceptable only as a fallback with documented reason (e.g. shift-rotation team where no single owner is reasonable).

F4 Tier vs criticality mismatch

A staff laptop classified Tier 0 ("everything is critical to the user!") or a domain controller classified Tier 2. Action: use the Tier definitions in core-concepts to push back. Tier reflects organisational impact, not user perception.

F5 Missing classification when personal data is present

Personal data = Yes but data classification = L1 (Public) or no GDPR tag. Action: reject; personal data triggers L2 minimum classification and GDPR tagging.

F6 Backup/monitoring "TBD"

Asset is going Live but operational fields say "TBD" or "Will configure later". Action: reject. If genuinely transitional, asset stays in Onboarding state until operational coverage is confirmed.

F7 Duplicate of existing record

The asset already exists in GRC-REG-ASSET, possibly under a slightly different name. Action: reject the submission and direct the submitter to update the existing record. Common cause: submitter unaware of existing record (often when ownership has changed).

F8 Onboarding/last-reviewed/next-review dates inconsistent

E.g. Onboarded 2024-03-12, Last reviewed 2023-12-01 (before onboarding). Action: request correction. Often a copy-paste error; rarely a sign of more serious data-quality issues.

F9 "Other" asset type without justification

Asset type = OTH used as a default. Action: ask the submitter to attempt a more specific type. OTH is genuinely "I cannot fit this elsewhere"; if the submitter can describe the asset, one of the 13 specific types usually applies.

F10 Cross-agency asset assigned to a single agency

Submitter records a centrally-managed SaaS as belonging to AKHS only when it serves all agencies. Action: reject; the operating unit should be CENTRAL. Common cause: submitter is from AKHS and naturally lists their agency.

Section 6
Decision Flow

After working through the checklist, the reviewer reaches one of three dispositions:

Gate 1Any naming-conformance failure (N1–N6)? REJECT — record cannot be processed until the naming issue is resolved. Notify submitter with specific reference.
Gate 2Any mandatory-field omission (S1–S8)? REJECT — record is incomplete. Provide a list of missing fields; do not fill them yourself.
Gate 3Any consistency or cross-reference issue (C1–C6, X1–X4)? REQUEST CLARIFICATION — the record may be salvageable; ask the submitter to clarify or correct. Set a 5-working-day deadline for response. If no response, the submission is closed; submitter may resubmit.
Gate 4All checks pass? APPROVE — move asset status to Live; publish in GRC-REG-ASSET; notify submitter; record approval in audit trail with named approver and approval date.

6.1 What to record on approval

  • Approver name and role
  • Approval date and time
  • Reference to which checklist version was used
  • Any waivers applied (e.g. "team mailbox accepted as technical owner due to shift rotation — confirmed with LCSO")
  • Onboarded date (the date status moves to Live)
  • First scheduled review date (per Tier cadence)

6.2 What to record on rejection

  • Reviewer name and role
  • Rejection date
  • List of failed checklist items, with specific reference to GRC-SOP-05 sections
  • Specific feedback to the submitter (what to change)
  • Any cross-reference to existing records that may be relevant (e.g. "this appears to be the same asset as AKHSKEMOMSRV0007")
  • Resubmission instruction (resubmit with corrections vs. open a code request first vs. talk to GRC)
Reject ≠ judgement on the submitter. A clean rejection with specific feedback is professional. A vague rejection ("not good enough") is not. Submitters who receive specific feedback resubmit cleanly more than 80% of the time on the first attempt.
Section 7
Edge Cases

The cases below are not failures but require specific handling. Reviewers should know how each is treated rather than escalating each time.

Deviation request alongside submission

Submitter has flagged that the asset cannot conform to standard naming and proposes a deviation. Process the deviation request before the asset record. If deviation is approved (recorded in GRC-REG-CODES Deviations table), proceed with the asset record using the deviation. If not, the asset must be renamed.

Legacy asset (pre-SOP)

An existing asset is being formally registered for the first time. Tag the record with "legacy naming" if its current name doesn't conform, and add a note. The asset can go Live with non-conforming name; rename happens at the next decommissioning/rebuild per GRC-SOP-05 §14.3.

Cross-agency asset proposed by single-agency submitter

An LCSO from one agency submits a record for an asset that other agencies also use (e.g. a regional SaaS instance shared across 3 country offices). Confirm with the named technical owner whether scope is single-agency or cross-agency. If cross-agency, the operating unit is CENTRAL, not the submitter's agency.

Identical naming across operating units

Two agencies submit assets with the same Asset ID (only possible with non-conforming naming). Reject both, request agency-prefix renaming. The convention prevents this in conformant naming.

Asset with multiple business owners

An application used by HR, Finance, and Operations. Pick the primary business owner — the one who would make the call about retiring or replacing the asset. Note the secondary stakeholders in the notes field. Field 7 holds one accountable individual.

Submitter requests an exception during review

"Can you just approve this with backup TBD; I'll fill it in next week." Politely decline. If the submitter has authority to commit, they commit (and the record goes Live with the field populated). If they do not have authority, escalate. Approving with TBD undermines the register.

OT asset in scope

OT submissions often look unusual — vendor-managed firmware, no domain join, no EDR coverage. This is correct for OT. The reviewer's role is to confirm the OT-appropriate alternatives (vendor monitoring, segmentation, manual procedures) are documented. Don't reject for absence of IT-style controls.

Asset referenced in an active incident

If the asset is involved in a currently-open Sev 1 / Sev 2 incident (per GRC-IRP-01), pause the review. Asset records changing during an incident can disrupt response. Coordinate with the Incident Commander; resume review after incident closure unless the IC directs otherwise.

Section 8
Evidence Capture & Audit Trail

Every review — approve or reject — produces audit evidence. Auditors will sample reviews; consistent evidence makes audit straightforward.

8.1 Per-review evidence

ArtefactWhat it capturesRetention
Submission recordThe original submission as received, with submitter identity and timestamp7 years
Reviewer disposition recordApprove / Request clarification / Reject; reviewer identity; date/time; checklist version applied7 years
Feedback to submitterSpecific items requested; specific failures cited with section references3 years
Approval recordAsset ID; status transition Onboarding→Live; first review due dateWhile asset is in any non-Archived state, then 7 years
Waivers appliedAny deviations from standard interpretation and the rationale7 years

8.2 Aggregate evidence

The Head of GRC produces aggregate metrics monthly:

  • Submissions received vs reviewed; backlog vs SLA
  • Approval rate (target: >80% on first attempt; lower implies submission quality issue)
  • Most common rejection reasons (drives training and form improvement)
  • Time-to-disposition by Tier
  • Reviewer load distribution (no single reviewer should be the bottleneck)

These metrics are reviewed quarterly and feed into the annual review of GRC-SOP-05.

Section 9
Tooling & Workflow Mechanics

The current implementation is documentation-only — submissions are captured by form (today via the Asset Onboarding Form, future delivery), processed manually by GRC, and the register is maintained as a static page in the Knowledge Base.

9.1 Today (static documentation)

  • Submitter fills the Asset Onboarding Form and emails it to the GRC team mailbox
  • GRC reviewer applies this Work Instruction to the submission
  • On approval, the GRC team adds the entry to GRC-REG-ASSET and commits to the KB git repository
  • The KB rebuilds and the entry is visible

9.2 Future (Azure-hosted GRC application)

Once the planned migration to Azure is complete (see governance change log for Phase 1/2 plan), submissions will land in a backend with structured fields. This Work Instruction will continue to apply — the checks remain identical — but the workflow becomes more efficient: automated naming-conformance validation, real-time duplicate detection, integrated reviewer dashboards, and audit logging without manual intervention.

The reviewer judgement, however, does not change. Tools surface candidates; people approve.
