NW-WI-03: Block East-West Traffic

Tool-agnostic procedure for restricting internal (east-west) network traffic to stop lateral movement, internal scanning, and ransomware propagation — reducing blast radius during active incidents once approved.

🌐 Network threats Tier 1 / 2 Requires approval High-impact action

Section 1
Purpose

Provide a consistent, tool-agnostic procedure to restrict or block east-west (internal) network traffic to stop internal reconnaissance and scanning, prevent lateral movement, reduce blast radius during active incidents, and contain ransomware spread and credential abuse.

📌 Scope boundary: This WI does not authorise containment decisions. It defines how to implement internal blocking safely once approved. East-west blocking is a high-impact action — always use the minimum necessary scope.

⚠️ East-west blocking is complementary to — not a replacement for — endpoint isolation. If the threat is on an endpoint, consider EP-RB-02: Isolate Endpoint in parallel.

Section 5
Pre-Checks & Safety Notes
🚫 Mandatory constraints
Do NOT apply broad segment isolation without Tier-2 and Network Operations approval
Do NOT block management, backup, or monitoring networks without explicit validation
Do NOT use east-west blocking as a replacement for endpoint isolation
Prefer source-based host restrictions over broad segment blocks wherever possible

⚠️ Broad segment isolation can disrupt critical services, authentication, and backup operations. Always confirm with Network Ops if significant business impact is possible before proceeding.

Section 6
Step-by-Step Actions
1
Validate Scope & Targets
1
Review the approved scope — source(s), destination(s), protocols, and ports. Confirm all identifiers match what was observed in network activity and that scope aligns exactly with the approval.
If any ambiguity exists — pause and escalate before proceeding.
2
Select Blocking Strategy

✅ Host-to-host (preferred)

Most precise — restricts a specific source to a specific destination

Host-to-segment

Blocks one host from reaching an entire segment

Protocol-specific

Block a protocol only — e.g. SMB/RPC — across hosts or segments

Segment-to-segment

Broadest — use only when all traffic between segments is approved for blocking

📌 Always use the minimum scope that achieves containment. Segment-to-segment blocks require Tier-2 + Network Operations approval.

3
Implement East-West Blocking Control
1
Using the approved internal control, apply the restriction. Common controls include:
  • Internal firewall deny rule (host or segment)
  • Network segmentation policy
  • NAC quarantine profile
  • Protocol-specific ACL (e.g. deny SMB from source)
2
Record: rule or policy name/ID, source and destination scope, duration, timestamp, operator
4
Validate Containment Effectiveness
1
Verify:
  • Blocked traffic is now denied — deny events appear in logs
  • No new internal scanning or lateral movement attempts succeed
  • Management, monitoring, and backup traffic is unaffected
2
If activity persists — do not widen scope. Escalate to Tier-2 for reassessment.
5
Coordinate with Endpoint Containment
1
Confirm alignment with endpoint isolation status (EP-RB-02). Ensure the network block complements but does not conflict with endpoint containment controls.
6
Monitor for Secondary Behaviour
1
Post-block, monitor for:
  • Alternate protocols or paths used to circumvent the block
  • Credential abuse attempts from the contained host(s)
  • New C2 indicators or callback attempts
  • Ransomware propagation signals from other hosts
2
Escalate immediately if new indicators appear — the threat may have already spread before containment
7
Preserve Evidence & Document
1
Retain logs showing pre-block activity and block enforcement. Do not remove rules or logs without explicit Tier-2 instruction.
2
Update the incident record with: scope and strategy applied, control and rule references, timestamp, validation outcome, observed business impact. Set a review date if the block is temporary. Attach screenshots or logs.
Section 7
Expected Outcome

✅ Internal reconnaissance or lateral movement blocked
✅ Blast radius reduced — threat contained to the minimum necessary scope
✅ Endpoint isolation and network block aligned
✅ Clear audit trail with rule reference, scope, and timestamp

Section 8
Common Mistakes to Avoid
Blocking an entire segment when a host-level block is sufficient — always use minimum scope
Failing to check management and backup traffic dependencies before applying broad blocks
Expanding scope without Tier-2 approval when activity persists
Treating east-west blocking as a substitute for endpoint isolation — they are complementary controls
Leaving temporary blocks without a review date — stale rules accumulate and become security risks
Section 9
Escalation Conditions
Blocking causes service disruption — authentication, backup, or monitoring affected
Multiple segments require isolation — scope is beyond what was approved
Lateral movement or ransomware propagation persists post-block
Ransomware indicators are escalating rapidly — incident may require IR Lead engagement
Critical systems — DC, backup servers, monitoring — are within the affected scope
