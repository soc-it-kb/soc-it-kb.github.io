GRC-TTX-05 — Azure Region Failure During Business-Critical Period

Tabletop scenario: Azure declares a major service event affecting the agency’s primary region while clinical, educational, or financial-cycle processes are operating at peak. Tests the multi-workload failover decisions in GRC-DR-WI-02 Scenario 1. Technical lean — IT and Cloud Platform teams primary; CMT and BPOs in support.

Primary documents tested
DR-WI-02 (Scenario 1), IT-CL-WI-04, workload runbooks

Azure region events do happen — they are rare but real, and when they occur, the agency’s workload-by-workload failover design is what determines whether recovery happens at the documented RTO or whether the agency discovers gaps the hard way. This scenario tests both the design (does it actually work as documented?) and the coordination (failing over multiple workloads independently is harder than it sounds when they share dependencies).

Objectives
  • Test the per-workload failover procedures: are they current, runnable, and validated for the actual configurations in production?
  • Test the cross-workload coordination — the sequencing of foundation services, data layer, application layer, and integration endpoints
  • Test the “confirm region-scope event vs service-level event” decision before initiating failover — premature failover risks split-state
  • Test the failback decision and sequencing — failback is itself a controlled change, not an automatic reversal
  • Test the communication with dependent BPOs during the failover window: business operations need to know what is happening and what to expect

Ground rules
  • No-fault learning environment.
  • Participants may reference real workload runbooks, Azure portal, network diagrams. Inability to access something is a finding.
  • Failover actions are spoken (“I would manually fail over the SQL database failover group”) not executed. Facilitator confirms outcomes.
  • The scenario assumes failover designs exist per IT-CL-WI-04. Where they do not, that is the first major finding.

Initial impact
Multiple workloads degraded

Initial situation: at 10:00 local time, multiple monitoring alerts triggered. A clinical/financial/student application is reporting slow database response; an HR application is unreachable; a document workflow application is partially responsive. The Cloud Platform Lead checks the Azure status portal: an investigation is underway, region [primary region], multiple service categories. The IT DR Coordinator joins. The first BPO has called about user impact.

What participants know at T+0:

  • Azure investigating an event in the primary region; service-category impact still being characterised
  • Multiple agency workloads showing degraded performance in different ways
  • Tier-1 and Tier-2 workloads include identity, EHR/SIS, finance, HR, document workflow
  • It is a peak operating period for at least one critical process
Injects
T+10 min Confirm region-scope or service-level event?

The Cloud Platform Lead reads the Azure status update: “Customers may experience issues with Compute, Storage, and SQL resources in [region].” Three named service categories. The IT DR Coordinator asks: is this region-scope or service-level? Is the threshold for initiating failover met?

Discussion prompts
  • Per DR-WI-02 Scenario 1.1, what is the criterion for region-scope vs service-level?
  • Who has authority to declare a region failover — per workload, or coordinated decision?
  • What is the risk of premature failover (split-state) vs delayed failover (extended RTO breach)?
  • How does the agency monitor Azure status — subscribed alerts, manual checking, vendor channel? Is the subscription current?
T+20 min Per-workload runbook execution begins

The DR Coordinator declares region-failover invocation. IT Service Owners begin per-workload procedures. The first Service Owner asks: my runbook references “the failover SQL endpoint” but the endpoint hostname has changed since the runbook was last reviewed. Is there a current version anywhere? A second Service Owner asks: my workload depends on identity, which has not yet been confirmed as failed-over — do I wait?

Discussion prompts
  • Stale runbook information — is this a one-off or systemic? What is the runbook review cadence per the DR policy?
  • Identity is multi-region by Microsoft design and does not need explicit failover — is that documented anywhere where Service Owners can find it?
  • Sequencing across workloads: foundation services first (identity / DNS / Front Door), data second, application third — is this written down or relying on collective memory?
  • The IT DR Coordinator’s role: are they coordinating or executing? Distinction matters under pressure.
T+35 min Front Door endpoint change; manual DNS for non-Front-Door workload

Front Door-fronted workloads automatically begin routing to the secondary region. One workload — the older document-workflow application — is not behind Front Door; its public DNS still points to the primary-region public IP. Network Engineering reports: the DNS change to the secondary-region IP can be made in 5 minutes but propagation depends on TTLs — some configurations have 24-hour TTLs.

Discussion prompts
  • Are the agency’s public DNS TTLs configured for resilience-friendly values? When were they last reviewed?
  • For the workload not behind Front Door — is this an architecture gap that should be treated post-event regardless of how this scenario ends?
  • What is the user impact if DNS takes hours to propagate? Communication to users?
T+50 min SQL failover-group manual failover required for two workloads

SQL Database failover groups for two workloads are configured manual-failover (not automatic). The DR Coordinator asks who initiates: the Service Owners directly, or the Cloud Platform Lead centrally? The procedure is in the workload runbooks — in different places, with slightly different language. The first Service Owner initiates and reports success. The second Service Owner is unsure of the exact command sequence under stress.

Discussion prompts
  • Should SQL failover-groups be automatic or manual? What are the trade-offs? Are they used consistently across workloads?
  • Runbook variation across workloads — how does the agency standardise enough to make procedures usable across teams?
  • The second Service Owner’s uncertainty — is it a runbook quality issue, a training issue, or a stress / fatigue issue? All three?
  • How is the agency tracking RTO progress in real time? Is the dashboard, status check-in, or other mechanism documented?
T+70 min Application healthy on secondary but reads stale data

One Tier-2 workload reports healthy on the secondary region but a Service Owner notices: a row of data updated at 09:55 (5 minutes before the failure began) is not visible. The data is on the primary-region database which was the active write target until 10:00. RPO commitment for this workload was ≤ 4 hours — technically met — but the user-facing concern is real. The BPO asks: what does the user see, and what does the agency say?

Discussion prompts
  • RPO is a contract; user expectation may be different. How is the gap communicated honestly?
  • Is there a procedure for users to recover data committed in the RPO window after failback? Is it documented?
  • The synchronous vs asynchronous replication choice was made when this workload was designed — is the trade-off (cost vs RPO) still appropriate now that the agency has experienced one?
T+4 hours Microsoft declares incident resolved; failback discussion

Microsoft has declared the primary-region incident resolved. Workloads are stable on the secondary region. The Cloud Platform Lead recommends: not failing back today — wait for Microsoft’s post-incident report; schedule failback in a maintenance window in the next 48-72 hours.

Discussion prompts
  • Failback during business hours doubles the operational exposure window — is the recommendation to defer accepted by CMT?
  • Failback to a region that may regress in the immediate hours post-event is risky — how is that risk assessed?
  • Some workloads may have accumulated state on the secondary that needs to migrate back — is the failback procedure as well-documented as the failover?
  • When does the AAR happen? Who is included? What feeds into it?

Evaluation criteria
  • Objective 1 (per-workload procedures): runbooks current and usable; commands executable under stress; gaps documented for treatment
  • Objective 2 (cross-workload coordination): sequencing explicit; foundation-first applied; IT DR Coordinator’s coordinating role distinct from executing roles
  • Objective 3 (region-scope decision): made on evidence (Azure status, scope of impact), not assumption; documented criterion applied
  • Objective 4 (failback decision and sequencing): planned, not improvised; scheduled in maintenance window; failback procedure documented
  • Objective 5 (BPO communication): cadence established; technical detail translated for business consumption; RPO communicated honestly
  • Cross-cutting: the agency’s recovery tier inventory is the basis for prioritisation throughout
After-action review framework

Conducted within 14 days. Distribution: IT DR Coordinator, Cloud Platform Lead, all participating Service Owners, CMT Lead, BCDR Steering Committee, CISO.

Common findings: runbook stale content for workloads not exercised recently; SQL failover-group configuration inconsistency; non-Front-Door workloads as architecture gap; DNS TTLs not resilience-tuned; cross-workload coordination ad hoc rather than documented; BPO communication cadence absent under technical pressure; failback procedure less documented than failover.
