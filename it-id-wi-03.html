IT-ID-WI-03: Role Design & Entitlement Management

The role catalogue: every assignable role has a documented owner, defined scope, naming convention, and review cycle. Group hygiene (nesting limits, empty-group cleanup, ownership). Entitlement packages for common access bundles (Entra Identity Governance). Separation of duties enforcement. Role-change workflow with direct-edit detection. Quarterly catalogue review. Monthly drift check on T0/T1. Operationalises §4.3 of IT-ID-SOP-01.

Prerequisites

Before applying this WI:

  • Authoritative source of identity established (IT-ID-WI-01) — role design needs reliable identity data to land on
  • Access tier classification documented per resource / system — T0 / T1 / T2 / T3 mapping consistent with core-concepts.html
  • Asset Register (GRC-REG-ASSET) at least partially populated — roles are scoped to assets; roles without identified assets are not actionable
  • Entra ID licensing checked — Entra Identity Governance entitlement packages need Entra ID P2 / E5; without those, the manual catalogue + Entra group pattern applies
  • For agencies with on-prem AD: AD group inventory baseline available — existing groups, members, owners, last-modified dates
  • Documented role catalogue destination — SharePoint list, GRC tooling, or Entra IG catalogue depending on tenant
  • Naming convention agreement across agencies (some agencies have legacy conventions that pre-date this baseline; document the transition state)
Role design vs role discovery. Most estates start with role discovery — observing how access is actually being granted and naming patterns retroactively. This WI is about design — defining the role catalogue intentionally so future access requests select from a documented inventory rather than ad-hoc group creation. Both phases are real; this WI helps the design phase, and the discovery work feeds it.
Step-by-step instructions

The steps below operationalise IT-ID-SOP-01 §4.3.

1
Establish the role catalogue
A documented, owned, reviewed inventory of every role that can be assigned to an identity.

The role catalogue is the single source of truth for assignable access. Without it, role design is an oral tradition.

  • Catalogue scope: every role / group that can be assigned to a human or non-human identity. Includes Entra roles, Entra groups, AD groups, system-specific roles in business applications (where managed via SSO / SCIM), entitlement packages.
  • Per-role record: role name (per naming convention — Step 2), description (plain-language purpose), scope (what resources / systems it grants access to), tier (T0/T1/T2/T3), named owner, justification (why this role exists), last-review date, last-review reviewer.
  • Discovery feed: existing groups in the directory enumerated; those without a catalogue record become discovery items; remediated within a documented timeframe.
  • Storage: Entra Identity Governance catalogue where licensed; SharePoint / GRC list where not. Either way, exportable, auditable, retained.
  • Coverage target: 100% of T0 and T1 roles in catalogue from day one of programme; T2/T3 build out over 6–12 months; aim for ≥95% coverage at end of FY 2026.
Verify. Catalogue exists; T0/T1 coverage at 100%; T2/T3 trending toward 95%; per-role records have all required fields populated. Sample 5 roles, trace from catalogue back to actual directory object — consistency confirmed.
NIST 800-53 AC-2(a) · CIS Controls 6.1 · ISO 27001 A.5.18
2
Apply naming convention discipline
Role names are self-describing; an analyst can infer scope and tier from the name alone.

Naming convention is unglamorous and high-leverage. A well-named role is one that doesn't need to be looked up.

  • Standard structure: <domain>-<scope>-<function>-<environment>-<tier>. Example: azure-vm-operations-contributor-prod-t1. Each segment uses a documented vocabulary.
  • Tier suffix for privileged roles makes T0/T1 visible at a glance and supports rule-based detection (e.g. alerts on direct-assignment to any role ending in -t0).
  • Function vocabulary: reader, contributor, operator, approver, owner, break-glass, auditor. Avoid ambiguous terms like "admin" without further qualification.
  • Environment vocabulary: prod, preprod, dev, test, shared. Cross-environment roles deliberately rare and flagged.
  • Legacy naming transition: estates with pre-existing groups will have names that don't conform. Document the transition state in the catalogue; rename in waves where the operational risk is acceptable; otherwise alias and document.
  • Forbidden patterns: spaces, special characters beyond hyphen, embedded user names (e.g. john-vm-access), embedded ticket numbers, names that don't survive a rename of their target system.
  • Tools: validation regex applied at role creation; non-conforming creates rejected automatically (with documented exception path for genuine edge cases).
Verify. Naming-convention document published. Validation regex active. Sample 10 recent role creations — conformance ≥90%; non-conforming have documented exception. Legacy-rename pipeline if applicable.
ISO 27001 A.5.18 · operational hygiene
3
Design roles with least-privilege defaults
Roles aggregate the minimum permissions for a defined function, not the maximum that's convenient.

The path of least resistance is to clone an existing over-permissive role and call it a new one. Resist.

  • Start from the function, not the existing roles. What does this user / job need to do? Map the actions to the minimum permissions that enable them.
  • Built-in roles preferred over custom where they fit the function — built-in roles are documented, reviewed by the platform vendor, and broadly understood. Custom roles deepen the maintenance burden.
  • Custom roles when needed: built-in roles that are wider than necessary should be replaced with custom roles scoped to actual need. Document the actions included; document why not built-in.
  • Scope at the lowest meaningful level: subscription-level role assignment is rarely necessary; resource-group or resource-level scope usually suffices. Same for AD groups — OU scope where applicable, not domain-wide.
  • Read vs write distinction: viewer / reader / read-only variants of every role where the function allows. Many users need to see what's happening, not change it.
  • Time-bounded permissions where possible — PIM eligibility for elevated roles (IT-ID-WI-05); resource locks for write operations on protected resources; just-enough-administration patterns.
  • Avoid wildcard / inheritance traps: assigning at a high scope and "letting it inherit downward" creates blast-radius nobody intended; scope explicitly.
Verify. Sample 3 recently created roles; for each, scope is at the lowest meaningful level; permissions are the minimum for the documented function; reader variant exists where applicable. Custom-role count vs built-in ratio is tracked; explosive growth flagged.
NIST 800-53 AC-6 · CIS Controls 6.8 · ISO 27001 A.5.18
4
Group hygiene — nesting, ownership, lifecycle
Groups are the workhorses of access; un-hygienic groups are how access becomes invisible.

Group sprawl is the most common pathology in estates with long history.

  • Nesting depth limit: maximum 3 levels of group-in-group nesting. Beyond 3 levels, access becomes invisible — "this user has access because group A contains group B contains group C contains group D contains them."
  • Circular references prohibited: group A includes group B, group B includes group A — symbolic but it happens and breaks tooling.
  • Empty groups: flagged after 30 days; archived after 90 days unless documented justification. Empty groups are either pre-provisioning shells (acceptable, time-bounded) or detritus from past structures.
  • Group ownership documented per group: at least one named owner, ideally two (avoiding the single-owner-departure orphan problem). Owner changes part of leaver process (IT-ID-WI-01 Step 4).
  • Membership review: per IT-ID-WI-02; group owners are the named reviewers for their groups.
  • Dynamic group rules (Entra) reviewed: dynamic memberships look elegant but the rule itself becomes the access-granting object; review the rules, not just the resulting memberships.
  • Distribution lists vs security groups: separate concerns. Don't add security-relevant access to distribution lists or vice versa; tooling and naming should differentiate.
  • Mail-enabled security groups: a hybrid; tolerated but called out in catalogue for the dual purpose.
Verify. Group inventory shows: nesting depth ≤3 across ≥99% of groups; circular references zero; empty-group count trending down; ownership populated for ≥95% of security-relevant groups; dynamic-group rule audit recent.
NIST 800-53 AC-2 · ISO 27001 A.5.18
5
Entitlement packages for common access bundles
Bundle commonly-requested access combinations into named packages with documented approval workflows.

Entra Identity Governance entitlement packages (available with Entra ID P2 / E5) operationalise access bundling: a package contains a set of roles / groups; access is requested as the package; approval is per-package; expiry is per-package.

  • Identify common bundles from joiner-pack patterns, project-team patterns, role-change patterns: "Finance Analyst Onboarding," "Project Phoenix Team," "Marketing Campaign Reviewer."
  • Per-package definition: name, included resources (roles / groups / apps), eligible requestors (who can ask for this), approvers (who decides), duration (time-bounded by default).
  • Approval routing: line-manager approval default; system-owner approval where the package touches their system; Privileged Access Authority for any T0/T1-touching package.
  • Self-service request for the user, not the IAE team: requester goes to MyAccess portal (or equivalent), browses available packages, requests, justifies, awaits approval.
  • Time-bounded: every package has a default expiry; renewals require re-justification; permanent assignment (no expiry) reserved for steady-state job-required access.
  • Tenants without Entra IG: a manual catalogue with documented bundles and approval workflows in ServiceNow or equivalent; mechanically clunkier but the same governance shape.
  • Package owner: a named individual who maintains the package definition, reviews its membership-eligibility, retires the package when no longer needed.
Verify. Catalogue lists entitlement packages with owners, approvers, durations; common access patterns covered by packages (not ad-hoc); user self-service portal works; package expiry / renewal cycle observed.
CIS Controls 6.8 · ISO 27001 A.5.18 · Entra IG
6
Separation of duties enforcement
Sensitive role combinations are forbidden in the catalogue itself, not just in policy text.

"No single user creates and approves payments" is a policy statement. The control is the catalogue refusing to grant both roles to the same identity.

  • SoD pairs documented: pairs of roles that must not be held by the same identity. Examples: "create payment" + "approve payment"; "create user" + "assign privileged role"; "modify firewall rule" + "delete firewall log"; "approve change" + "implement change" (for high-risk changes).
  • Pairs sourced from: business risk assessment (finance / regulatory pairs), security risk assessment (audit-tampering pairs), framework guidance (NIST 800-53 AC-5, ISO 27001 A.5.3).
  • Enforcement: Entra IG access reviews can detect SoD conflicts; access request workflow rejects when a pair would be created; periodic detection campaigns catch historical conflicts.
  • Approved-exception mechanism: legitimate cases exist (small agency where one person genuinely covers two functions); documented as named exception with compensating control (e.g. second-person review of all activities by that user).
  • Detection without enforcement — for AD-only scopes where tooling can't prevent the assignment, run a monthly detection script flagging conflicts; remediate.
  • SoD policy reviewed annually: business processes change; new SoD pairs emerge from new processes; obsolete pairs retired.
Verify. SoD pairs document published; enforcement active where supported; detection script running where not; SoD-conflict count trending down; named exceptions tracked with compensating controls.
NIST 800-53 AC-5 · ISO 27001 A.5.3 · SOC 2 CC6.1
7
Role-change workflow
Changes to role definitions follow a documented workflow; ad-hoc changes detected and remediated.

Roles drift when permission changes happen quietly. A role that grew an extra permission "just to fix something urgent" is the role that's now over-permissive.

  • Role-modification workflow: any change to a role definition (added permission, scope change, name change, owner change) goes through a documented ticket with system-owner approval. Includes built-in role re-purposing within a tenant.
  • Direct-edit detection: where the directory platform supports it, alert on role / group direct edits that bypass the workflow (e.g. PowerShell-driven changes by an admin without a corresponding ticket).
  • Backfill ticket: detected direct-edits trigger a backfill request to legitimise (or revert) the change with proper approval. Investigate root cause.
  • Emergency change path documented: for genuine emergencies (incident response, business-critical break-fix), accelerated approval available with retrospective documentation; not a routine pattern.
  • Role-version history retained: which permissions, when, by whom, why — minimum 1 year; longer for T0/T1 roles.
  • Owner sign-off on changes to roles they own: owner-blocked changes don't proceed regardless of requestor's authority.
Verify. Role-change tickets in last quarter have system-owner approval recorded. Direct-edit alerts firing where applicable; backfill SLA met. Role-version history available; spot-check a recent role for full change history.
NIST 800-53 AC-3 · ISO 27001 A.5.18
8
Quarterly role catalogue review
Roles unused for 12 months are flagged for retirement; over-broad roles are flagged for splitting; the catalogue stays current.
  • Usage telemetry: per-role activation count, last-assignment date, member count, last-use date (where the directory exposes this). Quarterly snapshot.
  • Retirement candidates: roles unassigned for 12 months; roles with declining usage; roles superseded by newer designs. Owner consulted before retirement; retired roles archived (not deleted) for 90 days then deleted.
  • Splitting candidates: roles with very high member counts and diverse use cases; roles where audit / recertification findings cluster; roles touching multiple tiers (the role is doing too much).
  • Consolidation candidates: near-duplicate roles ("operator-prod" and "operations-prod" doing the same thing); minor permission variants that proliferate; combined and renamed with owner sign-off.
  • Owner stale-check: roles whose owner has left or moved out of the supporting function; reassign owner or retire.
  • Review evidence retained: which roles examined, decisions, applied changes; quarterly summary to GRC.
Verify. Last quarterly review completed; retirement / consolidation actions in flight; review evidence captured; owner-stale list addressed.
NIST 800-53 AC-2(j) · ISO 27001 A.5.18
9
Tier-0 / Tier-1 monthly drift check
High-tier role assignments are sanity-checked against the catalogue more often than the standard quarterly cycle.
  • Monthly extract: every T0 and T1 role assignment in the directory (Entra PIM eligibilities, AD privileged groups, equivalent on-prem objects).
  • Diff against catalogue: assignments not in catalogue, assignments in catalogue but with assignees not in the expected eligible list, role definitions whose permissions changed since last snapshot.
  • Drift items investigated: each unexplained drift opens an investigation; either a legitimate change that didn't follow workflow (backfill), or an unauthorised change (security investigation).
  • Coordinate with IT-ID-WI-05: privileged eligibility review covers who's eligible; this step covers what does the role itself look like and is it intact.
  • Evidence retained for audit: monthly extract, diff report, remediation actions.
Verify. Monthly extract running; recent diff reports show low drift count; drift items in active investigation or resolved within 14 days.
NIST 800-53 AC-6(7) · ISO 27001 A.8.2

Rollback & troubleshooting

Role design changes are reversible but with care — rolling back a role rename, scope change, or permission tightening can break dependencies you didn't know about.

Common scenarios

  • Role retirement broke a downstream system — cause: role was used by an integration / service that wasn't captured in the inventory. Recovery: re-create the role (or restore from archive); investigate the integration; re-plan retirement with the dependency now known.
  • Permission tightening broke a legitimate use case — cause: function-mapping was incomplete; some valid actions were excluded. Recovery: identify the missing permissions; add them back with documented justification; review the function map.
  • SoD enforcement blocked a legitimate small-agency case — cause: small agency where one person genuinely covers both functions. Recovery: documented exception with compensating control (second-person review); SoD remains policy default for larger agencies.
  • Naming rename broke a script — cause: script referenced the old role / group name directly. Recovery: alias for transition period; update the script; document in the change log.
  • Group nesting flatten broke inheritance — cause: a deep nest was actually load-bearing; flattening removed a permission path. Recovery: restore the nest; redesign the access pattern more deliberately; pull the flatten into the deliberate-design pipeline.
  • Entitlement package wasn't being requested by the people it was designed for — cause: package was too narrow or too broad; users were going around it. Recovery: redesign package to match actual need; communicate; deprecate ad-hoc paths.

Rollback procedure

  1. Identify what changed and which entities are affected
  2. Apply the rollback (restore from catalogue history, re-create role, re-add member) with documented change record
  3. Communicate with affected users and dependency owners
  4. Run a post-mortem to identify what was missed; update the catalogue / dependency map
  5. Plan the re-attempt with the lessons applied
Common errors & tips
  • Role design is a programme, not a project. The catalogue is never "done." New systems, new processes, organisational change all create role-design work. Resource it as ongoing capacity, not a one-time clean-up.
  • Discovery feeds design, design doesn't replace discovery. An estate with 10 years of group history can't be designed top-down ignoring what exists. Discover what's there, decide what should be there, plan the transition.
  • Naming convention saves more time than it costs. Five minutes spent on a good role name saves hours of confusion across the role's lifetime. Don't compromise on naming for short-term speed.
  • Built-in roles are usually wider than you'd design. Vendor built-ins are deliberately broad to satisfy many customers. Custom roles fit your needs better — but every custom role is maintenance debt. Choose carefully.
  • Group nesting is invisible access. When access is granted four levels deep, no one knows who has it. Limit the depth; review the dynamic-group rules; flatten where it doesn't break things.
  • Entitlement packages move work to where the information is. Line managers know what their team needs; system owners know what their system requires. Packages capture both, automate the routing, and remove IAE as the bottleneck.
  • SoD enforcement is most contentious where it matters most. The agencies that resist SoD pairs hardest are usually the ones with the most operational coupling between roles. Compensating controls (second-person review) are the bridge.
  • Direct edits will happen. Detect them, don't try to prevent every one. Some are emergencies; some are legitimate admin work that should have been a ticket. The backfill workflow legitimises the legitimate and surfaces the not.
  • Retirement is harder than creation. Removing a role surfaces dependencies you didn't know existed. Archive before delete; communicate before archive; observe before communicate.
  • Standing assignments for T0 roles are the easy way to break the programme. Every standing T0 assignment is one more thing that doesn't need PIM activation, doesn't get logged at activation time, doesn't have the time-bound discipline. Push everything possible through PIM eligibility (per IT-ID-WI-05).
  • Role design is where IAE earns its name. JML is operational; recertification is administrative; role design is engineering. It rewards thoughtfulness and is the highest-leverage of the three.
