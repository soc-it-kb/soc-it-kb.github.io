GRC-BCP-SOP-01 — Business Continuity Policy

The Institution’s business-audience policy for business continuity. Defines agency obligations, plan ownership, escalation and invocation authority, crisis management governance, and plan currency requirements. Uses the methodology in GRC-BCDR-SOP-01; uses the procedures in WI-01 (BIA) and WI-02 (exercises).

Business-audience policy Agency obligations Crisis governance
1 — Purpose

This policy establishes the Institution’s commitment to continuing the delivery of critical services to beneficiaries and stakeholders during disruption, the obligations on each agency to maintain a business continuity programme, and the governance arrangements for invocation, escalation, and ongoing assurance.

It is a policy document. It states what must be done and by whom. It does not state how — methodology is in GRC-BCDR-SOP-01; procedures are in the BIA and Exercise WIs and in agency-authored continuity plans.

2 — Scope & applicability

This policy applies to all agencies of the Institution and to all critical business processes within those agencies. Each agency operates under this policy through an agency-specific implementation that reflects its mandate, geographic footprint, and regulatory environment.

In scope:

  • All processes identified as critical by an agency’s BIA (i.e. Mission-critical, Business-critical, or Important per the methodology classification).
  • All sites, country offices, and operating units delivering or supporting those processes.
  • Disruption from any cause: cyber, technology, supplier, physical, people, regional.

Out of scope:

  • Processes classed as Standard by BIA (no continuity plan required, though general resilience is expected).
  • Day-to-day operational incidents not rising to the threshold of continuity invocation.
  • Cyber incident response procedures (in GRC-IRP-01); BCP engages alongside when continuity is invoked.
3 — Policy statements

The Institution commits to the following:

  1. Every agency shall maintain a current business continuity programme covering at least all Mission-critical and Business-critical processes identified by its BIA.
  2. Every critical business process shall have a named Business Process Owner accountable for the continuity plan covering that process.
  3. Every critical business process shall have a current Business Process Continuity Plan populated using the central template (GRC-FRM-BCP-01), reviewed and signed off at least every 12 months, and updated immediately on material change.
  4. The agency’s BIA shall be refreshed at least every 24 months using the methodology in GRC-BCDR-SOP-01 and the procedure in GRC-BCDR-WI-01.
  5. Each Mission-critical process shall be exercised at least annually using one of the exercise types defined in GRC-BCDR-WI-02. Each Business-critical process shall be exercised at least every 24 months.
  6. Exercise outcomes shall be documented with named findings and assigned owners, tracked to closure through agency governance, with overdue findings escalated to the BCDR Steering Committee.
  7. Invocation authority is named in each plan. Invocation decisions are made by a named role or delegate authority, never ad hoc. The threshold for invocation is documented in each plan.
  8. Crisis Management Teams shall be established and trained per agency and per major site, with named members and tested communication paths.
  9. Gaps in plan coverage, currency, or exercise compliance shall be recorded as exceptions in the GRC exception register with a named owner and remediation date; persistent or material gaps shall be escalated to the BCDR Steering Committee and recorded as risks in the risk register.
  10. All continuity plans shall be accessible during disruption — not held only in systems that themselves may be unavailable. Offline / out-of-band copies are required for Tier-1 processes.
  11. The continuity programme shall be aligned to recognised frameworks: ISO 22301 for the management system; ISO 27001 A.5.29/A.5.30 for information-security aspects of continuity; sector-specific frameworks where applicable (e.g. healthcare regulator continuity requirements in jurisdictions where AKHS operates).

5 — Invocation & escalation

Invocation is the formal decision to activate a continuity plan in response to actual disruption. The threshold and authority for invocation are defined in each Business Process Continuity Plan, but the following principles apply across the programme:

  • Invocation is a documented decision. Time of decision, decision-maker, and basis are recorded contemporaneously. Plans must accommodate decision-making under uncertainty — do not require certainty before invoking.
  • Lower bar for Tier-1 processes. Where beneficiary safety is impacted, the threshold for invocation should be deliberately low — the cost of unnecessary invocation is far less than delayed invocation.
  • Crisis Management Team activates on invocation. Once the plan is invoked the CMT is convened (in person or virtually) and operates until the disruption is resolved and the plan is formally stood down.
  • Escalation paths are named. Single-process invocation may resolve at the agency level; multi-process or cross-agency invocation escalates to Group leadership; events with regulatory, reputational, or beneficiary-safety dimensions engage Communications and General Counsel.
  • Cyber incidents that trigger continuity invoke both BCP and the IRP. The two run in parallel: the IRP manages cyber response; BCP manages business continuity workarounds. The Crisis Management Team coordinates between them.
  • Stand-down is also a documented decision. Stand-down occurs when the disruption is resolved and the recovered processes are sustainably operating. An after-action review is mandatory within 30 days.
6 — Plan currency & assurance

Currency is the single most common failure mode for continuity programmes — plans exist but have not been reviewed, exercised, or updated since major changes. The following assurance mechanisms apply:

  • Document control table. Every plan includes a document control table with last review date, next review date, change history, and approval signatures.
  • Portfolio reporting. The agency BCP Coordinator maintains a portfolio view of all plans showing currency status. The BCDR Steering Committee receives quarterly summary across agencies.
  • Currency exceptions. Plans past their review date or missed exercises are logged in the exception register with a remediation date; persistent overdue items are escalated.
  • Material-change trigger. Plans are not only reviewed on cadence — material changes (new process, lost dependency, personnel turnover at named-role level, significant exercise findings) require immediate plan update.
  • Post-exercise update. Exercise findings feed back into the plan within 90 days; plans are updated even where findings are not formally accepted as defects.
  • Post-invocation update. A real invocation is the strongest evidence the plan can produce. After-action review findings are incorporated within 60 days of stand-down.
  • Internal audit cycle. Internal Audit tests the BCP programme on a multi-year cycle; findings are tracked alongside operational findings.
7 — Exceptions & non-compliance

Where conformance with this policy is genuinely not feasible — e.g. a new agency in early programme establishment, a process undergoing fundamental restructure, or an exercise that cannot be scheduled within cadence due to operational constraint — an exception is recorded.

Exception requirements:

  • Documented justification for non-conformance.
  • Named owner accountable for remediation.
  • Time-bounded approval (default 12 months; longer requires Steering Committee approval).
  • Compensating measures where applicable.
  • Recorded in the GRC Exception Register.

Persistent or material non-compliance escalates to a risk register entry per GRC-SOP-04 and is treated as an Institutional risk rather than a procedural exception.
