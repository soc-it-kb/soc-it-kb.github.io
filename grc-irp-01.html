Section 1
Purpose, Scope & Authority

1.1 Purpose

This plan establishes how the Institution prepares for, detects, contains, eradicates, recovers from, reports on, and remediates cyber security incidents. It exists to protect the Institution's people, data, services, mission, and reputation; to ensure response is consistent, lawful, and proportionate; and to provide the documentary evidence expected by external auditors, regulators, cyber insurers, and the Risk Committee.

Effective execution of this plan delivers improved resilience, reduced incident cost and dwell time, minimised reputational and regulatory harm, and a continuous-improvement loop fed by lessons learned.

1.2 Scope

This plan covers all cyber security incidents affecting:

  • Information processed, stored, or transmitted by the Institution, regardless of medium (digital, analogue, paper)
  • Systems, services, applications, and infrastructure operated by or on behalf of the Institution, including hosted, on-premises, SaaS, and managed-service environments
  • Devices used to conduct institutional business — corporate, BYOD, supplier-managed
  • Incidents originating at third parties that affect the institution's data, services, or operations

The plan applies to all employees, contractors, volunteers, and suppliers acting on behalf of the Institution, and to all in-scope agencies, subsidiaries, and operating units (collectively "operating units"). Each operating unit completes a Local Incident Response Annex (GRC-IRP-ANNEX-TEMPLATE) that adapts this plan to local contacts, regulators, and language requirements without altering the global methodology.

1.3 Authority

This plan is approved by the Risk Committee and the CISO. Where this plan and a local annex differ, this plan takes precedence except where local law mandates otherwise. The CISO is the authoritative interpreter of this plan during an active response; outside an active response, interpretation rests with the GRC team.

1.4 Out of scope

Non-cyber incidents (purely physical security events without an information dimension, HR misconduct unrelated to information systems, financial fraud handled exclusively through finance controls) are managed under their respective frameworks. Where such an incident develops a cyber dimension, this plan is invoked.

Section 3
Definitions

Common terminology underpins effective response. The following definitions are authoritative for this plan and all derived documents.

Event
Any observable occurrence in a system or network. Aligned with NIST SP 800-61r2.
Event of interest
An event that requires investigation because it may be an incident. An event of interest is either declared an incident or closed as a nominal event.
Alert
A notification of an event of interest, originating from security tooling, the SIEM, threat intelligence, employees, partners, or external parties.
Cyber security incident ("incident")
A confirmed or imminent violation of computer security policies, acceptable use policies, or standard security practices, with potential or actual adverse effect on the organisation.
Material incident
An incident assessed as Sev 1 or Sev 2, or any incident requiring regulator notification, public communication, cyber-insurance activation, or executive briefing.
Notifiable breach
An incident meeting the legal threshold for notification under applicable data-protection or sector-specific regulation. The decision is taken in consultation with Legal and recorded in the incident record.
Threat
Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact organisational operations, assets, or individuals through an information system (NIST).
Vulnerability
Weakness in an information system, security procedure, internal control, or implementation that could be exploited or triggered by a threat source (NIST).
CSIRT — Computer Security Incident Response Team
The team — drawn from SOC, IT Operations, Identity, and external partners — assembled to respond to an incident. Membership is incident-specific and led by the Incident Commander.
Incident Commander
The named individual leading response for an active incident, responsible for tempo, decisions, and escalation. By default the on-call SOC Manager; for Sev 1/2 the role escalates to the CISO or designated deputy.
Incident Handler
The analyst or engineer carrying out hands-on investigation and remediation under the Incident Commander's direction.
Containment / Eradication / Recovery
The three operational phases of active response (NIST SP 800-61): contain limits damage; eradicate removes the cause; recover restores normal operations.
Section 4
Roles & Responsibilities

Roles are organised into two layers. Institutional roles are persistent positions with standing accountability; they align to the risk-owner model in GRC-REG-RISK. Response roles are activated during an incident and may be filled by different individuals for different incidents.

4.1 Organisational roles

CISO

Ultimate accountability for cyber response. Approves Sev 1 declarations, regulator-notification decisions, and cyber-insurance activation. Briefs Risk Committee and executive leadership. Authoritative interpreter of this plan during an active response.

CIO

Accountable for IT response and continuity. Coordinates with BC/DR. Provides technical infrastructure expertise and authorisation for service-impacting actions (isolation, shutdown, restoration). Deputises for the CISO on technology-only incidents.

Head of Cyber Security Operations

Day-to-day owner of this plan and of the SOC's operational readiness. Default Incident Commander for Sev 3 and below; deputy Incident Commander for Sev 1/2. Maintains plan, runbooks, playbooks, and tabletop programme.

Head of GRC

Owns regulator-notification decisions, evidence preservation oversight, post-incident lessons-learned, and audit liaison. Cross-references incidents to risks in GRC-REG-RISK.

Head of IT Operations

Owns Recover-phase execution: restoration of services, validation of backups, hardware/software replacement. Owns RTO/RPO commitments.

Legal Counsel

Advises on legal privilege, regulator notification thresholds, contractual obligations, ransom payment legality, law-enforcement engagement, and chain of custody. Reviews all external communications. May engage external specialist counsel.

Data Protection Officer / Privacy

Owns privacy-incident classification, data-subject notification decisions, and Article 33 (GDPR) or equivalent regulator notification preparation. Maintains the breach register.

HR

Manages internal staff communications, supports investigations involving personnel, coordinates welfare for those affected, and contributes to disciplinary outcomes where relevant.

Public Relations / Communications

Owns external messaging — media, public statements, customer/beneficiary notifications. Drafts in collaboration with Legal, Privacy, and the CISO. No external comms without sign-off from this triad.

CFO

Assesses financial impact, advises on continuity funding, authorises emergency expenditure (external IR retainer, surge resourcing), and oversees insurance claim coordination.

CEO / Agency Lead

For Sev 1/2 incidents: final decision-maker on material aspects (ransom posture, public statements, customer notifications). Briefed regularly during active response. Engaged through the CISO.

Risk Committee

Approves this plan, reviews material incidents, endorses lessons-learned actions, and maintains oversight of the IR programme. Briefed quarterly and after every Sev 1/2 incident.

4.2 Response roles (activated during an incident)

Incident Commander (IC)

Leads the response. Sets tempo, makes calls on containment/eradication, drives the escalation clock, owns the incident record. Sev 3 and below: SOC Manager. Sev 2: CISO or Deputy CISO. Sev 1: CISO; deputised only with Risk Committee notification.

Lead Analyst / Investigator

Drives technical investigation: log analysis, host forensics, malware triage, indicator extraction. Hands off to specialised investigators (DFIR, identity, network) as needed. Reports to the IC.

Comms Lead

Single point of contact for the IC into HR/PR/Legal. Coordinates internal and external messaging. Maintains the comms timeline.

Recorder / Scribe

Maintains the incident journal in the agreed system of record (RTIR / ITSM tool / journal). Time-stamps every decision and action with named actor. Critical for chain of custody and after-action.

Liaison — External

Single point of contact for external parties (MDR partner, IR retainer, law enforcement, regulator). Prevents fragmented engagement.

Recovery Lead

Runs the Recover phase: backup validation, restoration sequencing, service-by-service health checks. Hands off to BAU operations when stable.

Section 5
Severity Tiers (Sev 1–5)

Every declared incident is assigned a severity tier on a 5-point scale. Severity drives the response posture, escalation path, and SLAs. An incident's severity may evolve during response; the Incident Commander re-assesses after each major decision and at each escalation gate.

Note on terminology. Incident severity (Sev 1 = highest) is intentionally distinct from the residual-risk severity in GRC-REG-RISK (where higher scores = higher risk). They serve different purposes: risk severity asks "how bad would this be if it happened"; incident severity asks "how bad is this right now". Both are industry standard and auditors expect to see them coexist.

🚨 Sev 1 — Crisis

Whole-Institution impact

Criteria: Material disruption to Institution-wide services; suspected or confirmed compromise of Tier-0 assets; sovereign or highly sensitive data exposure; ransom demand; mandatory regulator notification likely; potential global media exposure.

Response posture: War room. CISO is Incident Commander. Risk Committee chair and CEO informed. External IR retainer activated. Cyber insurer notified.

Declare ≤ 30 minEscalate ≤ 1 hFirst exec brief ≤ 2 hDaily Risk Committee update

🔴 Sev 2 — Critical

Significant operating-unit impact

Criteria: Confirmed compromise of Tier-1 assets; disruption to a critical business service; suspected data exposure (limited scope); credible threat actor presence; possible regulator notification.

Response posture: Dedicated incident bridge. CISO or Deputy CISO is Incident Commander. Executive sponsor briefed. Insurance and IR retainer on standby.

Declare ≤ 1 hEscalate ≤ 2 hFirst exec brief ≤ 4 hTwice-daily updates

🟠 Sev 3 — High

Localised impact, contained

Criteria: Compromise contained to a defined scope (single user, single host, single application); no confirmed data exposure; service impact limited to non-critical systems or a small user group.

Response posture: SOC Manager is Incident Commander. CSIRT activated as needed. CISO informed.

Declare ≤ 2 hEscalate ≤ 4 hCISO brief same dayDaily update

🟡 Sev 4 — Medium

Routine, monitored

Criteria: Confirmed but contained event with no impact to data or service; isolated phishing-clicks without compromise; policy violations without material risk; suspicious activity warranting investigation.

Response posture: SOC Tier-2 lead handles. SOC Manager informed. No executive engagement unless escalated.

Declare ≤ 4 hSOC Manager next-business-dayClosure within 5 working days

🟢 Sev 5 — Low

Informational / nominal

Criteria: Confirmed nominal event; near-miss; informational alert validated as non-incident; minor policy reminder. Closed without further action beyond record-keeping.

Response posture: SOC Tier-1 closes. No escalation. Used for trend analysis.

Closure same shiftAggregated weekly

Operational classification. The detailed criteria for assigning a severity tier — institutional impact, legal, privacy, financial, reputational, data classification, data volume, and operational dimensions — are maintained in SOP-IM-01 (Incident Severity Classification & Escalation). SOP-IM-01 will be refreshed to align with the 5-tier model in this plan; until that refresh is complete, the IC applies the 5-tier model from this plan and records the rationale.

Section 6
Incident Lifecycle (7 Phases)

The lifecycle follows NIST SP 800-61 and ISO/IEC 27035. Each phase has defined entry and exit conditions, owners, actions, and evidence captured.

1
Prepare
Maintain this plan, derived runbooks, playbooks, SOPs, and the tabletop programme
Maintain the tooling stack: SIEM, EDR, NDR, ticketing, SOAR; verify telemetry coverage
Maintain external retainers and contracts: MDR, external IR, cyber insurance, specialist counsel
Maintain the asset inventory and Tier-0/1 register so blast-radius assessment is fast during a real event
Run scheduled reviews of contact lists in each operating-unit local annex (quarterly minimum)
Evidence captured: Plan version history, runbook/playbook inventories, retainer contracts, tabletop reports, training records.
2
Detect & Analyse
Receive an alert or report from any source: SIEM, EDR, MDR, employee, supplier, threat intel, external researcher
Triage to confirm the alert is a valid event of interest (not a false positive)
Correlate across data sources; gather indicators; determine attack vector, scope, affected assets, and potential blast radius
Apply severity criteria and propose a severity tier; the Incident Commander confirms or adjusts
Open the incident record in the system of record; assign Incident Commander, Lead Analyst, Recorder
For Sev 1/2: open the bridge or war room; notify the escalation list per Section 8
Evidence captured: Original alert, triage decision, severity rationale, indicator list, scope assessment, incident-record opening time, IC assignment.
3
Contain
Determine immediate containment posture: short-term tactical containment to halt active damage, while planning durable containment
Apply containment actions: network isolation, account disable, session revoke, port/protocol disable, sandbox suspect code, block IOCs
Preserve forensic evidence before taking destructive actions (memory captures, disk images, log exports) — see Section 10
Validate that containment is effective: confirm no continuing communication with the attacker infrastructure
Communicate containment status to the IC and to affected service owners
Evidence captured: Action log with timestamps and actors, forensic captures, validation evidence (e.g. "no further beaconing observed since T+25 minutes").
4
Eradicate
Identify and remediate all exploited vulnerabilities (patch, configuration change, control addition)
Remove malicious artefacts: malware, persistence mechanisms, attacker tooling, modified configurations, rogue accounts, malicious mail rules
Hunt across the wider estate for the same indicators — "have we missed anywhere else"
Where compromise of identity is suspected, rotate credentials, revoke sessions, and review privileged access at scale
Confirm with the Lead Analyst that the cause is removed, not just the symptom
Evidence captured: Vulnerability remediation evidence (patch IDs, configuration changes), eradication action log, indicator-hunt results, identity-rotation record.
5
Recover
Validate backup integrity before restoration; never restore from a backup taken inside the compromise window without forensic clearance
Restore systems in priority order driven by Business Impact Analysis: Tier-0 first, then Tier-1, etc.
Apply enhanced monitoring on restored systems for a defined "watchful-eye" period (default 14 days, longer for Sev 1)
Confirm services are functioning normally with the service owner; obtain explicit "back to normal" sign-off
Hand off from CSIRT to BAU operations only when the IC declares the response phase closed
Evidence captured: Restoration log, backup-integrity attestation, post-restore monitoring data, service-owner sign-offs.
6
Report
Produce the after-action report: timeline, scope, decisions, actions, what worked, what didn't
Conduct a structured post-incident review meeting; capture lessons learned, with named owners and target dates for each action
For Sev 1/2: brief Risk Committee and Board (where applicable) within 30 days of closure
Update the regulator-notification record (where applicable) with final figures and outcomes
Update GRC-REG-RISK if the incident has materialised an existing risk or revealed a new one
Evidence captured: After-action report (template in GRC-FRM-14), lessons-learned register, regulator-notification updates, risk-register cross-reference.
7
Remediate
Track lessons-learned actions to closure; do not allow them to age into perpetual backlog
Update controls, baselines, runbooks, playbooks, and SOPs based on the incident
Refresh threat intelligence: indicators, TTPs, detection logic
If the incident triggered a new risk, complete GRC-FRM-13 and add to GRC-REG-RISK
Confirm remediation effectiveness through follow-up testing (red team, purple team, tabletop)
Evidence captured: Lessons-learned closure tracker, control-change records, updated runbooks, follow-up test results.

Section 9
Decision Points

Six decisions recur in nearly every material incident. Each is captured here as a structured decision flow. The decision and its rationale are recorded in the incident journal at the time the decision is taken.

9.1 — Is this an incident?
Q1Has a security policy, acceptable-use policy, or standard practice been violated, or is violation imminent?
Q2Is there potential or actual adverse effect on the organisation's data, services, people, or reputation?
→Both Yes → declare incident, assign severity. One No → record as event of interest, close as nominal. Uncertain → declare cautiously and downgrade later.
9.2 — Is this a notifiable breach?
Q1Is personal data of identifiable individuals confirmed or reasonably believed to have been accessed, exfiltrated, altered, or destroyed?
Q2Is there a risk to the rights and freedoms of those individuals (financial loss, identity theft, discrimination, reputational damage)?
Q3Does sector-specific regulation (NIS2, DORA, HIPAA, PCI-DSS) impose a separate notification obligation?
→Any Yes → engage Privacy/DPO and Legal immediately; start the regulator clock per local annex; do not delay decision pending complete information.
9.3 — Should we engage cyber insurance?
Q1Is the incident Sev 1 or Sev 2, OR involves ransomware, OR involves suspected data exposure?
Q2Is there any material expense already incurred or expected (external IR, legal, restoration, ransom)?
→Either Yes → notify cyber insurer per local annex within their notification window (typically 24–72 h). Late notification can void coverage. Notify even if a claim is uncertain.
9.4 — Should we engage external IR?
Q1Does the incident exceed in-house forensic, malware-analysis, or surge-capacity capability?
Q2Is there a regulatory or insurer expectation that an independent third party investigates?
Q3Is the response expected to last more than 5 working days?
→Any Yes → engage retained IR partner. CISO authorises; CFO confirms emergency expenditure if outside retainer scope.
9.5 — Should we make a public statement?
Q1Is service disruption visible to customers, beneficiaries, or the public?
Q2Has the incident attracted, or is it likely to attract, media or social-media attention?
Q3Is there a regulatory or contractual obligation to communicate?
→Any Yes → PR drafts; Legal, Privacy, and CISO sign off; CEO approves for Sev 1. Use the holding-statement template (GRC-FRM-14) where facts are still being established.
9.6 — Ransom: pay or refuse?
Q1Is the threat actor or affiliated group on a sanctions list (OFAC, UK, EU, UN)? If yes, payment is illegal — do not pay.
Q2Has Legal cleared the legal posture in all relevant jurisdictions?
Q3Does the cyber insurer concur? (Many policies require concurrence.)
Q4Are restoration alternatives genuinely exhausted?
→Decision is reserved to the CEO with CISO, Legal, CFO, and Risk Committee Chair. Never the IC alone. Decision and rationale recorded in the incident journal.
Section 10
Evidence Handling & Chain of Custody

All information created, gathered, or generated during incident response is treated as evidence by default, classified Confidential — Level 3, and retained accordingly. Treatment is downgraded only after Legal confirms the matter is closed and no related legal process is anticipated.

10.1 Principles

  • Preserve before you act. Capture volatile evidence (memory, network state) before destructive containment; capture disk images before reimaging; export logs before retention windows lapse.
  • Maintain chain of custody. Every artefact has a recorded origin, custodian, and movement history. Use the system of record's evidence module or a controlled spreadsheet annex.
  • Protect legal privilege. Where Legal directs, channel investigative work product through counsel to maintain client–attorney privilege. Mark privileged material clearly.
  • Default to over-retention. Do not delete potentially relevant material until Legal confirms it is safe to do so.

10.3 Custody & ownership

The CISO is the custodian of incident records. After closure, the GRC team holds long-term custody and authorises access. Access is logged. Destruction follows the data-retention schedule and is recorded.

Section 11
Communications Principles

Communications are the highest-risk dimension of incident response. Mishandled comms can convert a contained technical incident into a reputational, regulatory, or legal crisis.

  • Single source of truth. The IC's incident journal is the authoritative timeline; all communications draw from it.
  • One internal voice, one external voice. Internal comms via HR; external comms via PR. No freelance updates.
  • No external comms without sign-off. CISO, Legal, and Privacy/DPO review every external communication before release. CEO approves for Sev 1.
  • Tell people what they need to act on, no more. Operational details that aid an attacker (specific indicators, internal architecture) are withheld.
  • Confirm before you commit. Avoid speculative claims about cause, scope, or attribution. Use holding statements where facts are still being established.
  • Don't surprise the regulator. Where regulator notification is foreseeable, brief them under the local notification framework before the public learns.

Concrete templates — internal staff notification, executive briefing, regulator notification, customer/beneficiary notification, public statement, after-action report — are maintained in GRC-FRM-14: Communication Templates Pack.

Section 12
Tabletop Exercise Programme

Tabletop exercises are mandatory: they validate this plan, build muscle memory, and surface gaps before a real incident does. The Head of Cyber Security Operations owns the programme; the GRC team observes and produces the report.

12.1 Cadence

  • One Sev 1 / Sev 2 scenario per year minimum, with the executive team present (CISO, CIO, CEO or designate, Legal, PR, CFO).
  • Two SOC-level scenarios per year minimum, exercising the Detect → Contain → Eradicate flow at the analyst level.
  • Annual local tabletop in each operating unit, exercising the local annex (regulator notification, language, contacts).
  • Post-material-incident review within 90 days of closure to validate that lessons-learned actions have actually changed behaviour.

12.2 Outputs

Each exercise produces a written report covering: scenario, participants, timeline, observations against this plan, gaps identified, and actions assigned with owners and target dates. Reports are retained for audit evidence and reviewed by the Risk Committee.

Section 13
"Do Not" Actions

The following are explicit prohibitions. Any deviation requires CISO and Legal sign-off recorded in the incident journal at the time the deviation is taken.

Do not wipe, reimage, or restore systems before forensic capture is complete or explicitly waived by the IC.
Do not delete logs, alerts, or communications related to the incident under any circumstance.
Do not engage law enforcement without prior Legal sign-off, especially in cross-border cases where notification can have unintended legal consequences.
Do not pay a ransom without the full chain of authorisation in §9.6. Unilateral payment by an IC is grounds for disciplinary action and may be illegal.
Do not communicate externally — to media, customers, beneficiaries, suppliers, or social media — without sign-off from CISO, Legal, and Privacy/DPO.
Do not negotiate with threat actors directly. Negotiation, where authorised, is conducted via specialist counsel or the IR retainer with explicit Legal and CISO oversight.
Do not restore from backups taken inside the compromise window without forensic clearance — you may reintroduce the attacker.
Do not close the incident until lessons-learned actions are recorded with named owners and the after-action report is signed off by the CISO.
Do not assume the incident is over when the obvious symptom stops. Continue monitoring through the watchful-eye period; threat actors often pause and resume.
