IT-ID-WI-02: Access Recertification & Attestation

Periodic review of who has what access. Cadence per access tier (Tier-0 quarterly, Tier-1 semi-annual, Tier-2/3 annual). Named reviewer per scope. Evidence retention. Automated where possible via Entra Identity Governance access reviews; manual workflow where not. Operationalises §4.2 of IT-ID-SOP-01.

Prerequisites

Before applying this WI:

  • IT-ID-WI-01 (JML) in place — recertification reviews existing identities; provisioning lifecycle must be reliable for recertification scope to be reliable
  • IT-ID-WI-03 (Role Design) at least partially established — recertification reviewers need to understand what they're attesting to; role names and scopes must be meaningful
  • Access tier classification documented per resource / system — T0 / T1 / T2 / T3 mapping consistent with the Tier model in core-concepts.html
  • Named reviewer per access scope identified: line managers (direct reports), system owners (system-specific roles), Privileged Access Authority (Tier-0/1 eligibility)
  • Entra Identity Governance licensing checked — access reviews available at Entra ID P2 / E5; manual workflow used for tenants without it
  • Evidence retention destination established — recertification evidence retained 3 years minimum, typically in GRC document repository or Entra's built-in retention
  • Communication plan for reviewers covering their accountability, the cadence, what happens on non-response
Recertification is a control, not a chore. The temptation to bulk-attest ("approve all") makes the campaign visible without being effective. The procedures below explicitly forbid rubber-stamping at Tier-0 and Tier-1, and treat non-response as a revocation default. Reviewers are accountable for the attestations they perform; this is what makes recertification an audit-defensible control rather than a paperwork exercise.
Step-by-step instructions

The steps below operationalise IT-ID-SOP-01 §4.2.

1
Define recertification cadence per access tier
Every reviewable access scope has a documented review frequency aligned to the risk of the access.

Cadence is risk-based. Higher-tier access reviewed more often.

Access scopeCadencePrimary reviewer
Tier-0 privileged eligibilityQuarterlyPrivileged Access Authority
Tier-1 privileged eligibilityQuarterlyPrivileged Access Authority
Tier-0 standing access (e.g. break-glass)QuarterlyPrivileged Access Authority + sponsor exec
Tier-1 resource access (non-privileged)Semi-annualSystem owner
Tier-2 / Tier-3 accessAnnualLine manager + system owner
Guest / external user accessQuarterlyNamed sponsor
Service account / service principal ownershipQuarterlyTeam manager (named owner)
Group membership (high-impact groups)QuarterlyGroup owner
Full-population staff access (catch-all)AnnualLine manager
  • Cadence is the minimum; higher-risk events trigger ad-hoc reviews (e.g. mass role-change, agency reorganisation, suspected compromise during incident response)
  • Cadence is per scope, not per identity — if a single user holds access in multiple scopes (e.g. Tier-1 privileged + Tier-3 application), each scope's review fires on its own cycle
  • "Annual" means within a 365-day rolling window; campaign typically scheduled at a fixed time (e.g. financial year close) for evidence consistency
Verify. Cadence document published; every reviewable scope has an entry; calendar / scheduler shows upcoming campaigns; previous campaigns' dates align to the cadence (no slippage).
NIST 800-53 AC-2(j) · ISO 27001 A.5.18 · SOC 2 CC6.2
2
Assign named reviewers per access scope
Every recertification item has a named individual accountable for the attestation.

"Anyone in IT can approve" is not a control. Each review item maps to a named role.

  • Direct-report access — line manager. Sourced from HR. Manager attests to the access of their direct reports.
  • System-specific access — system owner per the asset register (GRC-REG-ASSET). System owner attests to who has access to their system regardless of department.
  • Privileged role eligibility (Tier-0 / Tier-1) — Privileged Access Authority (typically CISO + Head of IT + nominee). Reviews eligibility list against business need; can suspend eligibility pending re-justification.
  • Guest / external access — named sponsor (per IT-ID-WI-01 Step 6). Sponsor attests to ongoing collaboration need.
  • Service accounts / service principals — named owner (per IT-ID-WI-04). Owner attests to ongoing need.
  • Group membership — group owner. Owner reviews members against group purpose.
  • Reviewer-of-reviewer for high-impact reviews — e.g. Tier-0 eligibility review by Privileged Access Authority is itself reviewed by the CISO or an external auditor annually to prevent reviewer collusion.

Reviewer-coverage validation

  • Every access scope has a reviewer attribute populated; "TBD" or empty triggers an alert
  • Reviewer departure (per IT-ID-WI-01 leaver process) triggers reviewer-reassignment for their scopes; not the IAE team picking up the slack
  • Quarterly reviewer-list audit: is the assigned reviewer still in their role? still the right person?
Verify. Sample 5 review items; each has a named reviewer who is currently in the organisation and currently in the role that justifies them being reviewer. No "TBD" or vacant reviewer assignments.
NIST 800-53 AC-2(7) · ISO 27001 A.5.18
3
Run recertification campaigns — automated where possible
Reviewers receive their items, act on them within the campaign window, and the system records their decisions.

Mechanics depend on tenant licensing:

  • Entra Identity Governance access reviews (E5 / Entra ID P2) — native platform support: campaigns are configured per scope, reviewers receive in-portal and email notifications, decisions captured automatically, automatic apply (revoke on non-response or expired-decision) configurable
  • Manual workflow (lower-tier tenants) — recertification platform (e.g. SharePoint list, dedicated GRC tool, ServiceNow workflow) generates per-reviewer lists from an extract of current access; reviewers attest via that tool; decisions exported back to identity platform for action
  • On-prem AD scopes — Entra Identity Governance can review AD groups synced via Entra Connect (group membership); for AD-only scopes (e.g. security groups not synced), manual workflow or PowerShell-driven extract

Campaign window

  • Quarterly campaigns: 14-day window for reviewers to act
  • Semi-annual / annual: 21-day window
  • Bulk reviews (annual full-population): 30-day window with weekly progress reporting
  • Reminders at day 3, day 7, day 10 of a 14-day window; escalation to reviewer's manager at day 12

Decision options

  • Certify — access remains; reviewer accepts continued legitimacy
  • Revoke — access removed; system applies the change automatically (or generates a removal ticket for manual scopes)
  • Modify — access scope adjusted (e.g. from "all of system X" to "subset Y"); generates a follow-up adjustment ticket
  • Justify-required — reviewer attests but with a justification comment; used for unusual access that needs explanation/li>
Verify. Most recent campaign completed within window. Decision distribution looks plausible (not 100% certify). Reviewer engagement metrics available (response time, non-response rate).
CIS Controls 5.6 · ISO 27001 A.5.18
4
Non-response handling — revoke as the default
Access that no one will attest to is access that doesn't get to continue.

The most common reviewer behaviour is silence. The campaign needs a default action.

  • Tier-0 / Tier-1 non-response — the access is suspended (not revoked) until reviewed. The user loses the access temporarily; the reviewer is notified that suspension has occurred; restoration requires explicit re-attestation. This balances rigour with continuity — a missed attestation by a busy reviewer doesn't lose the access permanently, but it stops the access until accountable review happens.
  • Tier-2 / Tier-3 non-response — the access is revoked as a default. Restoration requires fresh access request, not silent re-grant. The principle: if no one will speak for the access, it doesn't exist.
  • Service account / service principal non-response — the owner's manager is notified; if still unanswered after 30 days, the identity is flagged for decommissioning (IT-ID-WI-04 Step 8).
  • Reviewer escalation — non-response by a reviewer escalates to their line manager at day 12 of a 14-day window. If still unresponsive, escalates to the Head of IT.
  • Pattern: chronic non-responders — reviewers who repeatedly miss campaigns are tracked; their patterns are GRC findings. Either the reviewer is wrong (someone else should be doing this) or the reviewer is unaccountable (a people problem, not a process problem).
Suspension as a forcing function. When Tier-0 eligibility is suspended pending re-attestation, the reviewer notices because the user notices: "I can't activate my role." This is precisely the right pressure. Make the suspension visible, not silent — the goal is the review happens, not punishment.
Verify. Non-response rate trending downward over the last 4 campaigns. Tier-0/1 suspensions, when they occur, are followed by re-attestation within 7 days on average. Chronic-non-responder report available; remediation in progress.
NIST 800-53 AC-2(j) · ISO 27001 A.5.18
5
Forbid bulk-attest at high tiers
Reviewers engage with each high-tier item individually; sampling acceptable only at lower tiers with documented sample size.

"Approve all" is the recertification anti-pattern. It satisfies the auditor's checkbox without performing the control.

  • Tier-0 / Tier-1 access — per-item attestation required. The tooling forbids bulk-approve actions at these tiers. Each item gets a yes/no/modify decision individually.
  • Tier-2 / Tier-3 access — per-item attestation preferred; sampling-based attestation accepted with documented sample size (typically 10% with a minimum of 30 items) and the sample selection method recorded. Sample findings inform the certification of the remainder.
  • Justification capture — for items kept (certified), an optional justification comment; for items revoked, no comment required; for items modified, the modification description is mandatory.
  • Decision time — expected to be longer at higher tiers (reviewers think before deciding); campaigns where Tier-0 decisions complete in under 30 seconds per item are flagged for review-quality concerns.
  • "Why does this user have this access?" prompt in the tooling — gentle nudge that surfaces context (when granted, by whom, last used) so the decision is informed rather than reflexive.
Verify. Tier-0 campaign decision times distribution (median, p95) within reasonable ranges. No bulk-approve events on Tier-0/1 items. Tier-2/3 sampling documented per campaign with sample size and selection method.
SOC 2 CC6.2 · GRC governance
6
Apply decisions — revocation is real, not symbolic
Revoke decisions remove the access from the identity platform; modify decisions adjust scope; the loop is closed.

Decisions captured but not applied are worthless. The action loop must close.

  • Automated apply — Entra Identity Governance reviews can be configured to auto-apply decisions on close of the review. Strongly preferred.
  • Manual apply — for scopes outside Entra IG or where auto-apply is disabled (e.g. high-risk scopes where IAE prefers manual application), decisions feed a ticket queue with apply SLA ≤ 7 days post-campaign close.
  • Apply audit trail — every revocation has: who decided, when decided, when applied, by whom (or "auto" for system-applied). Retained 3 years minimum.
  • Failed apply — technical failure to apply (e.g. group locked, downstream system unavailable) raises an alert and a remediation ticket; not silently ignored.
  • Re-grant prohibition — access revoked through recertification cannot be silently re-granted by the same line manager / system owner in the next month. Re-grant within 90 days requires explicit justification and Privileged Access Authority awareness (for privileged tiers).
Verify. Most recent campaign: apply SLA met (≤7 days for manual, immediate for auto-apply). Audit trail samples show full decision-to-apply traceability. No re-grant-within-90-days events outside documented exceptions.
NIST 800-53 AC-2(j) · ISO 27001 A.5.18
7
Evidence retention & audit support
Recertification produces audit-grade evidence: who reviewed, what they decided, when, with what justification, applied to which access.
  • Per-item evidence record: item ID (which access scope), identity reviewed, reviewer name, decision, justification (if any), decision timestamp, apply timestamp, apply method (auto / manual ticket ref)
  • Retention 3 years minimum; longer where regulatory requirement applies (financial services, healthcare, government)
  • Storage — Entra IG retains within the platform; manual workflows export to GRC document repository; both immutable for the retention period (cannot be back-dated or modified after capture)
  • Audit pack assembled annually for external audit: campaign schedule, completion rates, decision distribution, non-response handling, sample evidence records, exception register entries, reviewer-coverage validation
  • Tier-0 evidence reviewed annually by GRC as part of standing-privilege governance — not just on audit request
Verify. Sample 3 historical attestation records (from different campaigns); each contains all required fields and is unmodified since capture. Audit pack ready or assemblable on demand.
SOC 2 CC6.2 · ISO 27001 A.5.36 · regulatory retention
8
Orphaned-account remediation
Accounts without a legitimate current owner found during recertification are remediated, not ignored.

Recertification surfaces orphans — accounts whose listed owner is no longer with the organisation, whose system owner is unknown, whose business justification has lapsed.

  • Identify — recertification campaign captures items where the reviewer is unknown / departed / explicitly says "this isn't mine"; these become orphan candidates
  • Investigate — IAE traces the access: when was it granted, who requested, what is the access for, is it being used (last sign-in / activity), is there a legitimate current owner
  • Resolve within 30 days — either: assign a new named owner (with the owner's consent and attestation), revoke the access, or document an exception with named approver if neither is immediately possible
  • Track — orphan count is a standing metric (per IT-ID-SOP-01 §7.3); trending matters more than absolute count
  • Coordinate with IT-ID-WI-04 — orphan service accounts and service principals feed the non-human identity remediation queue
Verify. Orphan list maintained; new orphans from last campaign in active remediation; aged orphans (>30 days) escalated or exception-tracked.
CIS Controls 5.5 · ISO 27001 A.5.16
Validation & verification

Validate the recertification programme is operating as designed.

Campaign-cycle validation

  • All scheduled campaigns ran on time in the last 12 months (no skipped cycles)
  • Reviewer coverage at 100% (every item has a named, current reviewer)
  • Completion rates: Tier-0/1 at 100%; Tier-2/3 ≥ 95%
  • Non-response handling: suspensions applied where appropriate; non-responders escalated to managers
  • Decision distribution is plausible (not 100% certify); revocation rate trending is reasonable
  • Apply SLA met (≤7 days for manual, immediate for auto-apply)
  • No bulk-approve events at Tier-0/1 in last 4 campaigns
  • Evidence records complete, retained, immutable
  • Orphan remediation in progress; aged orphan count trending down
  • Annual full-population campaign completed within calendar window

Rollback & troubleshooting

Recertification decisions can be reversed but the audit trail of the original decision and the reversal must both be preserved. Don't quietly re-grant; document the reversal as a separate event.

Common scenarios

  • Mass revocation by mistaken reviewer — cause: reviewer clicked "revoke all" instead of "approve all" (the opposite anti-pattern of bulk-attest). Recovery: identify the affected items; re-grant with documented justification; investigate the tooling UX that allowed the mistake (consider confirmation step for bulk-revoke).
  • Auto-apply suspended someone who should retain access — cause: reviewer didn't engage with Tier-0 item, auto-suspension applied. Recovery: reviewer re-attests; suspension lifts; address why reviewer didn't engage (workload, missing notification, away from work). Not a "system failure" — the control worked as designed.
  • Apply ticket sat in queue past SLA — cause: manual workflow has bottleneck at apply step. Recovery: process the backlog; investigate root cause (capacity, prioritisation, integration); consider promoting to auto-apply for the scope.
  • Reviewer departed mid-campaign — cause: per IT-ID-WI-01 leaver process. Recovery: reassign their pending items to their successor or line manager; reset the window for the reassigned items.
  • Campaign ran on outdated reviewer list — cause: reviewer-list extract was stale at campaign generation time. Recovery: regenerate with current list; re-issue items where the previous reviewer is no longer correct; reviewer-list audit cadence reviewed.
  • Revocation broke a critical operation — cause: legitimate access was reviewed-as-no-longer-needed by a reviewer who didn't have full context (e.g. system owner reviewed but didn't know the user was also serving in a project). Recovery: re-grant with documented justification; consider improving the reviewer's context view in tooling.

Rollback procedure

  1. Identify the breaking decision (which item, which reviewer, which decision)
  2. Re-grant the access through the standard access request workflow with documented justification — do not silently undo
  3. Both the original revoke decision and the re-grant decision retained in audit trail
  4. For systemic issues (tooling, process, reviewer-list quality), raise as a programme improvement
  5. Communicate with affected user and reviewer to close the loop
Common errors & tips
  • Recertification without action is theatre. The campaign produces decisions; the apply step produces control. If decisions don't translate to actual access changes, the campaign is paperwork. Auto-apply where possible; SLA the manual queue otherwise.
  • "Approve all" is the anti-pattern. If your tooling allows bulk-approve at Tier-0, disable that capability. Per-item engagement is non-negotiable at high tiers.
  • Revocation rate matters as a signal. Always-approve (revocation near zero) suggests rubber-stamping; always-revoke suggests over-provisioning. The healthy range is somewhere in between, and the trend matters more than the absolute number.
  • Non-response defaults are how you avoid being held hostage by busy reviewers. If non-response means "access continues," the entire control collapses for reviewers who don't engage. Tier-0/1 suspension and Tier-2/3 revocation as defaults force engagement.
  • Reviewer-list quality is more important than reviewer effort. A diligent reviewer reviewing the wrong items is no control. Maintain the reviewer-to-scope mapping as carefully as you maintain the access itself.
  • The annual full-population campaign catches everything else. Per-scope campaigns can miss items that fell through scope categorisation. Annual full-pop is the safety net.
  • Justify-certified at Tier-0 is more informative than approve. When a Tier-0 reviewer attests with a justification, the comment is gold for the next reviewer (and for the auditor). Encourage it; don't make it mandatory (or it becomes filler text).
  • Don't re-grant the day after revocation. If the same access is granted within days of being revoked through recertification, the recertification didn't work. Either the revoke was wrong (fix the campaign) or the re-grant is suspicious (investigate). 90-day moratorium with exception process catches this.
  • Sampling at Tier-2/3 is fine; sampling at Tier-0/1 isn't. Tier-2/3 sampling is a pragmatic acknowledgment of scale; Tier-0/1 sampling would mean we're guessing about privileged access.
  • Evidence-once, not evidence-recreated-at-audit. Capture evidence at decision time, immutably. Recreating "who decided what when" months later from logs is fragile and audit-uncomfortable.
  • Decision-time medians flag rubber-stamping more reliably than completion rate. A 100%-complete campaign where every Tier-0 decision was made in 5 seconds is a worse signal than a 95%-complete campaign with thoughtful decisions.
  • Reviewer fatigue is real. Reviewers with too many items engage less per item. Consider scope granularity: a campaign with 500 items per reviewer will perform worse than 5 campaigns of 100 items each over the same period.
