IT-TI-WI-04: Threat Actor & Campaign Profiling

Step-by-step instructions for building and maintaining threat actor profiles and campaign records relevant to the organisation — providing SOC analysts with the context needed to triage faster and hunt more effectively.

Prerequisites
Access to MITRE ATT&CK Navigator or equivalent Threat intel platform or structured notes repository Understanding of organisation's sector and key technology assets
Step-by-step instructions
1
Identify the threat actor or campaign — from an incident, TI feed, vendor advisory, or ISAC report
2
Research the actor using MITRE ATT&CK, open-source reporting, and commercial TI — document name, aliases, suspected origin, and primary motivation (financial, espionage, hacktivism, ransomware)
3
Document known TTPs using MITRE ATT&CK technique IDs — initial access methods, persistence mechanisms, lateral movement techniques, and exfiltration approaches
4
Map known targeting — which sectors, geographies, and organisation types does this actor target? Are they known to target your sector or technology stack?
5
Document known tooling — malware families, offensive tools, and infrastructure patterns (IP ranges, domain registration patterns, TLS certificate characteristics)
6
Assess relevance to your organisation — is this actor likely to target you? Score: High / Medium / Low relevance
7
Extract actionable IOCs from the profile and process through IT-TI-WI-02 for validation and deployment
8
Identify detection gaps — are there TTPs in this actor's profile that your current detections would not catch? Raise as detection improvement tasks
9
Store the profile in a structured, searchable format. Link to related IOCs, incidents, and detection rules
10
Review and update the profile when new reporting is published — mark last reviewed date
Validation & verification
Profile covers motivation, TTPs, tooling, targeting, and relevance assessment
MITRE ATT&CK techniques documented with technique IDs
IOCs extracted and submitted to IT-TI-WI-02 for processing
Detection gaps identified and raised as tasks
Profile stored and linked to related IOCs
Common errors & tips
Start with actors known to target your sector — MITRE ATT&CK Groups and CISA advisories are excellent starting points
MITRE ATT&CK Navigator is free and lets you visualise an actor's TTP coverage against your detection capabilities
Threat actor names vary across vendors — Fancy Bear, APT28, Sofacy, Strontium are all the same group. Document aliases to avoid confusion
A profile that says 'this actor is not relevant to us' is still valuable — it prevents wasted effort during incidents
NIST CSF 2.0 alignment
ID.RA — Risk Assessment: threat actor context DE.AE — Adverse Event Analysis: TTP-based detection
