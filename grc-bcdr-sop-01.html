GRC-BCDR-SOP-01 — BCDR Methodology & Governance

Shared methodology underpinning Business Continuity (GRC-BCP) and IT Disaster Recovery (GRC-DR). Defines the common language, business impact assessment approach, RTO/RPO classification, exercise framework, governance committee, and review cadence used by both domains. Authoritative source for shared terminology and methodology decisions.

Shared methodology BIA · RTO · RPO Exercise framework ISO 22301-aligned
1 — Purpose & scope

This SOP provides the methodological foundation for business continuity and IT disaster recovery across the Institution. It exists because both disciplines depend on common definitions (what is “critical”, what RTO means, what counts as an exercise) and a common governance forum, and authoring these in two policy documents would create inevitable drift between them.

This SOP is therefore consulted by:

  • The Business Continuity Policy (GRC-BCP-SOP-01) for terminology, BIA approach, exercise standards, and governance.
  • The IT Disaster Recovery Policy (GRC-DR-SOP-01) for the same terminology, RTO/RPO classification, and the exercise standards.
  • The BIA work instruction (GRC-BCDR-WI-01) which operationalises the methodology defined here.
  • The Exercise work instruction (GRC-BCDR-WI-02) which operationalises the exercise framework defined here.

It is not a policy document. It does not define obligations on agencies or IT teams — those obligations live in the two policy SOPs. It defines the framework that those policies and the work instructions use.

2 — Terminology & definitions

The following definitions apply throughout the BCDR domain. Where a term is used elsewhere in the KB with a different meaning, the BCDR-specific meaning applies within BCDR documents.

TermDefinition
Business processA discrete activity that delivers value to a beneficiary, stakeholder, or internal customer. Examples: clinical consultation, student enrolment, payroll, donor reporting. The unit of analysis for business continuity.
IT serviceA technology capability supporting one or more business processes. Examples: identity service, email service, file service, electronic health record. The unit of analysis for IT disaster recovery.
RTO (Recovery Time Objective)The maximum acceptable time between disruption and restoration of a process or service. Set by BIA at business-process level; flows down to dependent IT services.
RPO (Recovery Point Objective)The maximum acceptable data loss measured backwards from the moment of disruption. Set by BIA; expressed as “no more than N hours / minutes of data loss”.
MTPD (Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption)The point beyond which disruption causes unrecoverable damage (regulatory, reputational, financial, beneficiary harm). RTO must be less than MTPD with margin.
MBCO (Minimum Business Continuity Objective)The minimum acceptable level of service during disruption. May be a percentage of normal capacity, a reduced feature set, or a manual workaround. Set per business process by BIA.
BIA (Business Impact Assessment)The structured analysis that identifies critical processes, dependencies, RTO, RPO, MTPD, MBCO, and disruption impact. See GRC-BCDR-WI-01.
Recovery tierIT-side classification (Tier 1–4) of an IT service’s recovery requirements derived from the RTO/RPO of dependent business processes. See GRC-DR-WI-01.
Business Process Continuity PlanThe document describing how a critical business process continues to operate (workarounds, alternative sites, alternative tools, communications) during a disruption. Owned by the Business Process Owner.
DR RunbookThe document describing how an IT service is recovered (steps, sequence, dependencies, validation, rollback). Owned by the IT Service Owner.
Crisis Management TeamThe named group activated during a disruption to coordinate the response. Per-agency, per-site, or per-event depending on scale.
ExerciseAny planned activity that tests a continuity or recovery plan. Includes tabletop, walkthrough, simulation, and live failover. See GRC-BCDR-WI-02.
InvocationThe formal decision to activate a continuity or recovery plan in response to an actual disruption. Distinct from exercise activation.
3 — BIA approach

Business impact assessment is the analytical foundation for both BCP and DR. The same BIA produces:

  • The list of critical business processes for an agency, ranked by impact;
  • The RTO and RPO for each critical process;
  • The dependencies of each process — IT services, suppliers, sites, people, data;
  • The MTPD and MBCO for each critical process.

Operational procedure is in GRC-BCDR-WI-01. The methodology principles are:

  • Process-first, not system-first. The BIA starts with what the agency does (delivers patient care, teaches students, conducts research), not with the systems used to do it. Systems are inferred as dependencies.
  • Impact across multiple dimensions. Each process is assessed for impact along: beneficiary safety, regulatory / legal, financial, reputational, operational. The highest impact dimension drives criticality.
  • Time-graduated impact. Impact is assessed at multiple time horizons (e.g. 1h, 4h, 24h, 72h, 1w) because some processes can tolerate short outages but degrade severely over time.
  • Beneficiary safety is the trump card. Where beneficiary safety impact is assessed as high or critical, the process is classed Tier-1 critical regardless of other dimensions.
  • Dependencies are the bridge to DR. The BIA’s dependency map is the input to IT recovery tier assignment.
  • Refresh on material change, not just on cadence. A BIA refresh is required when (a) the agency’s service portfolio materially changes, (b) a new critical IT system is introduced, (c) a regulatory change alters obligations, or (d) the standing cadence elapses (every 24 months at minimum).
4 — RTO / RPO classification

RTO and RPO emerge from the BIA. They are expressed at business-process level and flow down to IT services as IT recovery tier assignments. The Institution uses the following standard bands; agencies may set tighter RTOs for specific processes but not looser.

Process criticalityRTO (target)RPO (target)MTPD (typical)IT tier mapping
Mission-critical (beneficiary safety impact within hours)≤ 4 hours≤ 15 minutes24 hoursTier 1
Business-critical (regulatory / financial / reputational impact within a day)≤ 24 hours≤ 4 hours7 daysTier 2
Important (operational impact within days)≤ 72 hours≤ 24 hours14 daysTier 3
Standard (deferred or absorbable)≤ 7 days≤ 7 days30 daysTier 4

The IT tier mapping is the principal bridge between BCP and DR. Where a single IT service supports business processes at different criticality levels, the IT service inherits the highest tier among its dependents. Detailed tier criteria and IT minima per tier are in GRC-DR-WI-01.

5 — Exercise framework

Continuity and recovery plans are only as good as the last exercise. The exercise framework defines what counts as an exercise, what each type tests, and the minimum cadence.

Exercise typeWhat it testsEffortMinimum cadence
TabletopDecision-making, role clarity, communication paths. No real systems touched.Low (2–4 hours)All Tier-1 processes / services: annually. Tier-2: every 24 months.
WalkthroughProcedure correctness. Plan is followed step-by-step in a non-production setting; deviations are noted.Medium (half-day)All Tier-1 services: every 24 months.
SimulationProcedure execution in a simulated environment. Recovery is performed but on a copy or sandbox.High (1–3 days)Tier-1 critical services: every 24 months minimum; preferred over walkthrough where feasible.
Live failoverActual failover and failback in production. Tests the full path including human, technical, and supplier dependencies.Very high (multi-day; advance scheduling and approval)At least one Tier-1 service per agency per 24 months; rotate the service tested.

Operational procedure for each exercise type is in GRC-BCDR-WI-02. Methodology principles:

  • Higher exercise types do not replace lower ones. A live failover does not substitute for a tabletop — they test different things (decision-making vs technical execution).
  • Scenarios must vary. The same scenario every year tests memorisation. The scenario library in GRC-BCDR-WI-02 provides rotation.
  • Findings are mandatory output. Every exercise produces a findings document with assigned owners and target remediation dates. Findings are tracked to closure; overdue findings escalate to the Steering Committee.
  • Real disruption counts as exercise. An actual invocation that exercises the plan in earnest produces the same evidence as a planned exercise and counts against the cadence, provided a structured after-action review is completed.
  • Cyber incidents that invoke continuity also count. A ransomware response that activates the continuity plan counts as a live exercise for the processes invoked, with after-action review feeding both the BCP/DR programme and the SOC’s lessons-learned.
6 — BCDR governance committee

The BCDR Steering Committee is the cross-agency governance body for the BCDR programme. It is shared between BCP and DR; the same body governs both.

Composition.

  • Chair: senior accountable executive (rotating; typically a Group-level COO or equivalent).
  • Vice-chair: CIO or equivalent.
  • Members: each agency’s BCP Coordinator; each agency’s IT DR Coordinator; CISO or delegate; Head of Internal Audit (observer); General Counsel (observer); Head of Communications (observer).
  • Secretary: GRC team member responsible for the BCDR portfolio.

Cadence. Quarterly standing meeting. Out-of-cycle convene on (a) major disruption requiring cross-agency coordination, (b) material methodology change, (c) exercise outcome requiring escalation.

Standing agenda.

  • Portfolio status: plan coverage, plan currency, exercise compliance by agency and tier.
  • Exercise outcomes since last meeting: lessons learned, finding closure rates, escalations.
  • Invocations since last meeting (actual disruptions): after-action review summaries.
  • Risk register entries related to BCDR: status, treatment progress.
  • Methodology change requests: review, approve, schedule rollout.
  • Forward exercise schedule for next quarter.

Decisions reserved to the Committee.

  • Methodology changes (this SOP, terminology, RTO/RPO bands, exercise standards).
  • Approval of new agency BCP / DR programmes joining the portfolio.
  • Acceptance of material gaps in plan coverage (which become risk register entries).
  • Escalations from agencies that cannot be resolved locally.
