NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0

The National Institute of Standards and Technology Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 is the globally adopted voluntary framework used by this knowledge base as its primary cyber security reference structure. Published in February 2024, it organises cybersecurity outcomes across six core functions applicable to organisations of any size, sector, or geography.

The NIST CSF is not a compliance checklist — it is a risk-based outcomes framework. It describes what an organisation should achieve, not how to achieve it. This makes it technology-neutral, vendor-agnostic, and applicable whether you are a global enterprise, a regional NGO, or a small business with limited resources.

The framework consists of three components that work together: the CSF Core (the hierarchy of functions, categories, and subcategories describing desired outcomes), Organisational Profiles (mechanisms to describe current and target security posture), and Implementation Tiers (a scale characterising the rigour of an organisation's risk governance practices).

All SOPs and relevant playbooks in this knowledge base include explicit NIST CSF 2.0 function and category alignment references, enabling teams to trace every operational procedure back to the framework outcomes it supports.

Version history

CSF 1.0 — 2014

Original release. Five functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. Focused on critical infrastructure. 23 categories, 108 subcategories.

CSF 1.1 — 2018

Incremental update. Added supply chain risk management and identity management guidance. Expanded authentication and vulnerability disclosure guidance.

CSF 2.0 — February 2024

Major update. Added sixth function: Govern. Expanded scope to all organisations, not just critical infrastructure. Strengthened supply chain risk management. 22 categories, 106 subcategories.

Framework components

🏗️ CSF Core

The central hierarchy of Functions, Categories, and Subcategories. Describes desired cybersecurity outcomes at three levels of detail — from broad strategic objectives down to specific operational outcomes. Technology-neutral and sector-agnostic.

📊 Organisational Profiles

A mechanism for describing an organisation's current cybersecurity posture and its target posture. The gap between the two becomes the improvement roadmap. Profiles are customised to each organisation's context, risk appetite, and resources.

📈 Implementation Tiers

A four-level scale (Partial → Risk Informed → Repeatable → Adaptive) that characterises the rigour of an organisation's cybersecurity risk governance and management practices. Tiers are not maturity levels — they help contextualise priorities.

The six core functions
GV Govern New in v2.0
Establish, communicate, and monitor the organisation's cybersecurity risk management strategy, expectations, and policy. Govern is the central function — it informs and shapes all other functions. It encompasses roughly 30% of all subcategories, reflecting NIST's strong emphasis on governance as a security control.
GV.OC — Organisational Context GV.RM — Risk Management Strategy GV.RR — Roles, Responsibilities & Authorities GV.PO — Policy GV.OV — Oversight GV.SC — Supply Chain Risk Management
KB alignment: SOPs (SOP-IM-01, SOP-EP-01, SOP-ID-01, SOP-EM-01, SOP-NW-01) all support GV.RM and GV.RR by defining roles, responsibilities, escalation authorities, and risk-based decision frameworks.
ID Identify
Understand the organisation's current cybersecurity risks to systems, people, assets, data, and capabilities. The Identify function provides the foundation for prioritising cybersecurity efforts in alignment with the organisation's risk management strategy and mission needs.
ID.AM — Asset Management ID.RA — Risk Assessment ID.IM — Improvement
KB alignment: Tier-1 Checklists (ID-01 through ID-05, EM-01 through EM-05) support ID.RA by providing structured risk assessment workflows at the alert triage level.
PR Protect
Use safeguards to manage cybersecurity risks and support the ability to deliver critical services. The Protect function covers the controls and measures that limit the impact of a potential cybersecurity event — prevention, access control, data security, and resilience.
PR.AA — Identity Management, Authentication & Access Control PR.AT — Awareness & Training PR.DS — Data Security PR.PS — Platform Security PR.IR — Technology Infrastructure Resilience
KB alignment: Endpoint hardening WIs (IT-EP-WI-Win-01 through Win-05, PAW-01 through PAW-07) directly support PR.AA, PR.DS, and PR.PS. Identity SOPs support PR.AA.
DE Detect
Find and analyse possible cybersecurity attacks and compromises. The Detect function enables timely discovery of anomalies, indicators of compromise, and adverse events — supporting rapid response before damage escalates.
DE.CM — Continuous Monitoring DE.AE — Adverse Event Analysis
KB alignment: All Runbooks and investigation-only Work Instructions (EM-WI-01, ID-WI-01, ID-WI-04, NW-WI-01) directly support DE.CM and DE.AE. All SOPs reference these categories explicitly.
RS Respond
Take action regarding a detected cybersecurity incident. The Respond function covers incident management, analysis, mitigation, and communication — ensuring that incidents are contained and managed effectively to limit their impact.
RS.MA — Incident Management RS.AN — Incident Analysis RS.CO — Incident Response Reporting & Communication RS.MI — Incident Mitigation
KB alignment: All Playbooks (EM-PB, ID-PB, NW-PB, EP-PB series) and containment Work Instructions (EP-WI-02, EP-WI-03, EM-WI-04, EM-WI-05, NW-WI-02, NW-WI-03, ID-WI-02, ID-WI-05) directly support RS.MA, RS.AN, and RS.MI.
RC Recover
Restore assets and operations that were impacted by a cybersecurity incident. The Recover function supports timely restoration of normal operations, reducing the long-term impact of incidents and incorporating lessons learned into future improvements.
RC.RP — Incident Recovery Plan Execution RC.CO — Incident Recovery Communication
KB alignment: EP-WI-04 (Validate Persistence Removal & Recovery Readiness) and the recovery phases of all EP and EM Playbooks support RC.RP. Post-incident lessons-learned requirements across all SOPs support RC.CO.
Implementation tiers
1
Partial
Cybersecurity risk management is ad hoc. Limited awareness of risk. No formal policies or processes in place.
2
Risk Informed
Risk management practices exist but may not be organisation-wide. Awareness of cyber risk exists at leadership level but is not consistently applied.
3
Repeatable
Formally approved risk management practices that are expressed as policy. Organisation-wide awareness and consistent application across teams.
4
Adaptive
Cybersecurity risk management is continuously improved based on lessons learned, threat intelligence, and evolving organisational needs.
Organisational profiles

📍 Current Profile

Documents the cybersecurity outcomes an organisation is achieving today. An honest assessment of existing policies, processes, and controls mapped to CSF subcategories. Requires input from IT, security, legal, compliance, and leadership.

🎯 Target Profile

Describes the desired cybersecurity outcomes reflecting the organisation's risk management goals, regulatory obligations, and available resources. The gap between Current and Target becomes the improvement roadmap and budget justification.

Key changes in CSF 2.0 vs 1.1

➕ New Govern function

The biggest change. Govern was added as the sixth function, pulling governance, risk management strategy, roles, and supply chain risk into a dedicated function. It now comprises ~30% of all subcategories.

🌍 Expanded scope

CSF 1.x was originally designed for US critical infrastructure. CSF 2.0 explicitly applies to all organisations — any size, any sector, any country. NGOs, SMBs, and not-for-profits are explicitly included audiences.

🔗 Supply chain risk

Supply chain risk management (GV.SC) is the most detailed category in the framework with 10 subcategories. CSF 2.0 treats third-party and supply chain risk as a primary governance concern, not an afterthought.

📋 Implementation examples

CSF 2.0 introduces Implementation Examples — concise, action-oriented steps for achieving each subcategory's outcome. These make the framework significantly more actionable for smaller organisations.
