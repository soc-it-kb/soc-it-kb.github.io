GRC-TTX-02 — Entra Tenant Compromise

Tabletop scenario: SOC detects evidence of attacker presence in the Entra tenant including manipulation of privileged role assignments and an unrecognised app registration with broad Graph permissions. Tests the multi-week recovery decision space described in GRC-DR-WI-02 Scenario 4. Technical lean — SOC, IT, identity teams primary; CMT and executive in support roles.

Primary documents tested
DR-WI-02 (Scenarios 3 & 4), IT-CL-WI-01, IT-ID-WI-05, IRP

Tenant compromise is the worst-case identity-side scenario for any cloud-resident estate. Recovery is on the order of weeks to months for in-tenant clean-up, longer for clean-tenant migration. This scenario stresses the early decisions that shape the entire recovery: when do you call clean-tenant? Who decides? How is irreversibility communicated? What evidence preservation matters? Done well, the scenario surfaces gaps in identity governance, break-glass readiness, and CMT decision-making.

Objectives
  • Test the SOC’s tenant-compromise triage path — from detection through scope-establishment to recovery-threshold handoff
  • Test the in-tenant clean-up vs clean-tenant decision criteria from GRC-DR-WI-02 Scenario 4.2 — can decision-makers articulate them, and which way does this scenario point?
  • Test break-glass account readiness and use under exercise conditions per IT-CL-WI-01 and IT-ID-WI-05: do participants know who can access them, where they are, and what governance applies?
  • Test the joint SOC + DR + Identity working model — who owns containment, who owns recovery-to-operations, how the handoff works
  • Test escalation to Microsoft Support including the severity-A path and what tenant-recovery means if break-glass also fails

Ground rules
  • No-fault learning environment. Findings are about system, process, governance.
  • Technical participants may reference real platforms (Entra portal, SIEM, runbook documents) where useful. Inability to access something during the exercise is itself a finding.
  • Hypothetical actions are spoken (“I would revoke all refresh tokens”) not executed. The facilitator confirms outcomes and proceeds.
  • Break-glass details are not stated aloud (account names, vault locations, custodians may be referenced abstractly). Real break-glass governance information is not exercised through speech.
  • Scenario content and discussion are confidential to the exercise and the AAR distribution list.
Starting conditions (T+0)
Day & time
Friday, 16:45 local time
Setting
Approaching end of business week
Initial impact
Tenant integrity in question

Initial situation: At 16:30 local time, an SOC analyst on routine hunt observed an anomaly: a Global Administrator role assignment was made to an account two days ago that is not in the documented Global Admin inventory. Cross-checking, the account was created 11 days ago. The analyst escalated to the SOC Lead. Initial investigation found a parallel finding: an app registration was added 9 days ago with Microsoft Graph permissions including Directory.ReadWrite.All and RoleManagement.ReadWrite.Directory. The app registration is not in the documented application inventory. The SOC has not yet taken action. It is Friday late afternoon.

What participants know at T+0:

  • One unrecognised Global Admin account created 11 days ago, granted GA 2 days ago
  • One unrecognised app registration with broad Graph permissions, created 9 days ago
  • SOC analyst escalated to SOC Lead; no containment action yet
  • It is Friday 16:30; normal close-of-business in ~90 minutes
  • Tenant remains operational; no obvious user-visible impact

What participants do not yet know (held by exercise team):

  • The attacker observed the tenant for at least 11 days; established additional persistence beyond the two findings
  • One legitimate Global Admin’s session was hijacked early in the campaign
  • The attacker exported a subset of mail and SharePoint content
Injects
T+15 min Initial containment decisions

The SOC Lead has a containment menu: disable the unrecognised GA account; revoke the app registration; revoke refresh tokens for the tenant; force MFA re-registration for privileged users; isolate suspect workstations. The SOC analyst asks which to do, in what order, and how aggressively. The Cloud Platform Lead asks whether revoking refresh tokens will cause an outage to legitimate users at end of day on a Friday.

Discussion prompts
  • What is the containment sequence per IRP? Where is it documented? What is the authority for each action?
  • The end-of-week timing — does it accelerate or delay containment? Why?
  • Is there a difference between “contain” (preserve evidence, stop the bleeding) and “eradicate” (remove the attacker)? When does the scenario shift from one to the other?
  • If the attacker is watching their persistence go away, what do they do next? Does the response plan for that?
T+25 min SOC Lead: this needs CMT and DR engagement now

The SOC Lead concludes: this is tenant-compromise territory, not an opportunistic find. CMT should be convened; the IT DR Coordinator should be on the call; the BCDR Steering Committee should be made aware. The Identity & Access Engineering Lead joins the call. The CMT Lead asks: what is the scope of impact and what is the recovery path?

Discussion prompts
  • Who convenes CMT for cyber events at this severity? Per which document?
  • Is the BCDR Steering Committee’s standing-up the same as for a BCDR event? Or different for cyber? Where is that defined?
  • The SOC Lead’s answer to “what is the scope” is “we don’t know yet”. How is that communicated, and what decisions can the CMT make under that uncertainty?
T+40 min Hunt finds further persistence; legitimate GA session hijack confirmed

SOC hunting widens the picture. Forensic evidence of a hijacked session belonging to a legitimate Global Admin two weeks ago. Two further app registrations with narrower but still concerning permissions. Anomalous Graph API activity from the malicious app: mail and SharePoint content was exported. The Identity Engineering Lead asks: do we now treat every privileged account as potentially compromised? What about the directory’s integrity — are role assignments themselves trusted?

Discussion prompts
  • What does “directory integrity” mean operationally? How is it verified? Is the answer in any current runbook?
  • Mail and SharePoint exfiltration triggers data-breach assessment — who runs it? Within what timeline (regulator clock)?
  • Should break-glass accounts be activated now, before the attacker realises they’re being chased and takes destructive action? What does DR-WI-02 Scenario 3 say?
  • The hijacked legitimate GA — their device, credential, MFA — what is the response?
T+60 min Clean-up vs clean-tenant: the decision space

The CMT must brief the BCDR Steering Committee on the recovery path. The SOC’s position: the persistence is identified and removable, but the attacker had GA-level access for at least 11 days; the SOC’s confidence in bounded scope is “moderate”, not “high”. The Identity Engineering Lead notes that the regulatory framework the agency operates under has elevated assurance expectations after a breach. The Cloud Platform Lead estimates: in-tenant clean-up 2-3 weeks; clean-tenant migration 8-12 weeks. The BCDR Steering Committee chair joins the call.

Discussion prompts
  • What are the documented criteria for the in-tenant vs clean-tenant decision? (See DR-WI-02 Scenario 4.2.)
  • The SOC’s confidence is “moderate”, not “high” — does that meet the criterion for in-tenant clean-up, or does it tip toward clean-tenant?
  • What is the cost of being wrong — clean-up when clean-tenant was needed vs the reverse?
  • Once clean-up begins, evidence is altered and migrating to clean-tenant becomes far harder. Is that irreversibility understood?
  • Who actually makes this call — the BCDR Steering Committee, CEO, or someone else? Is the authority documented?
T+80 min Break-glass account use required

The recovery action requires Global Admin operations the SOC is not yet confident treating as trustworthy under normal admin accounts. The decision is made to use a break-glass account for the credential-rotation and role-cleanup phase. The Identity Engineering Lead asks: who can authorise break-glass use; is the access procedure documented; have we tested it in the last quarter as required by the cloud baseline?

Discussion prompts
  • Is break-glass authorisation defined? Per IT-ID-WI-05?
  • Is quarterly validation actually performed? If “not since last year” or “not sure”, that is the finding.
  • The break-glass operates from a clean device per the baseline — is one available? If PAW per IT-EP-SOP-PAW-01 isn’t in place, what is the fallback?
  • What does standing-down look like (credential rotation, return to vault, SOC alert reset)? Is the procedure followed exactly?
T+100 min Worst-case branch: break-glass account also locked out

Hypothetical inject. Assume for the next 15 minutes: the break-glass credential rotation has not been performed for over a year; the MFA device was decommissioned; the first break-glass account fails authentication. The second is similarly stale. The CMT is now in DR-WI-02 Scenario 3.6 territory — Microsoft Support escalation for tenant recovery.

Discussion prompts
  • Who is the named contact at Microsoft for tenant emergencies? Is it documented and current? Is it accessible without tenant authentication?
  • Microsoft tenant-recovery may take days. What runs on BCP for that window? Communications fallback — what works without M365?
  • How does this hypothetical change the agency’s view of break-glass governance? What follows in the AAR remediation?

Evaluation criteria
  • Objective 1 (SOC triage path): from detection to scope-establishment within 60 minutes; recovery-threshold concept used correctly
  • Objective 2 (clean-up vs clean-tenant): participants can articulate the documented criteria; the decision is connected to the criteria, not to convenience or familiarity
  • Objective 3 (break-glass readiness): participants know who authorises, where the credentials are, what governance applies; the quarterly-validation status is known
  • Objective 4 (SOC + DR + Identity working model): ownership clear at every step; handoffs explicit; no “they should be doing this” confusion
  • Objective 5 (Microsoft escalation): contact path located; understood as last resort, not first option
  • Cross-cutting: Friday-late-afternoon timing handled without abandoning the response or working unsustainable hours; CMT cadence sustainable for a multi-day event
After-action review framework

Conducted within 14 days. Distribution: BCDR Steering Committee, CISO, SOC Lead, Identity & Access Engineering Lead, IT DR Coordinator, CMT Lead.

Common findings: break-glass quarterly-validation gap; clean-up vs clean-tenant decision criteria not understood by decision-makers; CMT severity threshold for cyber events ambiguous or different from BCDR threshold; Microsoft account-team contacts hard to find outside the tenant; sustained-recovery communications not pre-planned; legitimate Global Admin recovery (post-hijack) not in any current runbook.
