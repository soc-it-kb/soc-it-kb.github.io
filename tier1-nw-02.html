NW-02: Internal Reconnaissance / Lateral Movement

Answer each question YES or NO and apply the decision logic. Escalate with evidence attached — do not reboot or alter the endpoint before escalating.

🌐 Network threats Tier 1 Published 28 April 2026
🔔 When to use this checklist
SIEM or NDR alert suggesting an endpoint or account is scanning internal hosts, attempting to access multiple systems, or exhibiting lateral movement behaviour.
Internal port scanning alert SMB connection to multiple hosts RDP connection anomaly Pass-the-hash indicator Unusual internal authentication attempts SIEM lateral movement rule

Step 2 — Apply decision logic
✅
Single connection, legitimate business use case confirmed — Document and close with monitoring note.
⚠️
YES to questions 1, 2, or 5 — Escalate to Tier-2. Isolate source endpoint. Invoke EP-PB-03.
🔴
YES to question 3, 4, or 8 — Escalate to Tier-3 immediately. Invoke NW-PB-03. Isolate source. Consider east-west block via NW-WI-03.
🔴
YES to question 6 or 7 — Escalate immediately. Tier-0/1 involvement or after-hours activity significantly raises severity.
Step 3 — Capture evidence before escalating
Source endpoint hostname and user
Destination IPs and hostnames targeted
Ports and protocols used in scanning
Authentication logs showing attempted or successful logons
Timestamps and duration of activity
Any new accounts or group membership changes observed
💡
Analyst Tip
Internal scanning is almost always intentional — legitimate tools don't randomly connect to dozens of internal hosts on port 445. The key question is whether the source endpoint has a business reason to connect to those destinations. A user workstation connecting to 20 servers in 5 minutes almost certainly indicates either a compromised endpoint or an attacker using stolen credentials.
