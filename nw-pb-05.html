NW-PB-05: Denial of Service (DoS) / Network Disruption

Standardised framework for detecting, investigating, and responding to DoS attacks and network disruption events — ensuring rapid service stabilisation, coordinated infrastructure response, and protection of critical services.

Section 1
Purpose

Provide a standardised decision and response framework for detecting, investigating, and responding to Denial of Service (DoS) attacks and network disruption events, ensuring rapid service stabilisation, coordinated response with infrastructure teams, and protection of critical services.

This playbook addresses availability-impacting incidents, which may be malicious (DoS/DDoS), abuse-driven (resource exhaustion), or symptomatic of a broader attack (ransomware, extortion, diversion tactic).

Attack type reference

Malicious

Intentional DoS or DDoS from attacker-controlled infrastructure

Abuse-driven

Resource exhaustion from crawlers, scrapers, or misconfigured systems

Diversion

DoS used to distract while a concurrent attack (ransomware, exfiltration) occurs

Section 2
Scope

✅ In scope

  • Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks
  • Targeted DoS against applications, networks, or services
  • Network saturation or bandwidth exhaustion
  • Resource exhaustion on network devices
  • Network disruption detected by firewalls, NDR, ISP alerts, or MDR

❌ Out of scope

  • Endpoint-level resource exhaustion → EP playbooks
  • Planned outages or maintenance events
  • Data exfiltration without service impact → NW-PB-04
Section 3
Trigger Conditions
  • Network or application becomes unavailable or severely degraded
  • Firewall or NDR detects volumetric traffic spikes
  • ISP or upstream provider reports DDoS activity
  • MDR escalates suspected DoS or service disruption
  • SOC observes sudden traffic surges, connection exhaustion, or service timeouts across multiple users
Section 4
Initial Validation (Decision Logic)

Validate whether the disruption is malicious rather than operational:

  • Is there an unusual and unexplained spike in inbound traffic?
  • Are requests malformed, repetitive, or clearly automated?
  • Is traffic sourced from multiple or spoofed IP addresses?
  • Does the issue persist after standard operational troubleshooting?

If NO — legitimate outage or misconfiguration suspected → Route to IT incident management
If YES or UNCLEAR → Proceed with DoS assessment (Section 5)

Section 5
DoS / Disruption Assessment

Assess type, scope, and confidence. Presence of any indicator = Suspected DoS / Network Attack:

Volumetric traffic spike significantly above baseline
Distributed source IPs or spoofed source addresses
Application-layer abuse (HTTP floods, API hammering)
Targeted service or resource exhaustion
Concurrent extortion or threat message received
Section 6
Severity & Escalation Criteria

Escalate to High or Critical Severity if any of the following apply:

🔴 Public-facing or mission-critical services impacted
🔴 Attack is sustained or actively escalating
🔴 Multiple services or geographic locations affected
🔴 DoS coincides with ransomware, exfiltration, or extortion threats
🔴 MDR or ISP confirms a malicious, organised attack

Section 9
Containment & Mitigation Logic

Select the mitigation strategy based on the attack type identified in Section 5:

Volumetric attacks

  • Activate upstream filtering or traffic scrubbing service
  • Engage ISP or DDoS protection provider
  • Apply null-routing for identified source IP ranges

Application-layer attacks

  • Apply rate limiting at the WAF or load balancer
  • Adjust WAF rules to block abusive patterns
  • Implement temporary service access restrictions

Internal disruption

  • Apply network segment isolation
  • Activate service failover if available
  • Identify and contain the internal source

Mitigation actions should prioritise stability and reduction of user impact. Coordinate all actions with the network team before applying changes to production infrastructure.

Section 10
Scope Expansion Logic

Scope expansion is mandatory for all NW-PB-05 incidents — particularly to detect diversion attacks:

Impact identification

  • Identify all services impacted by the disruption
  • Determine geographic and network scope of the attack
  • Monitor for secondary or follow-on intrusion attempts

Diversion check

  • Review concurrent security alerts (phishing, ransomware, C2)
  • Assess whether the DoS is a diversion for another simultaneous attack
  • Escalate to relevant playbooks if concurrent attack confirmed
Section 11
Communication & Coordination
  • Activate Incident Command if availability is materially impacted
  • Maintain a single source of truth for all incident updates
  • Coordinate technical actions with network teams and ISP simultaneously
  • Align external communications with Leadership and Comms/PR before publishing
  • Maintain MDR situational awareness throughout
Section 12
Closure Criteria

The incident may be closed when all of the following are confirmed:

  • Network and services fully stabilised and operating normally
  • Malicious traffic mitigated or naturally subsided
  • No secondary or concurrent attacks observed
  • Root cause identified and documented
  • Post-incident resilience review completed
Section 13
Evidence to Capture
Traffic volumes and timelines
Source IP ranges and ASNs
Targeted services and ports
Firewall / NDR / ISP alert exports
All mitigation actions taken and timestamps
Availability impact timeline
Section 14
Post-Incident Actions
  • Review DDoS protection posture and service provider capabilities
  • Improve rate-limiting and WAF rule coverage
  • Enhance network resilience and redundancy where gaps identified
  • Tune detection thresholds to reduce false positives and improve speed of detection
  • Update crisis and communications playbooks based on lessons learned
  • Conduct a formal resilience review and table-top exercise
