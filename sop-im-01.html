SOP-IM-01: Incident Severity Classification & Escalation

The SOC's operational standard for classifying, prioritising, and escalating cyber security incidents. Implements the 5-tier severity model defined in GRC-IRP-01 §5 and provides analyst-level triage guidance, declaration authority, and ticketing mechanics.

Cross-domain All SOC tiers Sev 1–5 Operationalises GRC-IRP-01
🛡️ This document operates under GRC-IRP-01: Cyber Security Incident Response Plan. The IRP is authoritative for the severity model, escalation paths, evidence handling, and communication templates. This SOP provides the analyst-level operational implementation.

Section 1
Purpose & Relationship to GRC-IRP-01

This Standard Operating Procedure defines how the SOC classifies the severity of confirmed cyber security incidents, who has authority to declare each severity tier, and how incidents are escalated through SOC tiers and into the broader response chain.

This SOP is the operational implementation of the framework defined in GRC-IRP-01. Where this SOP and the IRP appear to differ, the IRP is authoritative. Specifically:

  • Severity model — The 5-tier model (Sev 1 Crisis → Sev 5 Low) is defined in IRP §5. This SOP provides analyst-level criteria and concrete examples for assigning a tier.
  • Declaration authority — The "who can declare what" matrix in IRP §7.1 is reproduced here in operational form for analyst use.
  • Escalation paths — Cumulative notification lists and trigger-based notifications are defined in IRP §8. This SOP summarises the SOC-relevant subset.
  • Definitions — Authoritative definitions (incident, event of interest, material incident, notifiable breach, Incident Commander) are in IRP §3. This SOP adds SOC-local operational terms only.
Section 2
Scope

Applies to

  • All SOC analysts (Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3 / IR)
  • The SOC Manager acting as Incident Commander for Sev 3 and below
  • All confirmed cyber security incidents managed by the SOC
  • All events of interest under triage that may become incidents

Does NOT cover

  • Alert triage steps — see Runbooks
  • Incident response actions — see Playbooks
  • Tool-specific actions — see Work Instructions
  • Sev 1 / Sev 2 declaration mechanics, regulator notification, war-room operation — see GRC-IRP-01 §5–§9
  • External communications — see GRC-FRM-14
Section 3
Definitions

Authoritative definitions of incident, event of interest, material incident, notifiable breach, Incident Commander, CSIRT, and related terms are in GRC-IRP-01 §3. The SOC-local operational terms below are defined for use within this SOP and SOC tooling.

Severity tag
The Sev 1–5 value applied to the incident record in the system of record. Drives playbook selection, escalation behaviour, and reporting.
Triage outcome
The Tier-1 disposition of an alert: nominal event (close), event of interest (continue investigation), or confirmed incident (declare with severity).
Severity reassessment
A formal re-evaluation of the severity tag at a defined trigger point — when scope expands, when new evidence emerges, after containment, or at the request of the IC.
SOC handover
Transfer of incident ownership between SOC tiers (Tier 1 → Tier 2 → SOC Manager) or between shifts. Distinct from escalation, which expands visibility rather than transferring ownership.
Section 4
Severity Tiers (Sev 1–5)

The following five tiers operationalise the model defined in GRC-IRP-01 §5. Each tier card shows the qualitative descriptor, an SLA reminder, and concrete SOC-level examples to assist Tier-1/Tier-2 analyst classification. Authoritative SLAs and the relationship to the residual-risk severity in GRC-REG-RISK are detailed in the IRP.

🚨 Sev 1 — Crisis

Declare ≤ 30 min · Escalate ≤ 1 h · Exec brief ≤ 2 h

Material disruption to organisation-wide services, suspected or confirmed Tier-0 compromise, sovereign or highly sensitive data exposure, ransom demand, or potential global media exposure. The Incident Commander is the CISO; war-room is opened.

SOC analyst examples:

  • Confirmed ransomware deployment across multiple business units with active encryption
  • Domain Administrator account compromise with confirmed Tier-0 lateral movement
  • External party publishes proof of exfiltrated sensitive data with verifiable samples
  • Threat actor delivers ransom demand referencing organisation-specific assets
SOC actions for suspected Sev 1: Do not declare unilaterally. Propose to the SOC Manager who escalates to the CISO for confirmation. Open the bridge, assign a Recorder, do not take destructive actions, preserve forensic evidence.

🔴 Sev 2 — Critical

Declare ≤ 1 h · Escalate ≤ 2 h · Exec brief ≤ 4 h

Confirmed compromise of Tier-1 assets, disruption to a critical business service, suspected data exposure of limited scope, or credible threat actor presence. The Incident Commander is the CISO or Deputy CISO; dedicated incident bridge is established.

SOC analyst examples:

  • Confirmed BEC with completed financial fraud transaction
  • Privileged service-account credential theft with confirmed use against Tier-1 systems
  • Active malware on multiple endpoints with confirmed lateral movement attempts
  • Confirmed data exfiltration affecting a defined customer or beneficiary cohort
  • Sustained DDoS taking down a critical externally-facing business service

🟠 Sev 3 — High

Declare ≤ 2 h · Escalate ≤ 4 h · CISO brief same day

Compromise contained to a defined scope (single user, single host, or single application), no confirmed data exposure, service impact limited to non-critical systems or a small user group. The Incident Commander is the SOC Manager; CSIRT is activated as needed.

SOC analyst examples:

  • Single user clicks a phishing link AND submits credentials AND credentials are confirmed used by attacker
  • Malware execution on a single endpoint with confirmed payload but no lateral movement
  • Single web application targeted by a confirmed exploit with limited blast radius
  • Suspicious privileged-account activity confirmed as unauthorised, contained quickly

🟡 Sev 4 — Medium

Declare ≤ 4 h · SOC Manager next business day · Closure ≤ 5 working days

Confirmed but contained event with no impact to data or service, isolated phishing-clicks without compromise, policy violations without material risk, or suspicious activity warranting investigation. Handled by the SOC Tier-2 lead; SOC Manager informed but not engaged operationally.

SOC analyst examples:

  • User clicks a phishing link without submitting credentials or executing payload
  • Malware blocked at the perimeter with confirmed targeting (not generic spam)
  • Anomalous login from unusual geo-location with confirmed user-acknowledgement
  • Confirmed policy violation (e.g. unauthorised data-handling) without material risk

🟢 Sev 5 — Low

Closure same shift · Aggregated weekly

Confirmed nominal event, near-miss, informational alert validated as non-incident, or minor policy reminder. Closed by SOC Tier-1 with no escalation. Aggregated weekly for trend analysis only.

SOC analyst examples:

  • Generic spam email reported by user, no malicious indicators present
  • Routine vulnerability-scan blocked at perimeter, no targeting indicators
  • False-positive alert validated through enrichment (geo-IP, user attestation)
  • Policy reminder triggered (e.g. legitimate password reset that fired anomaly detection)
When in doubt, default to the higher severity and escalate. Downgrading is always easier than under-responding. If you cannot decide between two adjacent tiers, propose the higher one and let the IC adjust.
Section 5
Severity Assignment Criteria

When assigning a severity tier, analysts evaluate the incident against the four primary criteria below. Each criterion has an "upgrade" indicator that pushes severity higher, and a "downgrade" indicator that supports a lower tier. Apply the criteria together, not in isolation — a single high indicator is rarely sufficient on its own.

CriterionUpgrade indicatorsDowngrade indicators
Scope of impact Multiple users, multiple systems, multiple business units, or organisation-wide services affected Single user, single host, single application, or contained to a defined small group
Data sensitivity Personal data, special-category data, financial data, intellectual property, sovereign data, or regulator-controlled data is in scope No sensitive data in scope, or only public/internal data of low classification
Active threat presence Threat actor has confirmed access, is actively executing, lateral movement is observed, or persistence mechanisms are in place Threat is blocked at the perimeter, payload did not execute, or the attempt is closed without compromise
Asset criticality Tier-0 or Tier-1 assets affected, business-critical service disruption, or assets supporting regulatory/contractual obligations Tier-2 or Tier-3 assets only, non-critical service, or development/test environments
Reassessment is mandatory when scope expands, when new evidence emerges (e.g. confirmed exfiltration), after containment is achieved, and at the request of the IC. Record each reassessment with the new severity and the reason for the change in the incident journal.
Section 6
Decision Flow — From Alert to Severity

Every alert flows through three decision gates before a severity tag is applied. The decisions and their rationale are recorded in the incident journal at the time they are taken.

Gate 1Is this an event of interest? Has security tooling, an employee, a partner, or an external party reported something that may be an incident? Yes → continue. No → close as nominal event with reason recorded.
Gate 2Is this a confirmed incident? Has a security policy, acceptable-use policy, or standard practice been violated, with potential or actual adverse effect on the organisation? Yes → declare incident, continue to Gate 3. No → close as event of interest with investigation summary.
Gate 3What severity tier? Apply the criteria in §5 against the qualitative descriptors in §4. Propose a tier; the appropriate authority (per §7) confirms it.

For the broader 7-phase incident lifecycle (Prepare → Detect → Contain → Eradicate → Recover → Report → Remediate), see GRC-IRP-01 §6.

Section 7
Declaration Authority

Severity is assigned by the role with authority to confirm it, which depends on the proposed tier. The table below is the operational form of GRC-IRP-01 §7.1.

SeverityDefault declarer (proposes)Authority to confirm
Sev 5 — LowSOC Tier-1SOC Tier-2 reviews aggregate weekly
Sev 4 — MediumSOC Tier-2 (or Tier-1 with Tier-2 review)SOC Tier-2 lead
Sev 3 — HighSOC Manager (Incident Commander)SOC Manager — CISO informed
Sev 2 — CriticalSOC Manager proposes — CISO confirmsCISO or Deputy CISO
Sev 1 — CrisisSOC Manager or CISO proposes — CISO confirmsCISO — Risk Committee Chair informed within 1 h
Authority delegation. Where the named role is unavailable, the SOC follows the on-call rota. The on-call deputy declares cautiously, with the named role notified at the earliest opportunity. Out-of-hours declarations follow the same authority pattern using on-call escalation.
Section 8
Roles & Responsibilities

The roles below cover SOC operational responsibilities for incident severity classification and escalation. The full two-layer role model (organisational roles plus response roles activated during an active incident) is in GRC-IRP-01 §4.

RoleResponsibilities
SOC Tier-1 AnalystTriage alerts to determine event-of-interest status; propose initial severity for Sev 4/5; create and maintain the incident record in the system of record; hand over to Tier-2 with severity rationale documented.
SOC Tier-2 AnalystConfirm Sev 4/5 declarations; investigate and propose Sev 3 declarations; reassess severity as evidence emerges; coordinate with playbook owners; brief the SOC Manager on Sev 3 candidates.
SOC ManagerActs as Incident Commander for Sev 3 and below. Confirms Sev 3 declarations; proposes Sev 2/1 for CISO confirmation; assigns response roles; ensures SLAs are met; owns the incident journal during active response. For Sev 2/1, becomes Deputy IC under the CISO.
SOC LeadProgramme oversight, governance of this SOP, post-incident review, audit liaison. Reviews Sev 3+ trends weekly; reviews Sev 4 trends monthly; reviews Sev 5 aggregate weekly.
Terminology note. Earlier SOC documentation referred to "Incident Manager" or "IR Lead". Both terms are now superseded by Incident Commander across the IRP, this SOP, and the four sibling SOPs (SOP-EM/EP/ID/NW-01). The role definition is unchanged; only the name is aligned.
Section 9
Escalation Rules

Escalations are cumulative: notifying the Sev 1 list also implies notification of the Sev 2/3/4/5 lists. The full escalation tree (including non-SOC roles such as Legal, Privacy/DPO, CFO, Risk Committee Chair) is in GRC-IRP-01 §8. The summary below covers the SOC-relevant subset analysts must action.

SeveritySOC escalation actionMaximum time-to-notify
Sev 5SOC Tier-1 closes; aggregate weekly to SOC Tier-2 and SOC ManagerEnd of shift
Sev 4SOC Tier-2 leads; SOC Manager informed next business day4 h
Sev 3SOC Manager (IC), CSIRT activated; LCSO/CSLC notified for affected operating unit2 h
Sev 2+ CISO, CIO, Head of GRC, Local Executive Sponsor, Privacy/DPO1 h
Sev 1+ CEO/Agency Lead, Risk Committee Chair, Legal, HR, PR, CFO, Cyber Insurer, Oversight Committee (per IRP §8)30 min

For non-cumulative trigger-based notifications independent of severity, see §10 below.

Section 10
Trigger-Based Notifications (Independent of Severity)

Some events require specific notifications regardless of the tier assigned. SOC analysts must initiate these notifications as soon as the trigger is suspected — do not wait for severity confirmation. Authoritative list in GRC-IRP-01 §8.

Personal-data breach (suspected or confirmed) — notify Privacy/DPO, Legal, CISO within 1 h of suspicion. Do not delay pending complete information.
Ransom demand received — notify CISO, CIO, CEO, Legal, Cyber Insurer within 1 h. Preserve the demand exactly; do not respond.
Compromise of leadership or board accounts — notify CISO, CIO, Legal within 30 min.
Critical-data integrity or availability impact — notify BC/DR team, IT Operations, business service owners within 1 h.
Material organisational impact suspected — notify Legal, CFO, executive leadership within 1 h.
Regulator-notifiable breach suspected — engage Privacy/DPO and Legal to start the regulator clock per local annex.
Law-enforcement engagement under consideration — engage Legal first; LE engagement only with Legal sign-off.
Media enquiry or social-media exposure — notify PR, Legal, CISO within 30 min of receipt.
Section 11
Ticketing Mechanics

Every confirmed incident is opened as a record in the system of record (RTIR / ITSM tool). The fields and conventions below are mandatory and feed downstream reporting, audit evidence, and metric extraction.

1
Required fields at incident opening
  • Incident ID — assigned automatically; recorded in all subsequent communications
  • Severity tag — Sev 1 / Sev 2 / Sev 3 / Sev 4 / Sev 5 (single value, system-of-record dropdown)
  • Category — primary category from the IRP §7.2 taxonomy (account compromise, breach, malware, social engineering, etc.)
  • Affected assets — host names, account names, service names, with Tier classification (Tier 0/1/2/3)
  • Incident Commander — named individual (SOC Manager for Sev 3 and below; CISO/Deputy for Sev 2/1)
  • Lead Analyst — named investigator
  • Recorder — named scribe maintaining the incident journal (Sev 1/2 only)
  • Detection source — alert source, user report, partner notification, etc.
  • Triage rationale — short text justifying the severity assignment against §5 criteria
2
State transitions

Standard state model: Open → Triaging → Investigating → Containing → Eradicating → Recovering → Closed (response) → Closed (final). State transitions are time-stamped automatically. The incident does not move to Closed (final) until the after-action report is signed off and lessons-learned actions are recorded.

3
Severity reassessment

When severity is changed (upgrade or downgrade), the system of record creates a severity-change record. The analyst captures: previous tier, new tier, reason, named approver. The IC is notified automatically on any tier change.

4
Mandatory tagging

Tag the incident record with: affected operating unit (for local annex routing), data-classification level if data is involved, regulator-relevance flag, cyber-insurance-relevance flag, and the "material incident" flag for Sev 1/2.

Section 12
Exceptions

Any deviation from this SOP must be documented in the incident record at the time the deviation is taken, include the operational justification, and be approved by the SOC Lead or the Incident Commander. Material deviations (those affecting severity, declaration authority, or escalation timing) are reviewed by the GRC team and reported to the Risk Committee in the next quarterly cycle.

Section 13
Compliance & Audit

The following artefacts evidence compliance with this SOP and underpin both internal assurance and external audit activity:

✅ Incident records with severity history ✅ Severity-change records with rationale ✅ Escalation timestamps ✅ Activated playbook references ✅ Trigger-based notification records ✅ Incident journals (Sev 1/2) ✅ After-action reports ✅ Aggregate Sev-5 trend reports

Records are retained per GRC-IRP-01 §10: 7 years for incident journals and after-action reports; 3 years for forensic captures and log exports.
