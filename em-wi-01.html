⚡ Quick reference — key header fields
Received:
Relay chain — read bottom → top. Bottom entry = true origin.
From:
Display name + address. Easily spoofed — never trust alone.
Return-Path:
Bounce address. Should match sender domain. Mismatch = red flag.
Reply-To:
Where replies go. External redirect here is a BEC indicator.
Authentication-Results:
SPF / DKIM / DMARC outcomes. Read all three together.
Message-ID:
Unique message identifier. Format reveals sending platform.
X-Originating-IP:
Sending client IP (if present). Check against geo and reputation.
X-Mailer / User-Agent:
Sending application. Mass-mail tools or unusual clients are suspicious.

EM-WI-01: Analyse Email Headers

Tool-agnostic procedure for analysing email message headers to identify true origin, detect spoofing and impersonation, validate authentication results, and produce defensible technical evidence.

Section 1
Purpose

Provide a consistent, tool-agnostic procedure to analyze email message headers in order to identify the true sender and message origin, detect spoofing or impersonation, validate authentication results, and produce defensible technical evidence for escalation or closure.

📌 Scope boundary: This Work Instruction covers investigation and evidence collection only. No blocking, removal, or user notification actions are performed here — those are executed via the relevant Runbooks and Playbooks.

Section 2
When to Use This Work Instruction

Section 4
Inputs Required

Before starting, confirm availability of all of the following:

📧 Full raw email headers (original, unmodified) 📎 Email sample or Message-ID 🎫 Incident or alert reference ID

⚠️ Partial headers or screenshots are not sufficient. If you cannot obtain the full raw headers, document this and escalate before proceeding.

Section 5
Pre-Checks & Safety Notes

Before beginning analysis, confirm the following safety constraints:

🚫 Mandatory constraints — do not proceed if any are violated
Do NOT modify the email in any way
Do NOT reply to or forward the email
Do NOT rely on the display name or visible "From" field — these are trivially spoofed
Do NOT click any links or open any attachments during header analysis

📌 This WI is read-only and observational. Treat all headers as forensic evidence — handle with care.

Section 6
Step-by-Step Actions
1
Confirm Header Completeness
1
Confirm the headers are full, unmodified, and include all Received: entries
2
Verify the headers include authentication results, Message-IDs, and full routing information
If headers are incomplete → request full headers before continuing. Do not analyse partial data.
2
Identify the Message Origin
1
Locate the bottom-most Received: header entry
⚠️ Top entries show final delivery, not origin. Always read from the bottom up.
2
Record the originating source:
  • Sending mail server hostname
  • Sending IP address
  • Timestamp of origination
📬 Relay chain — reading direction
1 from attacker-server.xyz (203.0.113.42) ⬅ Read first — true origin
↓
2 by mail-gateway.provider.com Relay hop
↓
3 by mx.recipient-org.com (final delivery) Final delivery
↑ Headers appear in reverse order in the email — bottom entry is earliest
3
Analyse the Mail Relay Chain
1
Review all Received: headers bottom → top and validate logical hop order and timestamp progression
2
Flag any of the following anomalies:
  • Unexpected or unrecognised relay hosts in the chain
  • Geographic inconsistencies — e.g. claimed EU origin with Asian relay
  • Timestamps out of sequence or with impossible time gaps
  • Open relay behaviour (public mail servers routing internal-looking messages)
4
Review Sender Identity Fields
1
Compare all four sender-related fields side by side:
  • From: — the display address (easily spoofed)
  • Return-Path: — bounce destination (should match sender domain)
  • Reply-To: — where replies are directed (external redirect = red flag)
  • Sender: — actual sending entity if different from From
2
Flag any of the following:
  • Domains differ unexpectedly between From and Return-Path
  • Reply-To redirects to an external or unrelated domain
  • Free mail provider (Gmail, Outlook.com) used to impersonate a corporate domain
  • Look-alike or typo-squatted domain in any field
5
Review Authentication Results (SPF / DKIM / DMARC)
1
Locate the Authentication-Results: header and record each result
ProtocolResultWhat it meansInvestigation action
SPFpassSending IP is authorised for the domainCheck domain still matches expected sender
SPFfailSending IP not authorised — strong spoofing indicatorEscalate if domain is a trusted org
SPFsoftfailIP not authorised but policy is non-enforcingTreat as suspicious — correlate with other signals
SPFnoneDomain has no SPF record publishedLower confidence — check DKIM and DMARC
DKIMpassMessage content unchanged — signing domain verifiedNote the d= signing domain — confirm it matches sender
DKIMfailSignature invalid — message may have been tamperedTreat as suspicious
DKIMnoneNo DKIM signature presentNot necessarily malicious — note absence
DMARCpassSPF or DKIM aligned with From domainStill check domain alignment manually
DMARCfailNeither SPF nor DKIM aligned with From domainStrong spoofing indicator — escalate
DMARCnoneDomain has no DMARC policyEasier to spoof — rely on other signals

⚠️ Authentication pass ≠ legitimacy. A message can pass SPF and DKIM while still being malicious — if the attacker controls the sending domain. Always verify that the passing domain is the expected domain, not a look-alike.

6
Identify Spoofing & Impersonation Indicators
1
Look for and document each of the following indicators:
  • Free mail provider used to impersonate an organisation (e.g. ceo.company@gmail.com)
  • Look-alike or typo-squatted domain (e.g. company-inc.com vs company.com)
  • Missing, absent, or malformed authentication headers
  • DKIM signed by a domain unrelated to the From: domain
  • Unusual X-Mailer or User-Agent indicating bulk-mail tooling
2
Document each indicator clearly with the exact header field and value observed
7
Correlate with Message Context
1
Correlate header findings with the following contextual signals:
  • Email content — does urgency, tone, or requests align with spoofing indicators?
  • Targeting — single user, executive, finance, or broadcast to many?
  • Sender behaviour — first contact, new domain, unusual sending time?
2
Header legitimacy must align with content legitimacy — a legitimate header does not absolve suspicious content
8
Assess Header Verdict

✅ Legitimate

All fields consistent, auth passes, domain aligns, no anomalies

⚠️ Suspicious

Minor anomalies present — escalate to Tier-2 for validation

🟠 Likely Malicious

Multiple indicators present — invoke relevant playbook

🔴 Confirmed Malicious

Clear spoofing or impersonation — escalate immediately

📌 Document the technical rationale for the classification — list specific header fields, values observed, and the reasoning. Unsupported verdicts cannot be used for escalation or closure.

9
Summarise Findings
1
Prepare a concise summary for the incident ticket including:
  • Originating IP address and mail server hostname
  • Authentication results — SPF / DKIM / DMARC with domain alignment notes
  • Sender field assessment — From, Return-Path, Reply-To comparison
  • Spoofing or relay anomalies identified
  • Verdict classification and confidence level
  • Recommended next action (close / escalate / invoke playbook)
2
Attach the raw headers to the ticket — do not rely on summary notes alone
Section 7
Expected Outcome

✅ Clear understanding of email origin, routing, and relay chain
✅ Authentication results recorded and interpreted with domain alignment validated
✅ Spoofing and impersonation indicators documented or ruled out
✅ Defensible technical record suitable for escalation or closure

Section 8
Common Mistakes to Avoid
Trusting the display name or visible "From" field — these can be set to anything
Reading Received: headers top → bottom — always read bottom → top; top shows delivery, not origin
Treating SPF pass as proof of legitimacy — the passing domain must also match the expected sender domain
Ignoring Reply-To manipulation — a legitimate-looking From with an attacker-controlled Reply-To is a classic BEC indicator
Skipping DKIM signing domain alignment — DKIM pass with a d= domain that doesn't match the sender is still suspicious
Analysing partial or screenshot-based headers — partial data produces unreliable verdicts
Section 9
Escalation Conditions

Immediately escalate to Tier-2 or invoke the relevant playbook if any of the following are confirmed:

Spoofing or impersonation confirmed in header analysis
Authentication failures (SPF fail / DMARC fail) against a trusted or recognised domain
Header evidence aligns with phishing or BEC content
Multiple recipients identified — potential campaign
Legal, HR, Finance, or executive targets identified in recipient list
