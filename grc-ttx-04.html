GRC-TTX-04 — Critical SaaS Provider Sustained Failure

Tabletop scenario: a critical SaaS provider holding agency data discloses a serious cyber incident, suspends customer access for the duration of investigation, and provides no clear ETA for service restoration. Tests GRC-DR-WI-02 Scenario 5 at sustained scale. Mixed audience — CMT, legal, IT, and SOC all involved.

BCDR + cyber hybrid Mixed audience 90 minutes Supplier risk
Scenario at a glance

Primary documents tested
DR-WI-02 (Scenario 5), supplier risk, BCP plans

Supplier-side incidents are increasingly common, and the agency’s exposure to them grows as more functions move to SaaS. This scenario stresses the often-unaddressed question: what happens when our data is in the hands of a compromised provider whose own recovery may take weeks? Decisions about data exfiltration assessment, regulatory notification, business continuity workarounds, and supplier-relationship management all surface simultaneously.

Objectives
  • Test the response to a critical-supplier cyber incident — what is the agency’s playbook when the breach is at a supplier, not internal?
  • Test data-exposure assessment when the breach is at the supplier: what data was held, what may have been accessed, what is the agency’s notification obligation?
  • Test BCP workarounds for SaaS unavailability per the relevant BCP plans — including whether alternative-access patterns exist or were ever designed
  • Test supplier-relationship decisions: does the agency continue using this provider after recovery, switch, or remain in limbo?
  • Test cross-AKDN coordination — many AKDN agencies use shared SaaS providers; the incident affects multiple agencies in parallel

Ground rules
  • No-fault learning environment. Findings about supplier-management, contracts, BCP design.
  • Participants reference real supplier contracts and BCP plans where useful.
  • Specific named suppliers should be discussed abstractly (“the HR system”, “the donor management system”) unless the exercise is intentionally focused on one named provider.
  • Discussion of provider behaviour is in good faith — this exercise is about the agency’s response, not a critique of any real provider.
Starting conditions (T+0)
Day & time
Wednesday, 14:30 local time
Setting
Mid-week, normal operations
Initial impact
Critical SaaS access suspended by provider

Initial situation: for the purposes of this exercise, the affected SaaS is the agency’s primary [choose one: HR information system / donor management system / financial system / clinical scheduling system] holding personal data of staff and / or beneficiaries / donors. At 14:00, the provider issued a customer notification: “We are investigating a serious cyber security incident affecting our infrastructure. Customer access has been suspended as a precaution. We will provide updates as our investigation progresses. Customers are advised that personal data may have been accessed; assessment is ongoing.” Provider has not yet given an ETA. IT Service Owner has confirmed receipt and forwarded to the CMT Lead.

What participants know at T+0:

  • Customer access to the SaaS is suspended; this is the agency’s authoritative system for the affected function
  • Provider acknowledges possible personal-data exposure; investigation is ongoing
  • No ETA for service restoration
  • Provider has not yet given specific guidance on customer notification obligations
Injects
T+30 min Operational impact and BCP invocation

The BPO of the function dependent on the SaaS reports: normal operations require access to the system multiple times per hour. Without it, the function can operate for a few hours but not for days at any normal volume. Are there workarounds? Have they been documented? Have they been practised?

Discussion prompts
  • Is the BCP plan for this function up to date? Does it have a SaaS-unavailable workaround?
  • Is there an independent backup or export of the SaaS data the agency can read from? Per the DR policy, Tier-1 SaaS should have alternative-access patterns or a documented limit-of-recovery.
  • If alternative-access exists but was never tested, how do you test it under pressure?
  • How long can the function operate before this becomes material business risk?
T+60 min Data-exposure question; Legal asks for the data inventory

Legal / DPO asks: what data did the provider hold? Specifically, what personal-data categories, what volumes, which data subjects (staff, beneficiaries, donors)? The IT Service Owner’s answer is: “the system held everything that function operates on, but the precise data inventory is in the system itself which we currently cannot access”. Legal asks: do we have a data inventory independent of the system? When was the last DPIA performed for this processing?

Discussion prompts
  • Is there a data inventory for SaaS-held data independent of the SaaS? Per data-protection good practice there should be; in reality often there is not.
  • When was the last DPIA (data protection impact assessment) for this processing? Is it current?
  • Can the agency answer the regulator’s likely questions: how many data subjects, what categories of personal data, geographic scope?
  • If “we don’t know what data was exposed”, what does the agency do next?
T+90 min Regulator clock starts; notification decisions needed

Legal / DPO confirms: under the applicable data protection regulation, notification is required within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal-data breach where the agency is the controller. The provider is the processor; the agency is the controller. The 72-hour clock has started from the provider’s notification at 14:00. The CEO asks: what do we tell the regulator at this stage with incomplete information? What do we tell data subjects?

Discussion prompts
  • The controller-processor distinction — is it understood at CMT level? Does it affect what the agency must do (yes; the agency’s notification obligation is its own, not the provider’s)?
  • Notifying with incomplete information is required by regulation but goes against typical comms instincts. How is that resolved?
  • If data subjects need to be notified, what is the channel? Many data subjects’ contact info is in the affected SaaS — how does the agency reach them?
  • Is there a pre-prepared regulator-notification template?
T+3 days Provider update: 2 weeks minimum; data-access partially confirmed

The provider has issued a Day-3 update: “Restoration of service is expected to take at least 2 weeks. Investigation has confirmed that some customer data was accessed by the threat actor; specific exposure per customer will be communicated individually as confirmed.” The agency has not yet received a customer-specific notification. The Procurement Lead asks: do we wait, or do we activate a replacement?

Discussion prompts
  • Activation of a replacement provider in 2 weeks — is it feasible? Is there a pre-screened alternative? What data would need to be migrated, and from where?
  • Continuing with a provider that has had a serious incident — what is the agency’s position? Per GRC-POL-01 supplier security assessment, what additional assurance is needed?
  • If the provider has not given customer-specific notification yet, can the agency assess its own exposure? On what basis?
  • For donors / beneficiaries with growing concern over 3-day silence, what is the communication strategy?
T+7 days Cross-AKDN coordination: multiple agencies affected

Information emerges that other AKDN agencies also use this provider and are affected. AKDN central is convening a cross-agency response coordination. The question raised: do agencies act together or independently? Do they pool legal advice and notification approaches? Are migration decisions coordinated?

Discussion prompts
  • What is the framework for cross-AKDN coordination on supplier incidents? Is there one?
  • Each agency is the controller for its own data; coordination is helpful but the obligations are individual. How is that nuance navigated?
  • If multiple agencies decide to migrate, can it be a joint procurement? Does that accelerate or complicate?

Evaluation criteria
  • Objective 1 (supplier-incident response): playbook followed; controller-processor distinction understood; CMT moves promptly
  • Objective 2 (data-exposure assessment): independent data inventory exists or its absence is treated as the critical finding; DPIA status known
  • Objective 3 (BCP workarounds): documented, practised, or honestly assessed as absent and treated as the finding
  • Objective 4 (supplier-relationship decision): criteria articulated; decision connects to assurance and to operational need
  • Objective 5 (cross-AKDN coordination): framework discussed; controller obligations preserved; coordination distinct from shared decision-making
  • Cross-cutting: 72-hour regulator clock managed; templates ready or absence noted
After-action review framework

Conducted within 14 days. Distribution: CEO, CMT Lead, BCDR Steering Committee, BPOs, DPO, CISO, Procurement Lead, IT DR Coordinator.

Common findings: no independent data inventory for SaaS-held data; DPIA out of date or absent; BCP workaround for SaaS unavailability not documented; alternative-access patterns never designed for Tier-1 SaaS; regulator-notification template missing; data-subject notification channel dependent on the failed SaaS itself; supplier-relationship decision criteria not documented.
