ID-WI-05: Revoke OAuth Consent

Tool-agnostic procedure for revoking OAuth application consent and invalidating tokens to remove attacker persistence, stop non-interactive data access, and complete containment after phishing or BEC incidents.

Section 1
Purpose

Provide a consistent, tool-agnostic procedure to revoke OAuth application consent and invalidate tokens to remove attacker persistence, stop non-interactive access to email, files, or directory data, and complete containment after phishing or BEC incidents.

📌 Scope boundary: This WI does not decide which apps to revoke. App identification is performed in ID-WI-04. This WI defines how to revoke safely once those apps are approved for removal.

Section 5
Pre-Checks & Safety Notes
🚫 Mandatory constraints
Do NOT revoke admin-consented apps without Tier-2 and Identity team approval
Do NOT revoke broadly used business apps without impact assessment
Do NOT notify the user before revocation unless instructed
Ensure scope is exactly as approved — do not expand unilaterally

⚠️ OAuth revocation may immediately break legitimate app access and trigger user-visible disruption. Proceed deliberately and document any observed impact.

Section 6
Step-by-Step Actions
1
Validate Approved Scope
1
Review the approval record — confirm the exact app name, App ID, user vs tenant scope, and whether this is a user-consented or admin-consented app
2
If any mismatch exists between the approval and what you see in the platform — pause and escalate before proceeding
2
Revoke OAuth Consent
1
Using the approved identity administration method, revoke the OAuth consent for the specified app — user-specific or tenant-wide as approved
2
Confirm revocation completed. Record: app name and ID, scope, timestamp, operator.
3
Invalidate Active Tokens
1
Where supported, invalidate or revoke existing tokens and active sessions for the app
If token invalidation is not immediate, note the expected propagation delay and inform Tier-2.
2
Confirm tokens can no longer be used to access data
4
Validate Revocation Effectiveness
1
Confirm:
  • The app no longer appears in the user's consented applications
  • New access attempts by the app fail
  • No continued non-interactive data access is observed
2
If access persists — do not repeat revocation blindly. Escalate to Tier-2 for deeper investigation.
5
Coordinate Follow-On Actions
1
Confirm with the incident record whether these related steps are also required:
  • ID-WI-02 — Account credential reset (if not yet completed)
  • Mailbox rule review — check for inbox rules created by the compromised session
  • Endpoint investigation — if token theft was device-based
OAuth revocation alone is not full containment — it must be paired with credential hygiene.
6
Preserve Evidence & Document
1
Retain: app metadata, permission scopes, revocation confirmation, and timeline correlation with the incident. Do not delete preserved evidence.
2
Update the incident record with: app(s) revoked, scope and method, timestamp, validation outcome, observed user or service impact. Attach screenshots where available.
Section 7
Expected Outcome

✅ Malicious OAuth app access revoked
✅ Token-based persistence eliminated
✅ Non-interactive attacker access stopped
✅ Clear audit trail with revocation confirmation documented

Section 8
Common Mistakes to Avoid
Revoking without confirming exact app scope — always match App ID to what was approved
Assuming password reset invalidates OAuth tokens — revocation is a separate, explicit action
Revoking admin-consented apps without Identity team approval — this can cause business-wide disruption
Forgetting to check for active tokens after revocation — propagation delay can leave tokens valid temporarily
Treating OAuth revocation as full containment — credential reset and mailbox/endpoint review are also required
Section 9
Escalation Conditions
App access continues after revocation — possible token caching or platform propagation delay
Multiple users affected by the same app — tenant-wide scope may be required
App has tenant-wide or directory-level permissions
Business-critical application is impacted by revocation
Additional suspicious OAuth apps are discovered during or after revocation
