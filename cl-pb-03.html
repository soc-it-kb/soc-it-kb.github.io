CL-PB-03: Cloud Workload Compromise

Playbook for compromised cloud-hosted virtual machines, containers, Kubernetes workloads, serverless functions, app services, or platform workloads.

Section 1
Purpose

Playbook for compromised cloud-hosted virtual machines, containers, Kubernetes workloads, serverless functions, app services, or platform workloads.

This playbook provides a consistent incident lifecycle for cloud detection, containment, evidence preservation, recovery, and lessons learned.

Section 2
Scope

✅ In scope

  • Cloud-hosted virtual machines, containers, Kubernetes workloads, serverless functions, and app services
  • EDR or runtime alerts involving cloud workloads
  • Crypto-mining, web shell, malicious process, or abnormal outbound traffic
  • Unauthorised image execution or container escape signal
  • Workload identity, secrets, or data access linked to compromise

❌ Out of scope

  • Control-plane compromise or tenant-wide admin abuse → CL-PB-01
  • Public exposure without workload compromise → CL-PB-02
  • Pure endpoint workstation compromise → Endpoint playbooks
Section 3
Trigger Conditions

Initiate this playbook when any of the following occur:

  • EDR or runtime alert on cloud-hosted workload
  • Suspicious process, web shell, crypto-mining, or unauthorised image execution
  • Abnormal outbound traffic or command-and-control pattern
  • Container escape signal or unexpected privileged container
  • Vulnerability exploitation evidence on cloud workload
Section 4
Initial Validation (Decision Logic)

Validate whether the activity is expected, authorised, and business justified:

  • Is the alert linked to an active cloud workload and valid telemetry?
  • Is the process, image, function, or network behaviour expected for the service?
  • What business service, owner, identity, and data stores are attached?
  • Is there evidence of persistence, lateral movement, or credential access?

If a legitimate change or accepted exposure is confirmed, document the evidence and close as benign or approved exception. If unauthorised, suspicious, or unclear, continue with the assessment and escalation sections below.

Section 5
Cloud Workload Compromise Assessment
  • Identify workload type, owner, criticality, public exposure, attached identity, and data access.
  • Avoid destructive remediation before collecting evidence.
  • Review process execution, image provenance, exploited vulnerability, secrets exposure, lateral movement, and storage/database access.
  • Preserve logs, snapshots, image metadata, deployment history, network flows, and identity context.
Section 6
Severity & Escalation Criteria

Treat as High or Critical when compromise affects production workloads, public services, privileged workload identity, sensitive data, lateral movement, or active command-and-control. Treat as Medium when contained to non-production with no data or identity impact.

Section 7
Response Strategy
Stabilise the service and preserve evidence before rebuild.Required
Isolate network path, remove public ingress, and restrict outbound communication.Required
Snapshot disks, export configuration, preserve logs, and capture image or package evidence.Required

Section 9
Containment Decision Logic

Containment actions

  • Remove public ingress and restrict security group/firewall rules.
  • Quarantine subnet or detach from load balancer where approved.
  • Restrict outbound communication to known required destinations.
  • Snapshot before rebuild and preserve volatile context.

Approval boundary

  • Use read-only validation before changing production state where possible.
  • Obtain incident lead or authorised owner approval for disruptive changes.
  • Record every containment action in the incident or change ticket.
Section 10
Scope Expansion Logic

Expand investigation to

  • Review workload identity permissions, secrets, data stores, and adjacent workloads.
  • Check image provenance, CI/CD pipeline, container registry, and recent deployment history.
  • Search for similar vulnerable images, functions, and configurations.
  • Review outbound connections, dropped files, and lateral movement paths.

Stop expansion when

  • No related risky configuration, identity, workload, or data access remains.
  • Affected owner confirms business context and residual risk is accepted or removed.
  • Monitoring validates no recurrence after containment.
Section 11
Communication & Coordination
Incident Lead
Owns severity, coordination, and closure
Cloud / Platform Owner
Approves production-impacting actions
Business Owner
Confirms service and data impact
GRC / Legal / Privacy
Engage if data, regulatory, or notification risk exists
Section 12
Closure Criteria
  • Root cause and affected resources are documented.
  • Containment, recovery, and validation actions are complete or tracked as accepted risk.
  • Evidence package is attached to the incident record.
  • Preventive controls or remediation actions are assigned with owners and due dates.

Section 14
Post-Incident Actions
  • Update cloud security baselines, guardrails, and monitoring rules.
  • Correct IaC, policy, ownership, tagging, or review gaps.
  • Feed lessons learned into awareness, engineering backlog, and governance registers.
  • Review whether related runbooks or work instructions require updates.
