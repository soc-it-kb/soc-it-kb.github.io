IT-TI-SOP-01: Threat Intelligence Programme — Governance & Lifecycle

Defines how the threat intelligence programme is governed, what sources are consumed, how intelligence is processed and disseminated, and how it integrates with SOC and IT Operations.

Section 1
Purpose

This SOP defines the governance framework, operating model, and lifecycle for the Institution's Threat Intelligence (TI) programme. It establishes how threat intelligence is sourced, processed, disseminated, and used to improve detection, response, and risk decision-making across SOC operations and IT infrastructure.

📌 This programme is in a maturing phase — consuming open-source and selected commercial feeds to build operational intelligence capability. The objective is actionable intelligence that improves SOC effectiveness, not intelligence for its own sake.

Section 2
Scope
  • All threat intelligence sources consumed by the Institution — open-source, commercial, and community
  • All intelligence products produced or consumed by the SOC
  • Integration of TI into SOC detection, VM, patch prioritisation, and incident response
  • All personnel involved in consuming, producing, or acting on threat intelligence
Section 3
Guiding Principles

🎯 Intelligence must be actionable

TI that cannot be acted upon — through detection, blocking, or risk decision — has no operational value

📊 Relevance over volume

A small number of high-quality, contextualised indicators is more valuable than thousands of unvalidated feeds

🔄 Intelligence drives decisions

TI should actively influence SOC priorities, vulnerability remediation, and patching decisions — not sit in a separate silo

⏱️ Timeliness matters

Intelligence about an active campaign is only useful if it reaches the SOC in time to act on it

✅ Quality over quantity

Every indicator and feed must be validated before deployment into controls or detections

📝 Document everything

Intelligence sources, decisions, and actions must be traceable for audit and continuous improvement

Section 5
TI Lifecycle
1
Direction
Define intelligence requirements — what threats are most relevant to the Institution, its sector, and its technology stack. Review and update quarterly.
2
Collection
Consume intelligence from approved sources. Automate ingestion where tooling supports it. Log all sources and collection dates.
3
Processing
Deduplicate, normalise, and enrich raw intelligence. Apply confidence scoring. Remove expired or low-quality indicators.
4
Analysis
Assess relevance to the Institution. Correlate with known vulnerabilities, active campaigns, and existing detections. Identify actionable intelligence.
5
Dissemination
Route actionable intelligence to the right consumer — SOC for detection, VM team for prioritisation, IT Ops for patching, management for risk decisions.
6
Action & Feedback
Act on intelligence — deploy IOCs, update detections, escalate patches. Feed outcomes back to improve future intelligence requirements.
