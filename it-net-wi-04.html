IT-NET-WI-04: Wireless Hardening

Vendor-agnostic implementation guidance for hardening wireless access points and controllers: encryption, authentication, segmentation, rogue detection, RF management. Operationalises §4.4 of IT-NET-SOP-01.

Prerequisites

Before applying this WI:

  • The wireless controller (or cloud-management equivalent for cloud-managed APs) is hardened per IT-NET-WI-01 (management plane)
  • The certificate infrastructure is in place: organisation CA issuing server certificates for RADIUS / 802.1X authentication; client certificates issued via PKI for EAP-TLS where used
  • A RADIUS server (or equivalent) is deployed and reachable; 802.1X-capable identity store is configured (typically AD/Entra ID)
  • RF site survey is current; channel plan and coverage targets are documented
  • A guest-access architecture is defined (sponsor portal, rotated PSK, captive portal, or vendor flow)
  • Change window approved per IT-NET-WI-06
Wireless changes can disrupt all users at a site. SSID renames, encryption upgrades, and 802.1X migrations cause client re-authentication for everyone. Schedule outside business hours where possible.
Step-by-step instructions

The steps below cover the wireless controls required by IT-NET-SOP-01 §4.4.

1
Disable insecure legacy encryption
Remove WEP, open authentication on corporate, and any legacy ciphers.

On all SSIDs:

  • WEP — disabled, removed from any SSID, including legacy SSIDs (legacy devices that only support WEP need to be replaced or moved to a strictly-isolated segment)
  • Open authentication on corporate SSIDs — not permitted; guest SSIDs with open authentication require captive-portal and strict isolation
  • WPA-TKIP — disabled; WPA2/WPA3 with AES-CCMP or GCMP only
  • WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup) — disabled on all enterprise SSIDs
Verify. Show running configuration on controller / cloud portal — confirm no SSID uses WEP, TKIP, or open authentication on corporate flows. Run a wireless scanner (e.g. Kismet, vendor's tooling) near the site; confirm advertised authentication suites are WPA2/WPA3 only.
CIS Wireless: "remove insecure encryption"
2
WPA3-Enterprise (or WPA2-Enterprise as minimum)
Strong, authenticated, individually-credentialed access to corporate networks.

Configure corporate SSIDs:

  • WPA3-Enterprise where clients support it — provides forward secrecy and protected management frames
  • WPA2-Enterprise as the minimum baseline for corporate SSIDs — 802.1X authentication
  • EAP method — EAP-TLS (client certificate) is the strongest; PEAP-MSCHAPv2 acceptable as fallback where certificates are not yet deployed to all clients
  • RADIUS configuration — primary and secondary RADIUS servers; RADIUS shared secrets strong and stored in vault
  • RADIUS server certificate — signed by the organisation's CA, never self-signed; clients pinned to the issuing CA
  • Client certificate validation — for PEAP, clients are configured to validate the RADIUS server certificate (often missed; if clients accept any cert, the entire EAP defence collapses to a relay attack)
Verify. Authenticate a known-good client; confirm RADIUS authentication, certificate validation occurred, and the client is on the correct VLAN. Capture the EAP handshake; confirm the server certificate chain is valid.
CIS Wireless: "WPA2/3-Enterprise"
3
Protected Management Frames (802.11w PMF)
Protect deauthentication and disassociation frames from forgery.

Configure PMF per SSID:

  • WPA3 SSIDs — PMF required (this is mandatory in the WPA3 spec)
  • WPA2 SSIDs — PMF enabled (capable mode where mixed client support, required where all clients support it)
  • PMF protects against deauthentication attacks that would otherwise force client roaming or disconnect
  • Verify client support: most modern clients (Windows 10+, macOS 10.13+, recent iOS/Android) support PMF in capable mode; older clients may need replacement or strict-isolation
Verify. Show SSID configuration; confirm PMF state. Use a wireless analyser to inject a deauthentication frame; on a PMF-protected SSID, the client should ignore it.
CIS Wireless: "PMF / 802.11w"
4
Guest network isolation
Guest devices reach the internet only, with no access to corporate resources or each other.

Configure guest SSIDs:

  • Separate VLAN — guest traffic on a dedicated VLAN with no L3 path to corporate VLANs
  • Firewall-enforced egress — guest traffic egresses via the firewall, with rules permitting internet (TCP/80, 443, DNS to internet resolvers, NTP) and blocking everything else including corporate subnets, RFC1918 lateral, and private DNS
  • Client isolation enabled on the guest SSID (where vendor supports) — guest clients cannot reach each other over wireless
  • Captive portal / sponsor portal — users acknowledge AUP, optionally identified via sponsor or short-rotating PSK
  • Session limits — reasonable session lifetime (typically 24 hours) requiring re-authentication; bandwidth limits per user where the platform supports them
  • Logging — guest authentication and DHCP events logged centrally so abuse can be investigated
Verify. Connect a guest device; confirm captive portal flow. From the guest device, attempt to reach a corporate server — should fail. Attempt to reach another guest device — should fail. Reach the internet — should succeed.
CIS Wireless: "guest segregation"
5
Rogue AP detection and containment
Identify and respond to unauthorised access points in the RF environment.

Configure rogue-AP capabilities:

  • Enable rogue detection on the wireless controller / cloud-management dashboard
  • Configure the legitimate AP list (or use automatic learning of trusted APs by SSID and BSSID)
  • Classify rogues: friendly (neighbour businesses, public hotspots) versus malicious (broadcasting corporate SSIDs they shouldn't, or in physical proximity)
  • Alert on rogues impersonating corporate SSIDs — this is an evil-twin attack indicator
  • Where controllers support active containment (de-auth flooding a rogue), evaluate the legal and operational implications before enabling — some jurisdictions consider active containment problematic
  • Document an investigation procedure: rogue detected -> classify -> physical investigation if internal -> takedown or report
Verify. Show rogue AP list; confirm classification is sensible. Briefly broadcast a test SSID from a lab device on-site (controlled); confirm it is detected as a rogue.
CIS Wireless: "rogue AP detection"
6
WIDS / WIPS where licensed
Detect known wireless attacks beyond simple rogue-AP presence.

Where the wireless platform includes WIDS/WIPS capability:

  • Enable detection of: deauthentication flood attacks, EAPOL flood, association/authentication anomalies, KARMA-style probe-response attacks, fake-BSSID floods
  • Forward WIDS/WIPS events to the central SIEM
  • Tune to suppress false positives (busy environments, neighbouring legitimate networks)
  • Establish response procedures: critical detections trigger investigation; informational detections feed periodic review
Verify. Confirm WIDS/WIPS events stream to SIEM. Trigger a known-good detection (lab deauthentication flood); confirm the event appears.
CIS Wireless: "wireless intrusion detection"
7
RF management and channel planning
Match RF deployment to a documented design, not vendor defaults.

RF configuration:

  • Region / regulatory domain set correctly per site — non-negotiable; wrong region selection can be a legal issue
  • Transmit power per site survey, not "maximum everywhere" (over-power creates co-channel interference, encourages clients to roam less)
  • Channel plan — non-overlapping channels on 2.4 GHz (1/6/11 only); 5 GHz channels chosen with DFS where the regulatory domain permits
  • Band steering — encourage dual-band clients to 5 GHz; consider 6 GHz for Wi-Fi 6E capable clients in supported regions
  • Disable unused radios on dual/tri-band APs where bands are not in use (rare; usually all bands are desired)
Verify. Show AP configuration and radio status; confirm region, channel, power match design. Post-deployment survey confirms RF coverage matches targets without over-coverage bleeding into adjacent floors/buildings.
CIS Wireless: "RF management"
8
SSID hygiene
Limit the SSID footprint to what's needed; broadcast for security, not obscurity.

SSID hygiene rules:

  • Minimise active SSIDs per AP — each SSID consumes airtime; 3-4 maximum is typical (corporate, guest, optional separate IoT)
  • Disable SSIDs not in use on specific APs — not every SSID needs to broadcast in every location
  • SSID hidden / broadcast disabled is not a security control — clients still leak the SSID, and obscurity slows neither attackers nor accidental connections. Leave broadcast enabled; rely on authentication for security.
  • SSID naming should not reveal organisation-sensitive information (e.g. "AKDN-Finance" reveals more than "AKDN-Staff")
  • Retire SSIDs from old projects, mergers, or migrations — SSID sprawl is a real source of confusion and attack surface
Verify. Show SSID list per AP; confirm only intended SSIDs broadcast in each location. Audit SSID names for sensitive information leakage.
CIS Wireless: "SSID hygiene"
9
802.1X dynamic VLAN assignment (where used)
VLAN per user/group based on identity, not SSID.

Where dynamic VLAN assignment is in use:

  • RADIUS returns the VLAN attribute (IETF VSAs: Tunnel-Type, Tunnel-Medium-Type, Tunnel-Private-Group-ID)
  • VLANs that can be returned are documented in the RADIUS policy
  • Default VLAN (where no attribute is returned) is the least-privileged (e.g. quarantine or guest)
  • Authorisation failures route to a clearly-named "denied" VLAN, not "default"
Verify. Authenticate users from different groups; confirm each lands in the expected VLAN per the RADIUS policy.
CIS Wireless: "dynamic VLAN assignment"
10
Logging and metrics
Continuous visibility into authentication, association, and anomaly events.

Wireless-specific logging:

  • Authentication failures (high false-negative rates can indicate misconfigured client or brute force; high false-accept rates indicate weak PSK)
  • Association/disassociation events with reason codes
  • Rogue-AP detections
  • WIDS/WIPS triggered events
  • RF anomalies (channel utilisation, interference) feeding into ops dashboards, not just security feeds
  • All forwarded to SIEM per IT-NET-WI-01 step 7
Verify. Trigger a known auth failure; confirm event in SIEM. Confirm authentication, association, and security events are flowing without rate-loss.
CIS Wireless: "logging"
Validation & verification

After applying all steps, validate the wireless deployment.

Functional validation

  • Corporate clients authenticate via 802.1X with EAP-TLS or validated PEAP
  • Guest clients authenticate via the captive portal or sponsor flow
  • Guest clients cannot reach corporate resources or each other
  • WEP, WPA-TKIP, open authentication for corporate are not advertised on any SSID
  • PMF is active for WPA3 and enabled-capable for WPA2 corporate
  • Rogue AP detection alerts when a test SSID is broadcast on-site
  • Authentication and security events flow to SIEM

Rollback & troubleshooting

Wireless changes affect every connected user. Rollback approaches:

Common scenarios

  • Mass authentication failure after WPA3 migration — cause: clients don't support WPA3, fall back to WPA2 not configured. Fix: enable WPA2/WPA3 transition mode (advertises both) until client base migrates; document the transition end-date.
  • EAP-TLS fails for some clients — cause: client certificate not yet deployed, or root CA not trusted by client. Fix: identify the affected user/device, verify certificate deployment via MDM, fix per-device or per-group.
  • Guest captive portal not loading — cause: DNS or HTTP intercept rule failure, or certificate issue. Fix: from the guest network, run nslookup and curl tests; verify intercept; check captive-portal certificate is valid.
  • Some clients drop after PMF enabled — cause: older clients don't support PMF and can't associate in "required" mode. Fix: switch to "capable" (optional) mode until client base is current; document the upgrade timeline.
  • Rogue containment causes legal/operational concerns — cause: containment de-auths a neighbouring legitimate network. Fix: disable containment; rely on detection only; pursue physical investigation of rogues.
  • RF coverage gaps after power-down — cause: too aggressive transmit-power reduction relative to actual AP density. Fix: ensure coverage overlap is sufficient before reducing power, particularly on the floor edges and stairwells.

Rollback procedure

  1. Identify which change caused which symptom
  2. If broadly affecting users, revert via the controller / cloud portal (no OOB issue here usually — controllers are reachable from management network)
  3. For 802.1X changes that affect many users, sometimes the right answer is to add a temporary fallback SSID with WPA2-Personal for emergency access while resolving
  4. Update change record; post-incident review
Common errors & tips
  • PEAP without client certificate validation is barely better than WEP. If clients accept any RADIUS-server certificate, an attacker can stand up a rogue RADIUS, harvest credentials. The MDM / GPO configuration to validate certs is mandatory, not optional.
  • EAP-TLS deployment is a project, not a step. Certificate provisioning to every user/device takes planning, MDM integration, and a fallback for non-MDM-managed devices. Plan accordingly.
  • WPA3-Enterprise transition mode is your friend during migration. "WPA2/WPA3 mixed" allows old and new clients to coexist; flip to WPA3-only once you confirm the client base supports it.
  • Guest captive-portal HTTPS interception breaks modern apps. Mobile apps that don't trust intercepted certs will fail; users may rage. Plan for a guest user experience that doesn't depend on captive-portal in-app behaviour.
  • Don't disable SSID broadcast as a "security" measure. Clients will probe for the hidden SSID everywhere they go, leaking the SSID to anyone who listens.
  • Rogue containment is operationally and legally fraught. Default is detection only; containment requires legal review.
  • RF survey age matters. A 5-year-old survey doesn't reflect today's client density, building changes, or 6 GHz availability. Resurvey when changes accumulate.
  • RADIUS shared secrets are credentials. Vault them, rotate them, treat them as secrets — don't email them.
