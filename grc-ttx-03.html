GRC-TTX-03 — Regional Disaster: Sustained Site Evacuation

Tabletop scenario: a severe weather event triggers a multi-day evacuation of a major operating site, with secondary impacts on transport, communications, and staff availability across a wider region. Tests BCP at scale — the kind of disruption multi-country development organisations experience periodically. Executive lean — CMT and senior leadership primary; technical teams in support roles.

Primary documents tested
BCP plans (multi-process), site continuity, regional escalation

For organisations operating across multiple countries with significant physical presence, regional disasters are the most common cause of major continuity invocations — far more common than cyber events. This scenario stresses what cyber-focused exercises typically miss: physical site loss, staff-displacement, communication fragmentation when telecoms degrade, parallel impact on suppliers and partners, coordination across geographies, and the days-to-weeks duration that distinguishes a BCDR event from a cyber one.

The scenario deliberately does not specify the hazard type (flood, earthquake, severe storm) — the response pattern is similar across natural disasters and the agency’s real exposure varies by country.

Objectives
  • Test BCP invocation across multiple business processes simultaneously — not a single-process exercise
  • Test the agency’s alternative-site arrangements: are they current, accessible under disruption, and able to absorb displaced operations?
  • Test communications when normal channels are degraded (telecoms impact common in natural disasters); the fallback channel and its reliability
  • Test cross-agency coordination — multiple member operating units often operate in the same country and a regional disaster affects them in parallel
  • Test the multi-day cadence of CMT operation — cyber-style intense first-24-hours response is not the right pattern for a multi-week BCDR event

Ground rules
  • No-fault learning environment. Findings are about plans, coordination, decision-making.
  • Participants respond as themselves in their real role.
  • Participants may reference real plans, contact lists, alternative-site agreements. If material is hard to find or out of date, that is a finding.
  • The scenario assumes a hazard already occurred — this exercise is not about hazard prediction or pre-event preparedness, it is about operating under sustained disruption.
  • Beneficiary impact may be discussed candidly but with respect for confidentiality of real situations the discussion may evoke.
Starting conditions (T+0)
Day & time
Monday, 07:00 local time
Setting
Regional event has occurred overnight
Initial impact
Main site evacuated; staff dispersed

Initial situation: a severe natural hazard event (flooding / earthquake / severe storm — facilitator chooses based on agency context) occurred overnight, affecting a regional centre where the agency has its largest operating site. By Monday 07:00 the situation is: the main site has been evacuated under government instruction; the building is intact but inaccessible due to road conditions and a precautionary cordon; staff are dispersed; mobile networks are operating at reduced capacity; landlines at the site are unreachable; power supply to the area is intermittent; the airport in the regional capital is closed.

What participants know at T+0:

  • Main site inaccessible; physical structure not damaged
  • Staff have not been able to confirm a head-count over normal channels
  • Mobile networks degraded; SMS is intermittent; some areas have no signal
  • Government has declared a regional emergency; the duration is unknown but at least 5-7 days expected
  • Other member operating units are present in the same region and may be coordinating in parallel
Injects
T+30 min First CMT call attempted; partial attendance

The CMT Lead attempts to convene the Crisis Management Team via the documented call. Two CMT members are reachable; three are not (one is in the cordoned area; one is on home leave abroad; one’s mobile has no signal). The CMT Lead asks: do we have the quorum to make decisions? Who has authority to act for the unreachable members?

Discussion prompts
  • Does the BCP plan define quorum for CMT decisions? Does it name deputies for each member?
  • Does the communication fallback work in this scenario — is SMS reliable enough? Is there a satellite / radio option for the highest-priority comms?
  • How does the CMT confirm staff welfare when normal channels are degraded? Is there a pre-arranged check-in procedure?
T+90 min Critical processes assessed; immediate invocations needed

BPOs of the impacted processes report status. Clinical services need to keep operating — patients depending on them are also dispersed and likely in greater need than usual. Educational services are postponed for the duration. Support functions (HR, finance) can operate remotely with reduced capability. The senior CEO asks: what are we invoking, in what order, and who is leading each invocation?

Discussion prompts
  • What is the agency’s priority order across processes when multiple are impacted simultaneously? Is it documented?
  • For clinical services: where do they operate from now? Is the alternative-site arrangement current and accessible under the regional disruption?
  • For staff working remotely on support functions: do they have what they need (devices, connectivity, access)? Was remote work pre-provisioned or is it being arranged ad hoc?
  • The educational postponement — is the comms to families/students ready, or being drafted now?
T+4 hours (Day 1 afternoon) Donor and partner enquiries; press contact

The Communications team has fielded 6 enquiries: 3 from donor organisations asking about operational continuity and beneficiary safety; 2 from partner organisations asking about coordination; 1 from international press doing a regional disaster story. The donors are particularly concerned because some have grant agreements with continuity clauses. Comms Lead asks the CEO: what is our position, and what channels do we use to communicate it?

Discussion prompts
  • Is there a pre-prepared donor-communications template for major disruptions? If not, can one be drafted within 24 hours?
  • How does the agency talk to other member operating units operating in the same region? Is there a coordinating role? Is it acting?
  • The press contact — what is the agency’s position on engagement (proactive, reactive, decline)? Who approves?
  • Is there a single source of truth for status updates that internal and external audiences both reference?
T+24 hours (Day 2 morning) Duration extends; staff fatigue and dependent-care issues

By Day 2 morning, government has extended the emergency to at least 14 days. Some staff are starting to report dependent-care issues — childcare arrangements disrupted, elderly relatives needing support — affecting their availability. Several key roles have unsustainable cover. The HR Lead raises: the CMT decisions are sustainable for a few days but not for two weeks; we need a different operating model from Day 5 onwards.

Discussion prompts
  • How does the CMT shift from intense initial-response to sustainable multi-week operation? Is the model documented?
  • Cross-training and succession for key roles — how is it tracked? Is it actually current?
  • What support is available to staff for the dependent-care issues? Is there a documented welfare-support model?
  • For roles where cover is unsustainable, what are the options — reduced service, external surge support, accept-degradation? Where does that authority sit?
T+5 days Inter-agency coordination escalation

By Day 5, central coordination team has convened a regional response coordination. The agency CEO is invited to join. The coordination is asking: what is each agency’s priority, what is each agency’s spare capacity, can resources be shared across agencies for the duration. The CEO asks the CMT: what is our position?

Discussion prompts
  • Has the agency thought about cross-agency support before this event? Is there a framework or is it improvisation?
  • What spare capacity does the agency have, realistically? Is there a documented view?
  • What would the agency ask for from other member operating units? Is there a documented list of what would be useful?
  • Who at the agency has authority to commit to mutual-support arrangements?
T+10 days Recovery and return-to-normal planning

Government indicates the emergency will end within the week. The agency must plan: return to the main site (cleaning, re-inspection, IT validation), backlog management, reconciliation of records made under BCP workarounds with the normal systems, staff debriefing. The CMT discusses recovery sequencing.

Discussion prompts
  • Is there a documented return-to-normal procedure, or is recovery improvised?
  • Records made under workarounds — who reconciles them, and against what quality bar?
  • Staff who worked under sustained disruption — what support is offered? Is there a documented welfare-debrief framework?
  • What is the trigger for stand-down of the CMT and BCP? Who makes that call?
  • AAR timing — when does it happen, and who is included?

Evaluation criteria
  • Objective 1 (multi-process invocation): all impacted processes invoked within 4 hours; priority order documented or established explicitly
  • Objective 2 (alternative-site arrangements): known, current, accessible under the disruption pattern of the scenario
  • Objective 3 (communications under degraded conditions): fallback channels work; CMT can communicate; external audiences receive consistent messages
  • Objective 4 (cross-agency coordination): agency can articulate its position, capacity, and asks
  • Objective 5 (multi-day cadence): CMT operating model adjusts to sustainable pace by Day 3-5; staff welfare addressed
  • Cross-cutting: documented authority and named deputies allow the response to continue when individual people are unreachable
After-action review framework

Conducted within 30 days. Distribution: Country / Regional CEO, BCDR Steering Committee, BCP Coordinator, all BPOs of impacted processes, HR Lead, Comms Lead, inter-agency liaison.

Common findings: CMT quorum and deputies under-specified; fallback comms never tested under degraded conditions; alternative-site agreements out of date or untested; donor-comms templates absent for major disruption; cross-agency coordination improvised; multi-day cadence shift not in any document; return-to-normal procedures not specified.
