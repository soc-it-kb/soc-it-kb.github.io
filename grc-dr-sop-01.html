GRC-DR-SOP-01 — IT Disaster Recovery Policy

The organisation’s IT-audience policy for technology service recovery. Defines IT obligations, recovery tier framework, backup strategy minima, replication patterns, runbook requirements, testing cadence, and cloud platform DR principles. Uses the methodology in GRC-BCDR-SOP-01; companion to the Business Continuity Policy (GRC-BCP-SOP-01).

IT-audience policy Recovery tiers 1–4 Cloud DR first-class
1 — Purpose

This policy establishes the organisation’s commitment to recovering IT services in support of business continuity, and the IT obligations that follow. It defines the recovery tier framework that every in-scope IT service is classified against, the minimum backup and replication standards per tier, and the runbook, testing, and governance requirements.

It is a policy document. It states what IT must do to provide the recovery capabilities that BCP relies on. Methodology — how tier classification works, how RTO/RPO flow from BIA, how exercises are run — is in GRC-BCDR-SOP-01. Operational procedures are in the DR WIs.

2 — Scope & applicability

This policy applies to all IT services within the organisation that support business processes identified as critical by BIA (Mission-critical, Business-critical, or Important tiers), and to the IT teams operating them.

In scope:

  • All IT services with at least one dependent business process at Tier-1, Tier-2, or Tier-3 criticality.
  • Cloud-resident services (M365, Azure, in-scope SaaS) as well as on-premises and hybrid services.
  • Identity and authentication services (which underpin nearly every dependent service).
  • Network, infrastructure, and security platform services (which are dependencies of dependent services).

Out of scope:

  • IT services supporting only non-critical (Standard) business processes — resilience is expected but no specific runbook is required.
  • Application-internal recovery procedures (which live in application-owner documentation; DR governs that they exist and are exercised).
  • Business-process workarounds (which are BCP’s remit).
  • Cyber incident response (which is IRP’s remit). DR addresses recovery to operations.
3 — Policy statements
  1. Every in-scope IT service shall have an assigned recovery tier derived from the BIA of dependent business processes per the methodology in GRC-BCDR-SOP-01. Where a service supports multiple processes at different tiers, the highest tier applies.
  2. Every in-scope IT service shall meet the minimum backup, replication, and resilience standards for its assigned tier as defined in GRC-DR-WI-01. Where a service cannot meet the standards, the gap is recorded as an exception or risk.
  3. Every Tier-1 and Tier-2 service shall have a current DR runbook populated using the template (GRC-FRM-DR-01), reviewed at least every 12 months, accessible to operators during disruption (including offline / out-of-band copies for Tier-1), and updated immediately on material change.
  4. Tier-3 services shall have a documented recovery approach (not necessarily a full runbook) describing backup sources, recovery sequence, and validation steps.
  5. Tier-4 services require a documented backup approach only — runbook is not required, recovery on a best-effort basis.
  6. Backup success is monitored against tier-specific SLAs. Failed backups for Tier-1 and Tier-2 services generate alerts within 4 hours of detection; sustained backup failure is escalated to the IT Service Owner and IT DR Coordinator.
  7. Recovery exercises are mandatory. Each Tier-1 service is exercised at least once every 12 months; each Tier-2 service at least every 24 months. Exercise type per GRC-BCDR-WI-02.
  8. Cloud platform DR scenarios are first-class and addressed by dedicated procedures in GRC-DR-WI-02: Azure region failure, M365 sustained outage, Entra tenant lockout, tenant compromise.
  9. Break-glass and emergency access provisions are tested quarterly for tenant-level scenarios. Stale break-glass accounts (no successful validation within the last 90 days) are treated as a Tier-0 risk.
  10. Backups are immutable or sufficiently isolated to survive a destructive cyber event. Tier-1 and Tier-2 backups for systems supporting Mission-critical processes shall include immutable copies or air-gapped / logically-isolated copies retained for at least 14 days.
  11. Recovery shall not introduce new vulnerabilities. Recovered systems are validated against the relevant security baseline before being returned to production use; recovered identities are not granted higher standing access than they held before the disruption.
  12. Findings from exercises and invocations are tracked to closure. Overdue findings escalate to the BCDR Steering Committee.
  13. Recovery tier assignments are reviewed annually by IT Service Owners and the IT DR Coordinator. BIA refresh triggers an out-of-cycle review.

5 — Backup & replication principles
  • 3-2-1 baseline for Tier-1 and Tier-2. Three copies of data, on two media types, with one off-site. Cloud-resident services satisfy this through cross-region geo-redundant storage where designed; on-premises services require an off-site copy.
  • Immutability for cyber resilience. Backup repositories supporting Tier-1 and Tier-2 services that face cyber risk shall have immutable or air-gapped copies with retention of at least 14 days. This protects against ransomware that targets backup infrastructure.
  • Restore tests, not backup tests. A successful backup is necessary but not sufficient. Restore tests — full or sample — are required on the cadence in GRC-DR-WI-01: Tier-1 quarterly, Tier-2 quarterly, Tier-3 semi-annually, Tier-4 annually.
  • Replication is for RTO, backup is for RPO. Replication reduces recovery time (warm or hot secondary); backup ensures point-in-time recoverability including against logical corruption. Both are needed for Tier-1; Tier-2 typically uses asynchronous replication; Tier-3 may rely on backup alone.
  • Cloud-native protection where appropriate. M365 retention, Azure Backup, Azure Site Recovery, geo-redundant storage are first-class mechanisms; the choice is per-service per the design patterns in IT-CL-WI-03 and IT-CL-WI-04.
  • Third-party SaaS responsibility is shared. SaaS data is the organisation’s responsibility regardless of provider availability commitments. Tier-1 SaaS systems shall have an independent backup or export mechanism if the provider does not offer point-in-time recovery within RPO.
  • Encryption at rest and in transit applies to all backup and replication data and is configured per the relevant security baseline.
6 — Cloud platform DR principles

Detailed scenarios and recovery paths are in GRC-DR-WI-02. The policy principles:

  • Paired-region by default for Tier-1 cloud workloads. Azure paired-region failover is the baseline; geo-redundant storage is the data baseline. Design patterns in IT-CL-WI-04 govern implementation.
  • Tenant lockout is a designed scenario. Break-glass accounts, emergency access procedures, and out-of-band administrative access are tested quarterly. Reliance on the tenant being available to recover the tenant is not acceptable.
  • Tenant compromise has a recovery path. The capability to operate from a clean tenant in the event of suspected tenant-level compromise is documented and tested. Full migration is a multi-week effort; the path is documented even if speed-of-execution is constrained.
  • M365 sustained outage has communication fallbacks. Critical communications during a sustained M365 outage do not rely on M365. Alternative channels are documented and tested.
  • SaaS provider failure is in scope. Tier-1 dependence on a single SaaS provider without an alternative-access pattern is a documented risk.
  • Cloud DR is not a substitute for backup. Cross-region replication and high-availability do not protect against logical corruption, ransomware on cloud-resident data, or accidental destruction. Backup remains required.

8 — Exceptions & non-compliance

Where conformance with this policy is genuinely not feasible — e.g. a legacy application that cannot be replicated at Tier-1, a SaaS provider that does not offer point-in-time recovery within RPO, a hybrid system in active migration — an exception is recorded.

Exception requirements are the same as BCP: documented justification, named owner, time-bounded approval (default 12 months), compensating measures, recorded in the GRC Exception Register. Persistent or material non-compliance escalates to a risk register entry.
