IT-ID-SOP-01: Identity Governance & Lifecycle

The authoritative policy-level baseline for identity governance across hybrid Entra ID and on-premises Active Directory environments. Covers human identities (joiners, movers, leavers, recertification) and non-human identities (service accounts, service principals, managed identities, certificates). Operationalised by IT-ID-WI-01 through IT-ID-WI-05.

Section 1
Purpose

This SOP defines the minimum requirements for identity governance and lifecycle management across the institution and its agencies. It covers two distinct identity classes — human (employees, contractors, students, alumni, guests, external collaborators) and non-human (service accounts, Entra service principals, managed identities, certificate-based identities, workload identities) — across a hybrid environment of on-premises Active Directory and Entra ID.

This SOP sits above the technical identity platform configuration. The configuration of Conditional Access policies, MFA enforcement, PIM activation rules, named locations, and break-glass accounts is covered by IT-CL-WI-01 — the how of tenant identity. This SOP covers the who, why, for how long, and reviewed by whom — the policy and lifecycle layer that makes the technical platform actually govern access rather than just enable it.

This SOP is operationalised by five Work Instructions: IT-ID-WI-01 (Joiner / Mover / Leaver Lifecycle), IT-ID-WI-02 (Access Recertification & Attestation), IT-ID-WI-03 (Role Design & Entitlement Management), IT-ID-WI-04 (Non-Human Identity Governance), and IT-ID-WI-05 (Privileged Identity Governance). See §4 for the requirements summary and the linked WIs for implementation detail.

Section 2
Scope

2.1 In scope

  • Human identities — every identity associated with a natural person who interacts with organisational systems, regardless of employment status (employee, contractor, intern, student, alumni, guest, external collaborator) and regardless of which identity platform hosts the account (on-premises AD, Entra ID synced from AD, cloud-only Entra ID).
  • Non-human identities — service accounts in AD (user accounts, gMSA, sMSA), Entra service principals, app registrations, system-assigned and user-assigned managed identities in Azure, workload identity federation, certificate-based identities used for service authentication.
  • Hybrid scope — identities that exist in both AD and Entra ID via synchronisation (Entra Connect), identities only in Entra (cloud-only), and identities only in AD (on-prem-only). The governance is unified; the technical mechanics differ.
  • Privileged identity governance — the eligibility layer above PIM activation: who is eligible for which privileged role, why, for how long, reviewed by whom. This is distinct from the activation mechanism itself.
  • All operating agencies of the institution where identity systems are in use.

2.2 Out of scope

  • Tenant-level identity platform configuration — Conditional Access policies, MFA enforcement, PIM activation rules, named locations, break-glass accounts, Identity Protection risk policies, SSPR — covered by IT-CL-WI-01 (Tenant Identity & Conditional Access).
  • PAM platform mechanics — credential vaulting, session recording, password rotation — covered by IT-SI-WI-04 (PAM Platform Hardening).
  • PAW build and lifecycle — covered by IT-EP-SOP-PAW-01 and the PAW Work Instructions.
  • Tier-0 AD administrative access enforcement — the technical implementation of tier separation — covered by IT-SRV-WI-DC-01.
  • Identity incident response — account compromise triage, sign-in investigation, OAuth abuse response, MFA bypass response — covered by SOC SOPs (SOP-ID-01) and the ID-PB / ID-RB content.
  • HR system internal processes — how HR captures joiner data, validates contractor onboarding, executes termination — is HR's responsibility. The interface between HR and identity systems is in scope; the HR processes themselves are not.
  • Application-internal authorisation — once an identity exists and has been granted access to a system, the system's internal role / permission model is the system owner's responsibility, not this SOP's.
Section 3
Governance Principles

The seven principles below shape every requirement. Where an implementation choice arises, resolve it consistently with these principles.

3.1 Identity flows from an authoritative source

Every human identity in the estate originates from a single authoritative source for that identity class: HR system for employees and contractors; Student Information System for students; sponsor-driven for guests and external collaborators. There are no "shadow" identity creation paths — accounts created outside the authoritative flow are exceptions, time-bounded, and tracked.

3.2 Least privilege by default

New identities start with the minimum access required to perform their role. Additional access is added by request, justified, and recorded. The opposite pattern — clone the previous starter's access — is forbidden because it permanently elevates the baseline as access accumulates across joiners.

3.3 Standing privilege is time-bounded

No human account holds permanent privileged role assignment except documented break-glass accounts (see IT-CL-WI-01 Step 1). Privileged access is held as eligibility (the right to activate when needed), not as standing assignment. The eligibility itself has a defined duration; on expiry, the user reapplies through the access request workflow.

3.4 Recertification is a control, not a chore

Access that is not periodically re-justified is access that should not exist. Cadence is per access tier — quarterly for Tier-0, semi-annual for Tier-1, annual for Tier-2/3. Reviewers are named by role (line manager, system owner, privileged access authority) and accountable for the review they perform. Rubber-stamping is a finding.

3.5 Every identity has a named owner

Human identities are owned by the named individual and their line manager. Non-human identities (service accounts, service principals, managed identities) are owned by a named human or named team — never by "IT" generically. Identities without owners are orphaned identities, which are a high-priority remediation category.

3.6 Non-human identities are first-class

The estate's service accounts, service principals, and managed identities outnumber its human accounts. The governance applied to them is equivalent in rigour to that applied to humans: named owner, justified scope, credential rotation discipline, periodic review, planned decommissioning. The principle "service accounts are someone else's problem" is the principle this SOP rejects.

3.7 Governance is separate from incident response

This SOP and its WIs cover governance — how identities are created, modified, reviewed, retired in steady state. Identity-related incident response — what to do when an account is compromised, when MFA fatigue occurs, when an OAuth grant is malicious — is covered by SOC SOPs (SOP-ID-01) and ID-PB / ID-RB playbook content. The boundary is deliberately maintained so neither team duplicates or misses work, and so that governance findings (e.g. orphaned account discovered) feed the incident-response side cleanly.

Section 4
Minimum Requirements

The requirements below are mandatory across the in-scope identity surface. Each requirement domain is operationalised by a dedicated Work Instruction (linked at the right of each domain header).

🔄

4.1 Joiner / Mover / Leaver Lifecycle

IT-ID-WI-01 →
  • Joiner: account provisioning triggered by authoritative source event (HR hire, SIS enrolment, sponsor invitation); not by request ticket
  • Joiner: account active no earlier than start date; provisioning within 1 business day of start date
  • Joiner: minimum required access at start — role-appropriate base bundle from the role catalogue (IT-ID-WI-03); additional access by separate request
  • Joiner: clone-previous-user pattern explicitly forbidden — every starter gets a clean baseline
  • Mover: access changes triggered by authoritative source role change; previous role access revoked at the time of grant, not later ("revoke-then-grant", not "grant-then-revoke")
  • Mover: temporary dual-role periods bounded to ≤30 days with explicit approval; longer periods documented as exceptions
  • Leaver: account disabled same business day as termination (immediate for involuntary departures)
  • Leaver: account retained in disabled state for 30–90 days for forensics and handover access; deleted thereafter unless legal hold applies
  • Leaver: privileged access revoked at the same moment as account disable — never relies on account-disable cascade
  • Leaver: forwarding and delegations reviewed (managed in IT-CL-WI-02 territory, but cross-checked here for completeness)
  • Student and alumni transitions: bulk lifecycle events per academic calendar; access bundle transitions documented (e.g. graduating student → alumni access bundle)
  • Guest: invitation by named sponsor; time-bounded to ≤180 days; renewal requires justification and re-sponsorship
  • Identity-source data quality: monthly reconciliation between authoritative source and identity platform; orphaned accounts and orphaned source records both flagged
📋

4.2 Access Recertification & Attestation

IT-ID-WI-02 →
  • Recertification cadence per access tier: Tier-0 quarterly; Tier-1 semi-annual; Tier-2/3 annual
  • Privileged role eligibility recertified quarterly regardless of resource tier
  • Guest / external user access recertified quarterly
  • Named reviewer per access scope: line manager (direct reports), system owner (system-specific roles), Privileged Access Authority (Tier-0/1 privileged eligibility), team manager (service accounts and service principals owned by their team)
  • Review actions explicit: certify (continue access), revoke (remove access), modify (adjust scope) — "ignored" or "no response" defaults to revoke on the relevant tier
  • Evidence retention: reviewer identity, decision, justification (for certify-with-comment), date — retained 3 years minimum or per regulatory requirement
  • Automated where possible: Entra Identity Governance access reviews (where licensed, typically E5); manual workflow where not
  • Bulk-attest forbidden — reviewers must engage with each item individually for Tier-0 and Tier-1; Tier-2/3 may use sampling-based attestation with documented sample size
  • Non-attestation of Tier-0 / Tier-1 reviews escalates to the role's eligibility being suspended (not revoked) until attested
  • Annual full-population recertification for all human accounts regardless of access tier (catches any tier-misclassification)
🎭

4.3 Role Design & Entitlement Management

IT-ID-WI-03 →
  • Role catalogue maintained: every assignable role / group has a documented owner, defined scope, justification, last-review date
  • Role design follows least-privilege — roles aggregate the minimum permissions for a defined job function, not the maximum
  • Standard role naming convention applied: domain prefix, scope descriptor, role descriptor (e.g. azure-vm-operations-contributor-prod)
  • Group hygiene: maximum nesting depth 3 levels; circular references prohibited; empty groups removed after 30 days; group ownership documented
  • Entitlement packages (Entra Identity Governance, where licensed) for common access bundles — joiner pack, role-change packs, project-team packs — reduces ad-hoc requests
  • Separation of duties enforced for sensitive role combinations: no single user holds combinations like "create payment + approve payment", "modify firewall rule + delete firewall log"
  • Role-change requests routed through documented workflow with system-owner approval; ad-hoc changes (e.g. direct group membership change without ticket) trigger an alert and a backfill ticket
  • Quarterly role catalogue review: roles unused for 12 months flagged for retirement; over-broad roles flagged for splitting
  • Tier-0 / Tier-1 role assignments visible in Entra PIM (or equivalent for AD) and reviewed against the role catalogue monthly for drift
🔧

4.4 Non-Human Identity Governance

IT-ID-WI-04 →
  • Inventory: every service account, gMSA, Entra service principal, app registration, system-assigned managed identity, user-assigned managed identity, and certificate-based service identity has an inventory record
  • Named owner per non-human identity — named human or named team, not "IT" or "DevOps" generically; owner reviewed annually
  • Scope justification documented per non-human identity: what permissions, why, when granted, by whom — captured at creation, not retrospectively
  • Credential rotation: AD service account passwords rotated annually minimum (60-day target where the service supports it); gMSA passwords managed automatically; service principal secrets max 1-year expiry, rotated before expiry; certificates rotated per certificate-lifecycle policy
  • Managed identities preferred over service principals in Azure — no secret to manage; lifecycle tied to the resource
  • Secret expiry tracking: dashboard of upcoming credential expiries; owner notified ≥30 days before expiry; SOC alerted on expired-but-still-used credentials
  • OAuth scope review (Entra service principals): permissions reviewed quarterly; over-permissive scopes reduced; consent grant audit-trailed
  • Decommissioning: non-human identity unused for 90 days flagged; 180 days disabled; 365 days deleted (with named-owner approval at each step)
  • Service accounts with interactive sign-in capability flagged for review — service accounts should not be sign-in-capable in modern designs
  • Workload identity federation preferred over long-lived service principal secrets where the workload pattern supports it
  • Privileged non-human identities (Tier-0 service accounts, high-scope service principals) follow the same eligibility / activation pattern as privileged human identities where the platform supports it
👑

4.5 Privileged Identity Governance

IT-ID-WI-05 →
  • Privileged role catalogue: every privileged role (Tier-0 and Tier-1) is documented with scope, named individuals currently eligible, eligibility duration, last-review date
  • Eligibility, not assignment — privileged role membership is held as PIM-eligible (cloud) or equivalent (on-prem) with activation required; standing assignments forbidden except documented break-glass accounts (see IT-CL-WI-01)
  • Eligibility duration: maximum 12 months for Tier-0; maximum 18 months for Tier-1; renewal requires re-justification
  • Approval workflow: Tier-0 eligibility requires two-person approval (requesting manager + Privileged Access Authority); Tier-1 may use single approver per role definition
  • Separation of duties: no single individual holds Tier-0 eligibility across functionally separated domains (e.g. cloud identity admin + AD enterprise admin in the same person is a finding unless deliberately approved)
  • Standing-privilege count tracked monthly: target ≤4 (the documented break-glass accounts only); any other standing privilege is an exception
  • Quarterly eligibility review: every eligible assignment re-examined; expired or unjustified eligibility removed; new eligibility requires fresh approval
  • Privileged access request workflow: requestor justifies, approver decides, both events logged; the request itself is the audit trail
  • Tier-0 activation correlated with PAW use (see IT-EP-SOP-PAW-01) — Tier-0 administrative actions from non-PAW devices are alerts
  • Tier-0 administrative activity logged to immutable destination (central SIEM via WI-03/WI-04 of Cloud domain); retention ≥1 year
Boundary with IT-CL-WI-01 §7. The PIM activation mechanism — how a user activates an eligible role, what MFA is required, how long the activation lasts, whether approval is required at activation time — lives in IT-CL-WI-01 Step 7 because it's tenant-platform configuration. This SOP §4.5 governs who is eligible in the first place, and how that eligibility is itself reviewed. Activation = WI-01; eligibility = WI-05.
Section 5
Roles & Responsibilities

Head of IT

Owns this SOP. Approves baseline changes and material exceptions. Accountable to the Risk Committee for identity-related control state. Sets reporting cadence and oversees domain conformance.

Identity & Access Engineering

Implements the WIs. Maintains the role catalogue, JML automation, recertification campaigns. Operates Entra Identity Governance where licensed. Co-owns non-human identity inventory. First-line responder for identity-system operational issues.

HR Information Systems

Owns the authoritative source for human identity attributes. Maintains HR → identity integration. Triggers JML events. Quarterly data-quality review with IAE.

Line Managers

Recertify direct-report access at cadence per tier. Sponsor mover changes. Initiate leaver events when not automated. Accountable for the accuracy of certifications they perform.

System Owners

Recertify access to their systems. Define role / entitlement structure for their application. Named contact for access requests outside the standard catalogue. Maintain their portion of the role catalogue.

Privileged Access Authority

Approves Tier-0 and Tier-1 privileged eligibility. Owns privileged access request workflow. Quarterly eligibility review. Separation-of-duties enforcement. Typically a small named group (CISO + Head of IT + nominee).

Non-Human Identity Owners

Named human or team per service account / service principal / managed identity. Responsible for credential rotation, scope review, decommissioning. Reviewed quarterly.

GRC

Owns governance of the domain. Maintains recertification evidence. Reports access posture to Risk Committee quarterly. Tracks orphaned accounts and standing-privilege exceptions. Reviews baseline annually.

Separation of identity governance and identity incident response. The roles in this SOP own steady-state identity governance. Identity incident response — account compromise, sign-in anomaly, OAuth abuse, MFA bypass — is the SOC's responsibility per SOP-ID-01. Governance findings (e.g. orphaned privileged account discovered during recertification) flow to SOC for investigation; SOC incident findings (e.g. compromised account) flow to IAE for remediation. The handoff in both directions is via the standard ticket / incident workflow.

Section 7
Governance & Review

7.1 Ownership and update authority

This SOP is owned by the Head of IT with co-ownership by the Head of GRC and the Head of Identity & Access Engineering. Material changes (new requirements, scope changes, cadence changes) require sign-off from all three. Minor clarifications and editorial improvements may be applied by the owner with notification to GRC.

7.2 Review cadence

  • Annual review — minimum, on the anniversary of approval, by the owner with GRC sign-off
  • Out-of-cycle review — triggered by: HR system change, Entra licensing change affecting access reviews, major incident involving identity, new identity class (e.g. workload identity federation maturation), audit finding
  • Per-WI review — each Work Instruction is reviewed independently and may update more frequently than the SOP, particularly WI-04 (non-human identities) as the cloud identity landscape evolves

7.3 Metrics & reporting

The metrics below are monitored monthly by IT Operations and reported quarterly to GRC and the Risk Committee:

  • JML SLA conformance — joiner ≤1 day, mover ≤5 days, leaver same-day. Target ≥95% on time.
  • Orphaned account count — target 0 within 30 days of termination event.
  • Recertification completion rate — 100% for Tier-0/1, ≥95% Tier-2/3, within campaign window.
  • Recertification revocation rate — tracked as a trend. Too low → rubber-stamping concern; too high → over-provisioning at grant.
  • Standing privileged access count — target ≤4 (break-glass only).
  • Non-human identity coverage — named owner + last-review ≤12 months. Target 100% by end FY 2026.
  • Service principal secret expiry hygiene — <1 year expiry, rotated on schedule. Target ≥98%.
  • Role catalogue health — documented owner + scope + last-review ≤12 months. Target ≥95%.

7.4 Exception handling

Where a requirement in §4 cannot be met, the operating team follows IT-GOV-SOP-01 (Baseline Exception & Risk Acceptance). Exceptions are time-bounded, recorded in GRC-REG-EXCEPTIONS, linked from the relevant identity-class record, and reviewed annually.

Three exception categories deserve specific treatment:

  • Orphaned service accounts of unknown lineage. Discovery efforts (codepath search, log analysis, controlled disable test) given a defined window; after that, forced decommission with named approver. Stalled discovery is not a permanent exception.
  • Legacy applications that cannot consume modern identity (LDAP-only, certificate-only without rotation). Documented as platform-debt items with remediation roadmaps and compensating controls (network restriction, monitoring intensity).
  • HR-system integration gaps preventing automatic JML for specific identity classes (e.g. contractors not in HR, students in a separate SIS without integration). Manual JML procedures documented as transitional state; integration maturation tracked as a programme item.

7.5 Compliance & audit

Baseline conformance is measured through: JML SLA dashboard, recertification campaign reports with reviewer attestation evidence, standing-privilege report, non-human identity inventory with last-review dates, role catalogue health report, exception register entries. Audit evidence includes named-reviewer attestation records, role-change approval tickets, eligibility approval workflow records, credential rotation logs, decommissioning records. Reports produced monthly to IT Operations and quarterly to GRC and the Risk Committee.
