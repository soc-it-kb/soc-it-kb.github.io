SOP-ID-01: Identity Threat Operations

Standard Operating Procedure defining how the SOC detects, triages, investigates, escalates, and responds to identity-based threats — ensuring consistent protection of user identities, privileged accounts, and authentication mechanisms.

Section 1
Purpose

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines how the Security Operations Centre (SOC) detects, triages, investigates, escalates, and responds to identity-based threats, ensuring consistent protection of user identities, privileged accounts, and authentication mechanisms across the organisation.

Identity is treated as a Tier-0 security asset, and identity compromise is considered a high-impact threat vector with potential for rapid lateral movement, data loss, and business disruption.

Section 2
Scope

Applies to

  • Global SOC (Tier 1–3)
  • Regional SOCs
  • MDR provider
  • Identity & Access Management (IAM) teams
  • Local IT / Cyber teams supporting remediation

In-scope threat categories

  • Suspicious or anomalous login activity
  • Brute-force and password spraying attacks
  • MFA fatigue, bypass, or abuse
  • Privilege escalation and role abuse
  • OAuth / token abuse and consent misuse
  • Confirmed or suspected account compromise

Section 6
Operating Procedure (High-Level)
1
Detection & Alert Intake

Identity threats may be detected through identity provider alerts, SIEM correlations, EDR/XDR identity telemetry, MDR escalations, or user/IT-reported access anomalies. All alerts must be logged in the incident management platform.

2
Initial Triage & Classification

The SOC must determine account type (standard user, privileged, service, executive), authentication method (password, MFA, token, legacy protocol), source characteristics (IP reputation, geo-location, TOR/VPN), and presence of MFA challenges or failures. Classification must follow the applicable ID-PB-XX playbook.

3
Investigation & Analysis

Mandatory investigation steps include: review of authentication logs and timelines, correlation with endpoint and network telemetry, analysis of MFA prompts and approvals, detection of privilege or role changes, and validation of OAuth grants or token usage. Evidence must be preserved for audit and potential IR escalation.

4
Containment & Mitigation

Containment actions may include forcing password resets, revoking active sessions and tokens, disabling or locking accounts, enforcing MFA or stronger authentication, and rolling back unauthorised privilege changes. For high-risk identities, containment takes precedence over user disruption.

5
Escalation & Communication

Escalation is mandatory when: privileged or Tier-0 accounts are involved, MFA bypass or fatigue is confirmed, multiple accounts show correlated activity, or identity compromise leads to lateral movement. Communication must follow SOC escalation and incident communication procedures.

6
Recovery & Lessons Learned

For confirmed identity incidents: validate account security posture, perform access reviews, confirm no persistence mechanisms remain, and update detection logic where gaps are identified. Lessons learned must be recorded for continuous improvement.
