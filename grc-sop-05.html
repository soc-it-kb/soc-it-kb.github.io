Section 1
Purpose, Scope & Authority

1.1 Purpose

This procedure establishes how the Institution identifies, names, classifies, and manages the lifecycle of all IT and OT assets across operating units. It defines the asset taxonomy, the naming convention, the record schema, the lifecycle states, and the workflows for onboarding, review, and decommissioning. It exists to give a single, authoritative answer to the questions an analyst, an engineer, or an auditor most often asks: "what is this asset, who owns it, where does it sit, what depends on it, what is it allowed to do, and is it still ours?"

This SOP is a foundational control. Effective asset management underpins every other security and operational discipline: incident response (blast radius), vulnerability management (which assets are affected), change management (impact analysis), business continuity (what must be restored first), regulatory compliance (where personal data lives), and threat detection (what's normal for this asset).

1.2 Scope

This procedure covers all assets — physical, virtual, cloud-hosted, software-as-a-service, identity stores, data stores, and operational technology — that are operated by or on behalf of the Institution, regardless of medium, hosting model, or operating unit. It applies to all operating units worldwide. Each operating unit must identify, register, and maintain records for the assets within its accountability.

1.3 Authority

This SOP is the authoritative source for asset management and the naming convention across the Institution. It supersedes all earlier local conventions, draft naming standards, and ad-hoc registers. Where existing assets have legacy names that do not conform to this SOP, they are governed under the migration provisions in §11.

1.4 Out of scope

  • Detailed financial accounting (depreciation schedules, capitalisation rules) — these live in the finance system, not this register
  • Software licence management beyond a basic reference field — covered under a separate licence-management procedure
  • Personal devices not used to conduct Institutional business (true BYOD with personal data only)
  • Disposable media (USB sticks, optical media) — covered under data classification and removable-media procedures
  • End-user accounts (individuals) — covered under identity management; only service/system accounts are tracked here as assets

Section 3
Definitions

The terms below are authoritative for this SOP and all asset-management activities.

TermDefinition
AssetAny item of value to the Institution that processes, stores, transmits, or supports the processing of information, or that is used to conduct Institutional business. Assets may be physical (servers, network devices, endpoints), virtual (VMs, containers), logical (applications, services), or relational (identity stores, data stores).
Configuration Item (CI)An asset under formal configuration control. Every CI is an asset; not every asset is a CI. CIs require change management when modified.
Asset RecordThe structured entry in the Asset Register (GRC-REG-ASSET) that describes a single asset and tracks its lifecycle.
Asset Identifier (Asset ID)The primary key of an asset record. For physical and located assets, the Asset ID equals the hostname per the naming convention. For non-host assets, a synthetic Asset ID is used per §7.
Operating UnitAn agency, country office, subsidiary, or other Institutional unit accountable for a defined population of assets. Each asset belongs to exactly one operating unit (or to "Central" for cross-agency centrally-managed assets).
Business OwnerThe role accountable for the business outcome that the asset supports. Decides whether the asset's purpose is still relevant.
Technical OwnerThe role accountable for the technical operation of the asset. Maintains it, patches it, and authorises technical changes.
Security ContactThe named individual or role to contact for security-related matters affecting the asset, including incident response.
System of RecordThe authoritative source for a given attribute. The Asset Register is the system of record for asset identity, ownership, classification, and lifecycle. Other attributes (vulnerabilities, patches, financial data) have their own systems of record; the Register holds references, not authority.
VerificationAn activity to confirm that an asset record is accurate. May be active (a person checks) or automated (a tool reconciles).
ReconciliationThe process of comparing an asset record against an authoritative external source (e.g. Active Directory, Intune, CrowdStrike) and resolving any differences.
Section 4
Roles & Responsibilities

Asset management requires shared accountability across central and local roles. The model below distinguishes central roles (governance, architecture, oversight) from local roles (operating-unit-level execution).

4.1 Central roles

CISO

Ultimate accountability for the security posture of all in-scope assets. Approves this SOP and material changes to it. Owns the relationship between the asset register and incident response.

CIO

Accountable for the IT estate. Approves naming convention extensions and code-vocabulary additions that have Institution-wide impact.

Head of GRC

Day-to-day owner of this SOP. Maintains the SOP, the Codes Register (GRC-REG-CODES), and the Asset Register (GRC-REG-ASSET). Reviews submissions, processes deviations, runs annual reviews.

Head of IT Operations

Owns automated reconciliation between the Asset Register and authoritative source systems (AD, Intune, CrowdStrike, vulnerability scanners). Identifies drift.

Architecture / Technical Authority

Adjudicates ambiguous asset classifications, technical taxonomy questions, and convention edge cases. Approves new asset-type codes.

Risk Committee

Reviews aggregate asset-coverage metrics quarterly. Endorses material changes to scope, schema, or convention.

4.2 Operating-unit (local) roles

LCSO / CSLC

Local Cyber Security Officer / Coordinator. Accountable for the completeness and accuracy of asset records for their operating unit. Sponsors agency, city, and asset-type code requests.

Local IT Lead

Accountable for technical accuracy of asset records. Submits new asset records, updates existing ones, and validates reconciliation findings.

Asset Submitter

Any local IT staff member authorised to submit asset records on behalf of the operating unit. Submitters do not approve their own submissions.

Business Owner (local)

Identified per asset. Accountable for the asset's continuing business relevance and for criticality classification.

Separation of duties. The asset submitter and the asset approver are different individuals. Local IT submits; central GRC reviews and approves. This separation provides independence and is required for audit evidence.

Section 6
Naming Convention

This section defines the authoritative naming convention for all assets across the Institution. All physical and located assets must be named according to the structure below. Synthetic identifiers for non-host assets follow §7.

6.1 Structure

The standard asset name is composed of five segments, concatenated without separators, in capital letters and digits only:

<AGENCY><COUNTRY><CITY><TYPE><SEQ>
AGENCY (3–5 alpha) COUNTRY (2 alpha, ISO 3166-1) CITY (3 alpha) TYPE (3 alpha) SEQ (4–6 numeric)

6.2 Segment rules

SegmentLengthFormatSource of valid values
Agency3–5 charsUppercase A–Z onlyGRC-REG-CODES (Agency codes table)
Country2 charsISO 3166-1 alpha-2 uppercaseISO 3166-1 alpha-2 standard
City / location3 charsUppercase A–Z onlyGRC-REG-CODES (City codes table)
Asset type3 charsUppercase A–Z onlyGRC-REG-CODES (Asset type codes table) — see §5.1
Sequence4–6 digitsNumeric, zero-paddedIssued locally per (Agency × Country × City × Type)

6.3 Examples

The following examples demonstrate the convention in practice:

AKDNPTLISSRV0001
AKDN PT LIS SRV 0001

AKDN agency, Portugal, Lisbon, Server, sequence 0001

IISUKLONLPT01234
IIS UK LON LPT 01234

IIS agency, United Kingdom, London, Laptop, sequence 01234

AKHSKEMOMNET005
AKHS KE MOM NET 005

AKHS agency, Kenya, Mombasa, Network device, sequence 005

6.4 Length considerations

The minimum compliant name length is 13 characters (3+2+3+3+4 with shortest sequence). The maximum is 19 characters (5+2+3+3+6). This may exceed the legacy NetBIOS limit of 15 characters for some agencies and sequence widths. The Institution accepts this trade-off:

  • Modern Active Directory and DNS support computer names up to 63 characters. NetBIOS is largely irrelevant in modern environments.
  • SMB/CIFS browsing by NetBIOS name is no longer required where DNS resolution is functional, which is the standard for all modern AD environments.
  • Where a specific tool or vendor product imposes a hard 15-character limit, the local operating unit submits a deviation request per §14.
  • Sequence width is chosen by the operating unit based on expected asset volume per (Agency × Country × City × Type) tuple. Use 4 digits where total volume will not exceed 9,999; use 6 digits for higher-volume sites.

6.5 Capitalisation, characters, and separators

  • All segments are uppercase; lowercase is non-conformant
  • No separators between segments; the full name is one continuous string
  • Alpha segments use A–Z only; no digits, no special characters
  • The sequence segment uses 0–9 only; no alpha
  • Hyphens, underscores, and other symbols are not permitted in any segment
Section 7
Asset Identifier Rules

The Asset Identifier (Asset ID) is the primary key in the Asset Register. Two patterns apply, depending on whether the asset has a hostname or location.

7.1 Pattern A — Physical & located assets

For any asset that has a hostname or a fixed location (servers, workstations, laptops, mobile devices, network devices, peripherals, OT devices), the Asset ID equals the hostname per §6.

Example: a Lisbon server with hostname AKDNPTLISSRV0001

Asset ID = AKDNPTLISSRV0001 (identical to the hostname). One identifier across hostname, asset record, and SIEM correlation.

7.2 Pattern B — Non-host assets (operating-unit-owned)

For assets that have no hostname and no fixed physical location (SaaS services, applications, identity stores, data stores, cloud workloads at the logical-service level), use a synthetic identifier:

ASSET-<AGENCY>-<TYPE>-<SEQ>

Example: ASSET-AKDN-SAAS-0001 for a SaaS service owned by AKDN

7.3 Pattern C — Cross-agency / centrally-managed assets

For assets provided centrally and consumed by multiple operating units (e.g. a global Microsoft 365 tenant, a global Entra ID directory, a central SIEM), use the explicit CENTRAL prefix to make the cross-agency nature visible:

ASSET-CENTRAL-<TYPE>-<SEQ>

Example: ASSET-CENTRAL-SAAS-0001 for a global Microsoft 365 tenant.
Example: ASSET-CENTRAL-IDS-0001 for the global Entra ID directory.

7.4 Multi-instance and clustered assets

The treatment of clustered or multi-instance assets depends on the asset type:

  • Physical hosts get one record per host. A 6-node cluster of database servers is six asset records (six hostnames). The cluster relationship is captured via dependency links, not via merging.
  • Applications and SaaS services get one record per logical service. A clustered application running on six hosts is one application asset record; the six hosts are six separate server records, linked via "hosts" dependency.
  • Cloud workloads follow the unit-of-management. If a Kubernetes deployment is managed as a single unit, it is one asset record; if individual pods or VMs are independently managed, they are separate records.
  • Clusters defined explicitly: where a parent–child relationship matters (e.g. a database cluster), create a parent record (one CLD or APP) and link child records (the underlying SRVs) via dependency.

7.5 Hostname change handling

Where a hostname changes (rename, re-deployment, environment migration), the Asset Record retains its history. The Asset ID is updated to the new hostname; the old hostname is recorded in the "Previous identifiers" field. The asset is the same logical thing; only the identifier moves.

Where an asset is decommissioned and a new asset takes its place at the same physical location, the new asset receives the next available sequence number in its (Agency × Country × City × Type) series. Sequence numbers are not recycled; once an Asset ID is used, it remains permanently associated with the original asset record.

Section 9
Lifecycle & Status Model

Every asset progresses through defined lifecycle states. Transitions are recorded with date and approver in the asset record's audit trail.

1
Proposed

Asset record created; not yet built or deployed. Used for assets in procurement or design phase. Reviewable by GRC but not yet operationally relevant.

2
Onboarding

Asset is being built, configured, or deployed. Subject to onboarding checks (see §10). May appear in monitoring tools but is not yet load-bearing.

3
Live

Asset is operational and authorised to support business activities. Subject to all standard controls (monitoring, patching, backup as defined for its type and tier).

4
Under review

Periodic or triggered review in progress. Asset remains operational but a verification activity is underway. Returns to Live after review closes.

5
Decommissioning

Asset is being retired. Service is being migrated, drained, or shut down. Decommissioning checklist (see §12) must be completed before final state.

6
Decommissioned

Asset is no longer operational. Record is retained for audit. Decommissioning evidence (data destruction, asset disposal) is attached to the record.

7
Archived

Asset record is moved out of the active register, retained for audit per §15 retention rules. Archived records are not editable; they are referenced by ID only.

Section 10
Onboarding Workflow

An asset is onboarded when its record is created and validated. The workflow below applies to every new asset, regardless of type.

Step 1Submit — Local IT Lead (or delegated submitter) creates the asset record using GRC-FRM-XX (Asset Onboarding Form, future delivery), or the equivalent form provided by the GRC team. All mandatory fields per §8 must be present. Status = Proposed.
Step 2Validate naming — Submitter confirms Asset ID conforms to §6/§7. Where any segment requires a code not yet in GRC-REG-CODES, the submitter raises a code request first (see §14).
Step 3Classify — Submitter assigns asset type (§5.1), function tags (§5.2), criticality tier, data classification, and regulatory tags. Where uncertain, the submitter proposes and flags for GRC review.
Step 4Identify owners — Business owner, technical owner, and security contact must be named and contactable. "Team mailbox" is acceptable as a fallback only where a named owner is genuinely unavailable.
Step 5Build / deploy — Asset is built or deployed per local procedures. Status moves to Onboarding.
Step 6GRC review — Head of GRC (or delegate) reviews the record per the Reviewer Work Instruction (GRC-WI-05). Common review failures: incomplete classification, mismatched naming, missing owners, conflict with existing record.
Step 7Approve and publish — Once GRC approves, status moves to Live and the record is published in GRC-REG-ASSET. Onboarding date is recorded.

10.1 Onboarding completeness checklist

  • Asset ID assigned and conformant with §6/§7
  • All required codes present in GRC-REG-CODES (no pending code requests)
  • Asset type and function tags assigned per §5
  • Criticality tier and data classification assigned
  • Business owner, technical owner, and security contact named with valid contact details
  • Environment, hosting model, OS/platform recorded
  • Backup, monitoring, and patching ownership defined
  • Key dependencies recorded
  • Open risks and known exceptions linked where applicable
  • Initial review date scheduled per Tier (annual T0/T1, 18 months T2, 24 months T3)
Section 11
Review & Verification

Asset records must be kept current. Verification combines scheduled active reviews and continuous automated reconciliation against authoritative source systems.

11.1 Active review cadence

Criticality tierActive review intervalReviewer
Tier 0AnnualLocal IT Lead with GRC sign-off
Tier 1AnnualLocal IT Lead with GRC sign-off
Tier 2Every 18 monthsLocal IT Lead
Tier 3Every 24 monthsLocal IT Lead (sample-based for high-volume types)

An active review confirms: ownership remains current; technical attributes are accurate; classifications are still appropriate; dependencies remain valid; the asset is still authorised to operate.

11.2 Triggered review (out-of-cycle)

An out-of-cycle review is required when:

  • Ownership changes (business owner, technical owner, security contact)
  • The asset is involved in a Sev 1 or Sev 2 incident (per GRC-IRP-01)
  • A material technical change occurs (re-platform, major version upgrade, hosting migration)
  • A new risk is registered against the asset (linked from GRC-REG-RISK)
  • An audit finding identifies the record as inaccurate
  • Reconciliation against an authoritative source surfaces drift

11.3 Automated reconciliation

The Head of IT Operations operates automated reconciliation between the Asset Register and authoritative source systems. The minimum reconciliation set is:

  • Active Directory / Entra ID — every server, endpoint, and identity-store record reconciled against directory presence
  • Endpoint management (Intune, JAMF, equivalent) — workstation and laptop records reconciled against managed-device fleet
  • EDR (CrowdStrike or equivalent) — server, workstation, laptop records reconciled against EDR coverage
  • Vulnerability scanner — IP-routable assets reconciled against scanner coverage
  • Cloud subscription inventory — cloud workload records reconciled against subscription/tenant inventory

Reconciliation findings are categorised:

  • In-register, in-source: match — no action
  • In-register, not in-source: stale record — investigate (asset retired without record update?)
  • Not in-register, in-source: missing record — initiate retroactive onboarding
  • Attribute drift: ownership, OS version, environment differs — update or investigate

Reconciliation reports are produced monthly and reviewed by the Head of GRC and the Head of IT Operations.

Section 12
Decommissioning & Disposal

Decommissioning is a controlled lifecycle event. Skipping or rushing decommissioning is a leading cause of orphaned data, persistent vulnerabilities, and compliance findings.

12.1 Decommissioning checklist

  • Decommissioning decision recorded with business and technical owner sign-off
  • Dependent assets identified; downstream impact assessed and communicated
  • Data classification reviewed; data-handling plan for retained, migrated, or destroyed data documented
  • Backup of asset state taken (where retention is required)
  • Connections to other assets terminated cleanly (DNS, network ACLs, identity grants, monitoring agents)
  • Asset removed from monitoring scope (with reason recorded)
  • Service accounts and machine identities revoked or rotated
  • Certificates issued to the asset revoked or expired
  • Physical disposal route confirmed (return to vendor, secure disposal, redeployment) with chain-of-custody record
  • For data stores: data destruction certified per data-classification level (cryptographic erase, secure wipe, physical destruction)
  • Asset record status set to Decommissioned with date and authoriser

12.2 Data destruction by classification

Assets carrying personal data, Confidential L3, or Confidential L4 data require certified destruction. The level of certification scales with classification:

Data classificationMinimum destruction methodCertification required
Confidential L4Cryptographic erase + physical destruction or NIST SP 800-88 PurgeCertificate of destruction signed by Head of IT Ops + retained for 7 years
Confidential L3Cryptographic erase or NIST SP 800-88 PurgeCertificate signed by Local IT Lead
Confidential L2Standard secure wipe (single-pass overwrite minimum)Logged in asset record
Public L1Standard wipe before disposalLogged in asset record

12.3 Identifier non-recycling

Once an Asset ID is allocated, it is permanently associated with the original asset. A new asset replacing a decommissioned one receives the next available sequence number. Recycling Asset IDs creates correlation errors in long-running data sources (SIEM logs, vulnerability history, audit trails) and is prohibited.

Section 13
Maturity Ladder

Operating units are not required to reach full ITAM maturity on day one. The ladder below sets out a progression. Operating units should be at Level 1 within 6 months of this SOP being in force, Level 2 within 12 months, and Level 3 within 24 months.

1Inventory established

Schema: mandatory fields (§8 groups A–E) populated for all in-scope assets.

Lifecycle: Proposed / Onboarding / Live / Decommissioned tracked manually.

Verification: active review only; no automation.

Outcome: "we know what we have." Foundational SCM.

2Operationally integrated

Schema: all SCM fields (groups A–G) populated; ITAM fields (group H) emerging.

Lifecycle: full state model in use; transitions logged; reviews on cadence.

Verification: active reviews + automated reconciliation against AD, EDR, vulnerability scanner.

Outcome: register supports incident response, vulnerability prioritisation, change-impact analysis.

3Full ITAM

Schema: all fields populated including ITAM extension (group H).

Lifecycle: integrated with procurement, finance, and BC/DR systems.

Verification: continuous automated reconciliation across all source systems; service mapping in place.

Outcome: register supports financial planning, vendor management, full BC/DR, supplier risk.

Section 14
Code Vocabulary & Deviations

The naming convention's three controlled vocabularies — agency codes, city/location codes, and asset-type codes — are maintained in GRC-REG-CODES. The Codes Register is the authoritative source.

14.1 Adding a new code

  1. The local LCSO or Local IT Lead identifies the need for a new code (e.g. a new agency onboards, a new city is opened, a new asset type emerges)
  2. Submitter raises a code request via the GRC team, providing: proposed code, full name, justification, expected scope
  3. The Head of GRC reviews against existing codes for collisions and intuitive uniqueness
  4. For asset-type codes, the Architecture / Technical Authority is consulted
  5. For agency codes that span multiple operating units, the CIO is consulted
  6. Approved codes are added to GRC-REG-CODES with status, owner, and date
  7. The code is then available for use in asset names and asset records

14.2 Deviations from the convention

Where an asset cannot conform to the naming convention (e.g. vendor-imposed naming, a tool with character restrictions, integration constraints), the operating unit submits a convention deviation request. The deviation is approved by the Head of GRC on the Risk Committee's behalf, recorded in GRC-REG-CODES (Deviations table), and linked from the affected asset records.

Deviation requests must include: the constraint causing non-conformance, the alternative naming used, the affected scope (one asset, a class of assets, an entire technology), the duration (temporary or permanent), and the proposed mitigation (e.g. tagging in the register so that searches still find the asset).

Deviations are bounded, not unlimited. A deviation that affects more than 5% of an operating unit's asset population is escalated to the Risk Committee for review. Deviations are reviewed annually for currency.

14.3 Migration of legacy assets

Assets that exist before this SOP was approved continue to operate under their legacy names until one of the following occurs:

  • The asset is decommissioned and replaced — replacement uses conformant naming
  • The asset is rebuilt, re-platformed, or migrated — the new instance uses conformant naming
  • A planned mass-rename project occurs, scoped and prioritised by the Head of IT Operations

Legacy-named assets are tagged in the Asset Register with a "legacy naming" flag. The flag is removed once the asset's name is brought into conformance.

Section 15
Records, Related Documents & Review

15.1 Records produced

  • Asset records in GRC-REG-ASSET (the operational register)
  • Code vocabulary in GRC-REG-CODES (agency, city, asset-type codes; deviations)
  • Onboarding submission records and approvals
  • Active review records (per Tier cadence)
  • Reconciliation reports (monthly, reviewed quarterly)
  • Decommissioning checklists and certificates of destruction
  • Deviation requests and approvals

15.2 Retention

  • Active asset records: retained while asset is in any non-Archived state
  • Decommissioned asset records: retained for 7 years from decommissioning date, then archived
  • Certificates of destruction (L3/L4): 7 years
  • Reconciliation reports: 3 years
  • Deviation approvals: retained while deviation is in force, plus 3 years

15.4 Review

This SOP is reviewed annually by the Head of GRC with sign-off by the CISO. Out-of-cycle reviews are mandatory after material Institutional change, after any audit finding affecting the asset register, after the addition of a new agency, and after any material change to the operating-unit footprint. The annual review covers: convention currency, schema fitness, taxonomy completeness, lifecycle adequacy, deviation patterns, reconciliation effectiveness.
